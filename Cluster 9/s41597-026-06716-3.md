This paper presents an open-access telemetry dataset designed to support research and training in 
intelligent fixed-wing unmanned aerial systems. The dataset contains 240 fully annotated autonomous 
missions flown outdoors over repeatable, waypoint-based trajectories using two onboard architectures: 
a compact SpeedyBee F405 flight controller running INAV, and a Holybro Pixhawk 6X paired with a 
Jetson Orin NX companion computer running PX4. The missions cover key phases, including take-off, 
cruise, dynamic manoeuvres, and autonomous landing. Each log provides synchronised multi-sensor 
telemetry (IMU, GNSS, barometric altitude, actuator states, flight modes, and power metrics) at high 
temporal resolution, enabling realistic modelling of flight dynamics, estimator behaviour, and sensor 
noise. The dataset supports benchmarking for trajectory tracking under degraded GNSS, anomaly 
detection, wind-aware navigation, and energy-optimised mission planning. The paper documents 
hardware integration, communication architecture, mission procedures, and the dataset file structure, 
and includes representative analyses to illustrate reuse for contested, safety-critical, and complex 
operational environments in field. No neural network is trained or evaluated; deep learning is cited only 
as a motivating application domain.
Background & Summary
Unmanned aerial systems (UAS) have become invaluable assets in both civilian and defence sectors, facilitating 
a variety of applications, including environmental monitoring, precision agriculture, tactical reconnaissance, 
and persistent surveillance. This operational versatility is driven by advances in flight control, navigation algo-
rithms and autonomous mission execution, enabling complex, long-range operations with minimal human 
intervention. In particular, fixed-wing UAVs offer superior endurance, coverage and energy efficiency compared 
to multirotor platforms, making them well-suited to large-scale missions such as border monitoring, maritime 
patrols and gathering area intelligenc.
Despite these advances, the autonomy of fixed-wing unmanned aerial vehicles (UAVs) in operational sce-
narios is still limited due to their heavy reliance on global navigation satellite systems (GNSS) and conventional 
proportional-integral-derivative (PID)-based control laws. In defence-relevant environments where GNSS sig-
nals may be degraded or denied due to interference, spoofing or jamming, this dependence reduces the robust-
ness and reliability of missions. However, recent progress in artificial intelligence (AI) and machine learning 
(ML), including deep learning (DL) architectures such as long short-term memory (LSTM) networks and 
multilayer perceptrons (MLPs), has demonstrated the potential to overcome these limitations. This is achieved 
through the enablement of robust trajectory tracking, adaptive control and even GNSS-free navigation using 
only onboard sensors1–3. However, rigorous evaluation of such methods requires high-fidelity, real-world telem-
etry that captures representative mission profiles and sensor behaviours under operationally relevant conditions.
Current research in aerospace engineering increasingly favours integrating ML into mission planning, navi-
gation and health monitoring, as discussed in4–6. In safety-critical defence systems, the focus is on interpretable 
and certifiable AI models that can maintain performance in the face of uncertainty and sensor degradation.

Success in these areas critically depends on the availability of large, well-annotated datasets that accurately rep-
resent real flight dynamics, actuator behaviour and environmental disturbances. This is particularly true for 
anomaly detection, fault diagnosis and performance degradation prediction, where a lack of labelled data and 
the high dimensionality of telemetry pose ongoing challenges (see, for example7–9). While existing frameworks 
such as AMBASSADOR for spacecraft10 and regression-based UAV anomaly detection11 demonstrate the oper-
ational impact of scalable, data-driven analytics, they also highlight the need for representative datasets to train 
and benchmark these tools. Although synthetic datasets can partially address the issue of limited data, they are 
unable to replicate the complexities of real operational environments effectively12. Open-access, richly anno-
tated, high-rate telemetry from fixed-wing UAV missions is therefore essential for validating machine learn-
ing (ML)-based navigation, trajectory forecasting, and control algorithms designed for contested or Global 
Navigation Satellite System (GNSS)-degraded environments.
However, most publicly available datasets focus on multirotor platforms in controlled environments and often 
lack GNSS, airspeed, actuator or power telemetry13–17. Benchmarks such as EuRoC MAV13, MMFW-UAV18 and 
Blackbird14 are valuable for visual-inertial odometry and aggressive manoeuvre research, but are limited to quadro-
tors in motion-capture arenas. Large-scale, outdoor datasets for fixed-wing UAVs, especially those involving repeata-
ble trajectory patterns, remain scarce. This hinders progress in developing and validating robust, AI-driven autonomy.
To address this issue, this study presents IDF-DS19, an open-access telemetry database comprising 240 fully 
annotated fixed-wing UAV flights totalling over 32 hours of airborne data. The missions were flown using two 
different avionics architectures: a lightweight SpeedyBee F405 with INAV and a high-performance Holybro 
Pixhawk 6X with Jetson Orin NX and PX4. Each flight log contains synchronised, high-rate telemetry data 
from onboard sensors (IMU, GNSS, airspeed, barometric pressure, actuator states, battery and power metrics)20, 
together with mission waypoints, aircraft specifications and wind estimates. By providing repeatable trajec-
tory data under different configurations and flight speeds, the dataset enables the rigorous benchmarking of 
machine learning (ML) based navigation, trajectory tracking, and anomaly detection methods for both civilian 
and defence applications. The main contributions of this work are: 
• 
A comprehensive, open-access dataset of 240 fixed-wing missions, covering all flight phases (take-off, cruise, 
loiter, waypoint tracking, autonomous landing) with two avionics architectures.
• 
High-resolution, synchronised telemetry including up to 400 Hz IMU, 10 Hz GNSS and airspeed, actuator 
and RC/CRSF channels, battery and power data.
• 
Demonstrative use cases for defence-relevant applications such as GNSS-degraded navigation, wind estima-
tion, anomaly detection, and energy-aware trajectory planning.
• 
Extensive mission metadata enabling reproducibility, re-simulation in PX4 SITL, and integration into ML 
training pipelines.
It should be noted that no neural network is trained or evaluated in this study. Deep learning is mentioned 
exclusively as a motivating application domain for the released dataset.
The release of this open, annotated database reduces the barriers to the development and evaluation of 
AI-based navigation and control algorithms for fixed-wing unmanned aerial vehicles (UAVs), while supporting 
hands-on, data-driven research and education in aerospace engineering. Following the introduction and review 
of the state of the art, the remainder of this paper is organized as follows. Section Methods describes the materials

Airframe specifications. 
This study uses the Volantex Ranger 2400 fixed-wing UAV platform (https://www.
volantexrc.eu/). This high-wing aircraft has a wingspan of 2,400 mm and is designed for long-endurance, stable 
autonomous missions. Its modular structure allows for the integration of avionics, power systems and sensing 
payloads, making it ideal for AI-based navigation research. Figure 1 shows a schematic view of the platform and 
its main dimensions.
The fuselage is made of thermoplastic ABS to provide structural rigidity and protect the onboard electronics. 
The wing and tail surfaces are made from expanded polyolefin (EPO) foam, which is lightweight and tolerant of 
damage, facilitating quick field repairs after landings or impacts. A rear-mounted 4023-class brushless outrun-
ner motor, rated at 1050 KV, provides propulsion when paired with a 10-inch, two-blade folding propeller. This 
configuration delivers high thrust during take-off and cruise, and the folding blades reduce drag during gliding 
phases by automatically retracting when unpowered. The motor is powered by a 4S (14.8 V) lithium-ion battery, 
which strikes a balance between torque and efficiency across all flight regimes.
Motor control is handled by a Hobbywing electronic speed controller (ESC), which features a Battery 
Eliminator Circuit (BEC) to supply 5 V to the servos and avionics. The ESC receives PWM throttle commands

Flight controllers. 
In recent years, significant progress has been made in developing low-cost, lightweight 
sensor technologies, enabling their widespread integration into UAV avionics. For small fixed-wing platforms in 
particular, design choices are often limited by factors such as payload capacity, power consumption and availa-
ble onboard computational resources. These constraints directly influence the selection of flight controllers and 
sensor suites. Figure 2 presents the two avionics configurations employed in this study, contrasting a compact, 
all-in-one flight controller with a modular architecture combining a Pixhawk 6X and a Jetson Orin NX compan-
ion computer.
Compact solutions like the SpeedyBee F405 consolidate core sensing and control functionalities into a sin-
gle, lightweight (approximately 36 × 36 mm) board, offering simplicity and ease of integration. By contrast, 
the Pixhawk 6X + Jetson Orin NX configuration has a modular design which, although significantly more 
powerful, results in increased volume, weight and wiring complexity. The baseboard is considerably larger and 
additional components, such as the PM02D power module and UBEC, are required for full system integration. 
From a cost perspective, the SpeedyBee-based system remains highly accessible, typically retailing for under 100 
€. Conversely, the Pixhawk 6X with Jetson Orin NX, when equipped with SSD storage, a camera, Wi-Fi and a 
power distribution unit, exceeds 1,700 €, reflecting its enhanced computational capabilities and suitability for 
on-board AI inference.
SpeedyBee F405.  Designed for fixed-wing and multirotor UAVs, the compact and cost-effective SpeedyBee 
F405 Wing flight controller is ideal for autonomous missions. Based on a 32-bit STM32F405 microcontroller 
running at 168 MHz, it integrates essential onboard sensors, including an MPU6000 six-axis IMU and a baro-
metric pressure sensor (typically a BMP280 or DPS310). The MPU6000 provides reliable inertial measurements 
via a stable Table1 kHz SPI interface, supporting flight stabilisation and basic attitude estimation. While its noise 
performance is inferior to that of the ICM-42688-P used in the Pixhawk 6X, it remains popular in the UAV com-
munity thanks to its reliability and consistent performance. The BMP280 provides adequate barometric altitude 
estimation for altitude hold, albeit with lower resolution and responsiveness than the SPL06-001.
The F405 supports multiple communication interfaces, including up to six UARTs, I2C, and SPI, enabling 
integration with external GPS modules, airspeed sensors, telemetry links, and ExpressLRS receivers. In this con-
figuration, an ExpressLRS 2.4 GHz receiver is connected via a dedicated UART port using the Crossfire (CRSF) 
protocol, allowing low-latency RC control and bidirectional telemetry. Real-time flight data, including battery 
voltage, flight mode, GPS status, and airspeed, are transmitted back to the pilot via the CRSF uplink. All actuator 
outputs (servos and ESC) are handled through direct PWM channels, simplifying wiring and minimizing latency.
The system runs INAV 6.1 firmware, selected for its mature support of fixed-wing platforms and integrated 
telemetry logging capabilities. Although it lacks companion computing and onboard AI inference, it sup-
ports full autonomous waypoint navigation and logs flight data to a microSD card. Its low weight, reduced 
power consumption, and minimal integration complexity make it well-suited for endurance-focused or 
resource-constrained scenarios.

Holybro Pixhawk 6X.  This study uses a high-performance flight control architecture that integrates a Holybro 
Pixhawk 6X autopilot and an NVIDIA Jetson Orin NX companion computer. Both are mounted on the Holybro 
Jetson Baseboard. This modular system separates the Pixhawk’s low-level, real-time control from the Jetson’s 
high-level computational tasks, such as AI-based navigation, sensor fusion and data logging. This hybrid archi-
tecture is becoming increasingly popular in autonomous UAV research as it separates safety-critical control from 
non-deterministic processing tasks.
The Pixhawk 6X complies with the PX4 open standard maintained by the Dronecode consortium and is 
equipped with a 480 MHz STM32H7 microcontroller, dual inertial measurement units (ICM-42688-P and 
BMI088), and a high-resolution barometric sensor (SPL06-001). The ICM-42688-P combines a three-axis gyro-
scope and accelerometer with a noise density of 3.1 mdps /
Hz enabling precise motion tracking with low 
drift21. The SPL06-001 provides high-resolution altitude estimation with update rates above 50 Hz and 
centimeter-level vertical resolution, making it suitable for accurate barometric navigation even in GPS-denied 
environments.
The Pixhawk 6X offers extensive connectivity, including six UART ports, two CAN interfaces, three I2C 
buses, multiple analogue-to-digital converter (ADC) channels, and up to twelve pulse-width modulation 
(PWM) outputs. This allows for the flexible integration of external peripherals, such as GPS receivers, airspeed 
sensors, rangefinders and additional compute nodes. The Jetson Orin NX module delivers up to 100 TOPS (tril-
lion operations per second) of AI inference capability via its Ampere GPU architecture and integrated NVIDIA 
Deep Learning Accelerators (NVDLA). This computational power enables the execution of demanding algo-
rithms onboard, including SLAM, computer vision pipelines, and deep reinforcement learning. Communication 
between the Jetson and Pixhawk is established via a high-bandwidth UART or Ethernet link using the MAVLink 
protocol, typically through MAVROS or MAVSDK. This facilitates the bidirectional exchange of telemetry, sen-
sor data, and trajectory setpoints.
In its current configuration, the Jetson Orin NX is responsible for synchronised logging of telemetry, sen-
sor measurements and camera data streams. The system is ready for the future integration of learning-based 
onboard navigation policies, including state estimation in environments where GPS is unavailable. Leveraging 
open-source tools such as PX4, ROS 2, MAVROS and MAVSDK makes the platform modular, reproducible and 
compatible with advanced autonomous flight architectures.

high-performance, cylindrical Samsung INR21700-50S cells. Each cell has a nominal capacity of 5000 mAh and

Data Acquisition. 
Diversity and generalisation in the collected flight dataset were achieved by deploying two 
distinct hardware configurations on a fixed-wing UAV platform with a common design. While preserving iden-
tical airframe geometry, propulsion system and control surfaces, these setups differ primarily in sensor payload 
and onboard computational capacity. This dual-architecture approach enables the future evaluation of AI models 
under varying sensor availability and processing constraints, and allows operational scenarios with different levels 
of autonomy to be simulated.
To ensure consistency across experiments, all flight missions followed predefined, waypoint-based trajec-
tories and were executed in fully autonomous mode using both control architectures: PX4 and INAV. Mission 
plans were uploaded to the PX4 system via QGroundControl and to the SpeedyBee-based configuration via the 
INAV Configurator. Each trajectory was flown multiple times with both systems to capture variability within 
missions induced by wind conditions, heading corrections and sensor noise. All flights were performed in open 
field environments with minimal GNSS interference to ensure accurate, reproducible data acquisition.
For every flight, the following telemetry and control data streams were recorded with synchronized 
timestamps: 
• 
Raw IMU measurements (accelerations, angular rates) from both internal sensors.
• 
GPS position and velocity.
• 
Barometric altitude.
• 
RC input and actuator PWM outputs (elevator, rudder, ailerons, throttle).
• 
Estimated and target states from PX4 (e.g., attitude, local position setpoints).
• 
Flight mode, failsafe flags, and system status.
Post-flight, timestamp alignment was performed using system clocks and GPS time references, enabling the 
fusion of telemetry and perception data. All logs are stored in a structured format that is suitable for offline pro-
cessing using Python or MATLAB. They can also be easily converted into supervised learning datasets for tasks 
such as regression, classification or sequence modelling.
Experimental Flight Areas. 
The flight experiments were conducted at a dedicated aeromodelling site in 
eastern Spain. Two distinct, waypoint-based missions were designed and carried out in the same geographical 
area. Each mission was tailored to a specific onboard electronics configuration and variations in the trajectory 
were accounted for in order to allow for differences in system mass, power consumption and flight dynamics. 
Despite sharing a common take-off and landing zone, the UAV exhibited different in-flight behaviour depending 
on the hardware setup.
The first trajectory, which is flown using the SpeedyBee F405 flight controller, follows a structured zigzag 
pattern that is designed to produce consistent and spatially diverse telemetry (see Fig. 5). The mission consists of 
17 waypoints arranged over a rectangular area, covering approximately 5.4 km per lap. After departing from the 
pilot area, the UAV flies multiple loops along the same path to enable repeatable data collection, which is suitable 
for validating estimators, control algorithms and AI-based models. Although the take-off and landing locations

Flight Conditions and Safety.  All test flights were performed in visual line-of-sight conditions at the Alzira 
Model Aircraft Club in Valencia, Spain (January-March 2024 and June-August 2025 for the Speedy and Pixhawk 
configurations, respectively). Environmental data (wind speed, temperature and humidity) was recorded for 
context. A manual RC override was available at all times for safety purposes. Each trajectory was flown repeat-
edly, resulting in 240 flights in total and over 75 × 106 logged samples. Between flights, the aircraft was visually 
inspected and the batteries were replaced to ensure consistency and system integrity.

figurations. One hundred and twenty of the flights used a SpeedyBee F405 flight controller and the remaining 
one hundred and twenty used a Holybro Pixhawk 6X integrated with a Jetson Orin NX 16 GB companion com-
puter. Designed for educational and research purposes, the dataset supports the development and validation 
of AI-based models for navigation, control, and anomaly detection, particularly in the fields of aerospace and 
computer science.
Each flight log contains timestamped telemetry data from onboard sensors, estimator outputs, and con-
trol channels. The recorded data includes measurements from the IMU, barometer, magnetometer, GPS and 
DroneCAN airspeed sensor (pitot tube), as well as actuator commands, RC inputs, flight modes, battery sta-
tus and estimator innovations. In the Jetson-based setup, additional metadata is also included, such as camera 
timestamps and inference tags from onboard vision models. The complete list of the 160 telemetry parameters 
and all telemetry log files are publicly available in the project’s Zenodo repository.
The dataset is organized into two top-level directories: SpeedyBee and Pixhawk_Jetson, each contain-
ing 120 flight subfolders named flight_XXX, where XXX is a zero-padded identifier.

Technical Validation
This section presents a set of representative use cases that demonstrate the operational relevance and research poten-
tial of the IDF-DS telemetry dataset. The selected examples focus on capabilities of particular interest to the aero-
space and defence communities, including GNSS-degraded or denied navigation, wind field estimation for mission 
planning and robust trajectory tracking under varying aircraft configurations. For each scenario, the relevant log 
files from the dataset are identified by their unique filename suffixes, and the associated problem statement, oper-
ational context and analysis methodology are outlined. The examples emphasise how high-fidelity, synchronised 
telemetry captured during repeatable fixed-wing missions can support the development, benchmarking and vali-
dation of machine learning models and advanced estimation algorithms for contested or uncertain environments.
Validation of IMU Data Against Ground Truth Accelerations. 
This section evaluates the integrity of 
the dataset by comparing accelerations measured directly by the onboard inertial measurement unit (IMU) with 
reference accelerations derived from flight trajectory data. The aim is to evaluate the reliability of the IMU sen-
sors, which is crucial for subsequent navigation, control and machine learning applications. The validation uses 
synchronized data from three sources: 
• 
sensor_combined_0.csv: contains raw IMU measurements, linear accelerations (m/s2) and angular velocities 
(rad/s), in the UAV body frame, sampled at high rates (200–400 Hz).
• 
vehicle_gps_position_0.csv: provides GPS-based position and altitude (lower frequency, 5–10 Hz), serving 
as an external reference.
• 
vehicle_local_position_0.csv: reports local position and velocity in the NED frame, sampled at 10–100 Hz; 
its higher resolution enables accurate derivation of reference accelerations.
Reference accelerations are computed by numerically differentiating the UAV’s local position twice using 
gradient methods. These accelerations are then compared with the corresponding IMU measurements along the 
aircraft’s X, Y and Z axes. Figure 8 shows the result of this comparison for a representative flight segment. The

direct impact on the performance of the system as a whole. The IDF-DS provides access to both estimated and refer-
ence velocity data streams, enabling rigorous validation of the onboard estimator. In PX4, estimated velocities along 
the X, Y and Z axes are generated by a sensor fusion algorithm, typically an Extended Kalman Filter (EKF), which 
combines IMU, GPS and barometric measurements. To evaluate the accuracy of these estimates, they are compared to 
velocities derived independently from position data. The following files from the database are required for this analysis: 
• 
vehicle_local_position_0.csv: contains PX4-estimated velocities (vx, vy, vz) and positions in the NED 
frame, with high-frequency time stamps.
• 
vehicle_gps_position_0.csv: provides GPS-based position data for independent numerical differentiation if 
ground truth is required.

Position Estimation in GPS-Denied Scenarios. 
One of the main research opportunities offered by the IDF-DS 
involves developing and validating artificial intelligence methods for estimating the position of fixed-wing UAVs that 
are operating without GNSS. The high-frequency, time-synchronised telemetry data collected from real-world flights 
can be used to explore learning-based solutions for GPS-denied navigation in realistic conditions. This use case demon-
strates the feasibility of estimating a UAV’s three-dimensional position (x, y, z) in a local North-East-Down (NED) 
frame using only onboard telemetry signals, completely independently of any GNSS input. No machine learning model 
is trained or evaluated here; instead, the structure and availability of the dataset provide a basis for such research.
To replicate this scenario and generate input-output pairs suitable for supervised learning, the log files were 
merged based on their respective timestamps: 
• 
Inertial measurements: linear accelerations and angular velocities (_sensor_combined_0.csv)
• 
Attitude estimation: quaternion orientation (_vehicle_attitude_0.csv)
• 
Airspeed: indicated airspeed (_airspeed_0.csv)
• 
Actuator outputs: control surface and throttle PWM signals (_actuator_outputs_0.csv)
• 
Optional: battery voltage and current (_battery_status_0.csv)
The ground truth required for supervised model training consists of local position estimates, specifically the 
x, y, and z coordinates expressed in the NED (North-East-Down) reference frame, extracted from the _vehi-
cle_local_position_0.csv file.
