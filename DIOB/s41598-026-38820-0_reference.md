ABSTRACT
This study proposes a robust cascaded hybrid control architecture 
combining the Linear Quadratic Regulator (LQR) with Super-Twisting 
Sliding Mode Control (STSMC) to address the attitude stabilization 
problem of underactuated quadrotor UAVs. In this framework, the inner 
LQR loop ensures optimal stabilization, while the outer STSMC loops 
provide robust reference modification to compensate for uncertainties. To 
maximize the robustness of the proposed hybrid architecture, the 
parameters of the STSMC component were globally optimized using the 
Big Bang-Big Crunch (BB-BC) algorithm. The effectiveness of the proposed 
strategy is rigorously evaluated on the Quanser 3-DOF Hover system model 
against two distinct benchmarks: the classical LQR and the optimization-
based Model Predictive Control (MPC). Comprehensive simulation 
scenarios were conducted, including stochastic wind disturbances, time 
varying thrust coefficients, sudden payload release, and critical motor 
failure. Additionally, a virtual 3D spatial analysis based on kinematic 
reconstruction was performed to validate the positional stability of the 
rotor tips. The comparative results demonstrate that while the MPC 
controller exhibits a faster transient response compared to the classical 
LQR, it suffers from significant performance degradation under model 
mismatches. 
In 
contrast, 
the 
proposed 
LQR-STSMC 
consistently 
outperforms both benchmarks, achieving error metrics (ISE, IAE, and 
ITAE) orders of magnitude lower than the competitors. The findings 
confirm that the hybrid scheme offers superior fault tolerance, faster 
stabilization, and precise tracking capabilities, making it a highly reliable 
alternative for safety critical flight operations in uncertain environments.

1. Introduction
Unmanned Aerial Vehicles (UAVs), particularly quadrotors, have attracted 
significant interest in both civilian and military domains due to their 
vertical take-off and landing capabilities, high maneuverability, low cost, 
and structural simplicity [1–2]. Despite these advantages, quadrotors are 
constrained by limited payload capacity, energy constraints, and high 
sensitivity to external disturbances [3-4]. Their flight stability and 
performance are closely tied to the effectiveness of the employed control 
strategies.
Conventional linear controllers such as Proportional Integral Derivative 
(PID) and Linear Quadratic Regulator (LQR) are widely used [5], but often 
fall short under real world conditions involving disturbances, uncertainties, 
or actuator faults. In this context, Model Predictive Control (MPC) has 
gained prominence for its ability to handle state constraints and optimize 
trajectory tracking; however, its heavy reliance on accurate internal 
models often leads to performance degradation under significant 
parametric uncertainties [6]. Consequently, more robust strategies such as 
Sliding Mode Control (SMC), Adaptive Control, and Fault-Tolerant Control 
(FTC) have been proposed [7-8] to ensure stability against unmodeled 
dynamics. As quadrotors are inherently underactuated systems, with six 
degrees of freedom but only four control inputs, they are especially 
vulnerable to external factors like wind, parametric changes, and sudden 
mass variations [9-12].
Recent advancements in control theory have introduced sophisticated 
nonlinear strategies to handle parametric variations and environmental 
disturbances in aerial systems. For instance, Maaruf et al. [13] proposed a 
robust architecture combining Adaptive Fractional-Order SMC with 
Nonsingular Fast Terminal SMC and a finite time disturbance observer. 
Similarly, Fikadu Assefa et al. [14] introduced an Adaptive Neuro-Fuzzy 
Inference System based SMC to effectively manage external disturbances 
and parameter variations in quadcopter UAVs. Addressing the specific 
challenges of formation flight, Nguyen and Pitakwachara [15] developed a 
Non Singular Terminal Super-Twisting SMC, highlighting that integrating 
super-twisting 
algorithms 
with 
disturbance 
observers 
significantly 
enhances response speed.
Beyond sliding mode variations, hybrid and optimization-based approaches 
have also been explored. Mahmoud and Maaruf [16] introduced a 
multilevel control strategy utilizing Radial Basis Function Neural 
Networks. Addressing the complexity of parameter tuning, Maaruf et al. 
[17] applied metaheuristic algorithms specifically Improved Grey Wolf and 
Whale Optimization to optimize backstepping controllers. Similarly, 
evolutionary techniques such as Particle Swarm Optimization (PSO) and 
Genetic Algorithms (GA) have been successfully employed to optimize 
Sliding Mode Controller gains for unstable nonlinear systems, including 
magnetic levitation trains and suspension systems [18-19].

Furthermore, robust control applications extend to the broader field of

mobile manipulators [21] and Adaptive Fuzzy Super-Twisting Sliding Mode

In parallel with these developments, recent studies have investigated other 
hybrid configurations to further enhance tracking accuracy. For example, 
[26] proposed an improved trajectory tracking control using a Novel SMC

for mobile robots. Additionally, [28] developed an Improved Nonsingular 
Adaptive STSMC to address system uncertainties.

driven architectures demonstrate impressive tracking capabilities, they 
often

significant 
implementation 
challenges. 
These 
methodologies typically entail high computational complexity due to the 
necessity of executing iterative optimization algorithms online, training 
neural structures, or designing intricate adaptation laws to handle 
fractional dynamics. Seeking a balance between computational efficiency 
and robust performance, this study proposes a hybrid control approach, 
combining LQR with Super-Twisting Sliding Mode Control (LQR-STSMC). 
The selection of the LQR-STSMC hybrid architecture is driven by the need 
to balance energy optimality with robust disturbance rejection, a trade off 
that single method controllers often fail to achieve. Linear controllers like 
LQR are renowned for their ability to minimize control effort and steady 
state error around the operating point, yet they degrade significantly under 
nonlinear 
disturbances. 
Conversely, 
nonlinear 
robust 
strategies, 
particularly conventional SMC, offer excellent insensitivity to uncertainties 
but typically suffer from the “chattering” phenomenon, which causes high 
frequency mechanical wear and energy waste. To overcome these 
limitations, this study proposes a synergistic approach. The LQR 
component is employed to dictate the nominal system dynamics, ensuring 
smooth and energy-efficient tracking. Simultaneously, the STSMC a 
second order sliding mode algorithm is integrated as a corrective term. 
Unlike standard SMC, the STSMC provides finite time convergence and 
significantly attenuates chattering by hiding the discontinuous sign 
function under an integral. This specific combination allows the quadrotor 
to retain the optimality of linear control in calm conditions while instantly 
leveraging the high robustness capabilities of STSMC when subjected to 
external perturbations or failures.

the philosophy of the proposed LQR-STSMC framework lies in its structural 
simplicity. Instead of relying on fuzzy approximations, the method

2. Review of Operational Scenarios and Control Challenges
To establish a comprehensive validation framework for the proposed 
hybrid controller, it is essential to analyze the specific operational 
challenges prevalent in UAV flight missions. While the Introduction 
provided a broad overview of control strategies, this section focuses on the 
state of the art regarding the four critical scenarios selected for this study: 
environmental disturbances, parametric uncertainties, actuator faults, and 
payload variations.
Scenario 1: Wind Disturbance Mitigation 
Atmospheric disturbances, particularly wind gusts, represent a primary 
source of instability for lightweight quadrotors, often inducing significant 
tracking errors [29-30]. Given the stochastic and nonlinear nature of wind 
dynamics, accurate aerodynamic modelling and robust control synthesis 
are critical [31]. For instance, Jeon et al. [32] integrated aerodynamic 
effects into the quadrotor dynamic model using blade element theory, 
validating their approach experimentally. Similarly, Svacha et al. [33] and 
Perozzi et al. [34] incorporated aerodynamic force estimations directly into 
the control loop, utilizing gain scheduled SMC to enhance tracking 
accuracy and reduce chattering. Furthermore, Raza et al. [35]

significantly minimize position errors under wind loading. These studies

Scenario 2: Handling Time Varying Thrust Coefficients
Time varying parametric uncertainties, stemming from factors such as 
motor heating or battery voltage depletion, pose severe challenges to flight 
stability [36]. To mitigate these effects, Mu and Zhang [37] proposed a 
neural network based robust controller designed specifically for coupled, 
time varying systems. Addressing delay induced instabilities, Liu et al. [38] 
introduced a hierarchical control structure capable of managing 
uncertainties effectively. Additionally, Hassani et al. [39] developed a 
hybrid SMC approach featuring adaptive gain adjustment, which achieved 
high robustness and fast convergence despite parameter shifts. 
Consequently, the literature underscores the importance of adaptive and 
delay tolerant techniques for operating in uncertain parameter spaces.
Scenario 3: Tolerance to Motor Failure
Actuator failure constitutes a critical safety risk in quadrotors, as it induces 
severe asymmetry in thrust and moments. FTC strategies aim to sustain 
flight capability using the remaining healthy actuators [40]. Research by 
Lee et al. [41] demonstrated the feasibility of maintaining control during a 
single motor failure by dynamically shifting the center of gravity. 
Betancourt et al. [42] introduced an observer-based architecture designed 
to accommodate both critical and non-critical actuator faults. Furthermore, 
Nan et al. [43] utilized Nonlinear MPC for stable maneuvering under thrust 
saturation limits, while Ke et al. [44] proposed a seamless compensator-
based controller requiring no logical switching. These approaches 
highlight the necessity of intrinsic fault tolerance in modern UAV control 
systems.
Scenario 4: Adaptation to Payload Variation
Sudden variations in payload mass drastically alter the system’s inertial 
properties and center of gravity, demanding rapid adaptation from the 
controller [45]. Sankaranarayanan et al. [46] addressed this by proposing 
a robust controller specifically for load carrying missions subject to varying 
constraints. Similarly, Dhiman et al. [47] introduced a hybrid PID-PD 
controller optimized for cooperative transport tasks, while Kumar and 
Dewan [48] designed a PID-SMC scheme to ensure robustness against 
mass variations. The recurring theme in these studies is the critical need 
for dynamic adaptation mechanisms to handle abrupt changes in system 
dynamics.
In this context, the proposed LQR-STSMC controller combines the 
optimality of LQR with the robustness of sliding mode techniques. Unlike 
many theoretical hybrid approaches, this method is validated through 
diverse simulation scenarios including wind, parametric uncertainty, 
actuator failure, and mass variation demonstrating superior transient and 
steady state performance and offering both practical relevance and a 
meaningful contribution to the literature.

solutions for individual challenges ranging from wind mitigation to fault 
tolerance few studies present a unified framework capable of addressing

3. Materials and Methods
3.1. System Description and Scope
The 3-degree-of-freedom (3-DOF) hover training facility comprises a 
structure equipped with four propellers. This system is mounted on a pivot 
joint that allows rotation around the yaw, roll, and pitch axes [49]. The base 
of the plant is fixed to a workbench and incorporates slip rings to enable 
free movement along the yaw axis with minimal friction.
Each propeller generates thrust, which is used to control the roll and pitch 
angles of the system. As the propellers rotate, the resulting torque induces 
motion around the yaw axis. In a controlled environment, when the forces 
generated by the propellers are balanced, the net torque becomes zero, 
thereby stabilizing the system.
For this study, the model provided in the manufacturer’s manual was used 
[49]. When a positive voltage is applied to any of the motors, buoyancy is 
generated, which causes the propulsion system to rise. The front and rear 
motors, controlled by voltages 𝑉𝑓 and 𝑉𝑏, govern the motion along the tilt 
and yaw axes. Meanwhile, the side motors, controlled by 𝑉𝑟 and 𝑉𝑙, are 
responsible for motion along the roll and yaw axes [49].

3.2. Linear Quadratic Regulator (LQR)
The LQR is a widely used control approach for linear dynamic systems. This 
method aims to develop an optimal control strategy for systems defined by 
linear differential equations. The primary objective of the LQR method is 
to design a control law that ensures the desired dynamic performance of 
the system while minimizing a predefined cost function.
This cost function is typically formulated to balance the deviation of system 
states from their desired values and the control effort applied, with the 
goal of minimizing the total cost over time.

However, while LQR offers optimal performance for linear systems under 
nominal conditions, it does not always guarantee sufficient performance 
under real world conditions such as external disturbances and parametric 
uncertainties. Therefore, although the high precision of LQR is 
advantageous, it has become increasingly common to employ LQR in a 
hybrid structure alongside sliding surface-based control methods that offer 
greater robustness.
In this study, the stable control capabilities of LQR are combined with the 
robustness of the STSMC technique to construct a more resilient 
framework capable of handling external disturbances and uncertainties 
effectively.
3.3. Model Predictive Control (MPC)
In this study, the MPC method, one of the model-based control approaches, 
is employed for feedback control design. MPC is a control algorithm that 
optimizes control inputs in real time by utilizing a mathematical model 
capable of predicting the future behavior of the system, with the objective 
of minimizing a predefined cost function. Its ability to incorporate system 
constraints during decision-making and its suitability for multi-input multi-
output (MIMO) systems are among the key features that make MPC highly 
advantageous in industrial applications [51].
The system used in this study is represented by a linearized MIMO state-
space model with four inputs and three outputs. The controller was 
implemented in real time within the Simulink environment, and the entire 
control process was carried out using the MATLAB/Simulink interface. The

future behavior of the system is predicted over the defined prediction and 
control horizons. Only the first control input in the optimized sequence is 
applied, and the process is repeated at the next sampling instant to adapt 
to the system dynamics in real time. Through this structure, MPC ensures 
optimal reference tracking and effectively handles physical system 
constraints by generating smooth control signals based on the internal 
predictive model [51].
3.4. Super-Twisting Sliding Mode Control (STSMC)
STSMC is a second order sliding mode control algorithm specifically 
designed for systems with a relative degree of one. This method generates 
a continuous control signal that drives both the sliding variable and its 
derivative to zero in finite time. STSMC demonstrates superior 
performance compared to conventional sliding mode control techniques, 
particularly in reducing undesirable chattering effects caused by 
discontinuous control inputs [52].
A key feature of this algorithm is the decomposition of the control signal 
into two distinct components: the first is derived from the time derivative 
of a discontinuous function dependent on the sliding surface, while the 
second is a continuous complementary term with respect to the surface. 
This structure enhances the system’s robustness against disturbances and 
model uncertainties, while also improving stability and overall tracking 
accuracy. Moreover, the spiral convergence of the system trajectories 
toward the sliding surface contributes to improved transient response 
behavior.

3.5. Integrated Use of LQR and STSMC: Robust Virtual Reference 
Generation
In this study, a cascaded hybrid control framework is proposed to enhance 
the robustness of the Quanser 3-DOF hover system. Unlike conventional 
additive control architectures where auxiliary signals are directly added to 
the control input, this strategy utilizes the STSMC as an outer loop robust 
compensator. The fundamental scientific principle here is the separation 
of nominal performance and robustness: the LQR is dedicated to optimal 
stabilization based on the known linear dynamics, while the STSMC is 
tasked with estimating and rejecting “lumped disturbances” (including 
external wind, parametric uncertainties, and unmodeled dynamics).
The control architecture operates in a hierarchical dual loop structure:
Outer Loop (Robust Supervisor): The STSMC algorithm monitors the 
system’s output states. Instead of generating a direct torque, it functions 
as a “virtual controller” that calculates the necessary state deviation 
required to counteract the acting disturbances.
Dynamic Reference Modification: This corrective signal acts as a virtual 
offset. It is subtracted from the user-defined nominal reference to generate 
a robust modified reference (rmod) Essentially, the STSMC shifts the 
equilibrium point perceived by the linear controller to balance out the 
external forces.
Inner Loop (Optimal Tracking): The LQR controller acts as a high-
performance tracker for this new dynamic reference (rmod), By minimizing 
the error between the system states and this modified trajectory, the LQR

6. Conclusions
In this study, a hybrid control architecture, LQR-STSMC, was proposed and 
rigorously evaluated for the attitude stabilization of the Quanser 3-DOF 
Hover system. Additionally, the BB-BC optimization algorithm was 
employed to systematically tune the STSMC parameters, ensuring optimal 
disturbance rejection capability without manual tuning bias. To 
demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed strategy, a comprehensive 
comparative analysis was conducted against the classical LQR and the 
optimization-based MPC under various scenarios, including wind 
disturbances, motor failures, and sudden payload variations.
The quantitative results clearly demonstrate that the LQR-STSMC 
controller outperforms both benchmarks across all attitude axes (Yaw, 
Pitch, and Roll). While the MPC controller exhibited rapid initial responses, 
it suffered from significant steady-state errors and performance 
degradation under parametric uncertainties (such as payload release) due 
to model mismatch. In contrast, the proposed hybrid method maintained 
exceptional robustness. The performance metrics (ISE, IAE, and ITAE) 
indicate that the LQR-STSMC reduces tracking errors by orders of 
magnitude compared to both LQR and MPC, effectively suppressing both 
transient oscillations and long-term deviations.
Furthermore, the virtual 3D spatial analysis of the rotor tip trajectory 
confirmed the practical implications of these findings. The kinematic 
reconstruction showed that the superior attitude tracking provided by the 
LQR-STSMC directly translates into a tighter spatial envelope, significantly 
minimizing the physical oscillation and virtual positional drift of the system 
arms compared to the benchmarks.
