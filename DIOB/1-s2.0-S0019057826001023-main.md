A B S T R A C T
An adaptive fractional-order terminal sliding mode control technique is presented in this paper to improve quadro­
tor UAV control performance in the presence of stochastic disturbances and uncertainties. In order to fulfill 
mission-critical timing limitations, a fractional-order sliding mode controller is incorporated into the finite-time 
theory to guarantee system stability within a finite period. Second, a super-twisting sliding mode observer is inte­
grated to estimate external disturbances, which enhances the system’s resilience to environmental uncertainties, 
wind gusts and aerodynamic impacts. The Lyapunov theory is conducted to validate the closed-loop stability of 
the suggested controller. Conclusively, numerical simulations and semi-physical experiments demonstrated the 
effectiveness of the synthesized method with the existing results.
1 . Introduction
The quadrotor unmanned aerial vehicles (UAVs) are extensively 
utilized in a variety of fields due to their simple structure, strong ma­
neuverability, vertical take-off and landing (VTOL) capabilities and 
flexible control characteristics. The quadrotor UAVs may perform high-
maneuverability maneuvers, including hovering, fast turning and rolling 
by varying the speed of the four rotors. Owing to their ability to take off 
and land vertically and hover which doesn’t require a runway, they are 
appropriate for missions in confined spaces or complicated terrain, like 
indoor search and rescue, urban inspections, etc. The quadrotor UAVs 
are simpler and less expensive to manufacture than fixed-wing aircraft 
or helicopters [1,2]. They are likewise suited for large-scale forma­
tion operations and clustered collaborative control. There are numerous 
military and civilian uses for quadrotor unmanned aerial vehicles. For 
instance, they need to be able to automatically design the best course of 
action, detect pest and disease regions and track the condition of crops. 
In order to increase transportation efficiency and save labor costs, logis­
tics and transportation include tasks like medical supplies and expedited 
delivery. Border patrols and counterterrorism reconnaissance are a pos­
sibility by the employment of quad-rotor drones equipped with thermal 
imagers and target identification algorithms to continuously monitor 
border regions.
The quadrotor flight control, which is a common application plat­
form of contemporary control theory that incorporates intricate dynamic 
features such as multivariables, strong coupling and nonlinearity [3,4].

backstepping control, adaptive control etc. The hierarchical control ar­

tracking and obstacle avoidance functions. The inner loop attitude

The execution of the mission of the quadrotor drone will be unavoid­
ably impacted by a range of disruptions and model uncertainties due to 
the intricate working environment and unique dynamic features of the 
system [5,6]. The environmental elements such as gusts, atmospheric 
turbulence and sensor noise are the primary sources of external disrup­
tions [7]. The uncertainty of the quadrotor UAVs’ model also plays a 
substantial role in control performance, expressed as increased trajec­
tory tracking inaccuracy, attitude oscillation and even instability [8]. 
The system parameters vary as a result of the degradation of the sys­
tem power due to the drop in battery power. Establishing a dynamic 
model is challenging because of the rotor aerodynamic effect, which re­
duces the system’s accuracy. Taking into account that traditional linear 
control methods cannot effectively control the system under intense dis­
turbances, modern UAV control systems typically employ robust control, 
adaptive control, or disturbance observer (DOB)-based techniques to es­
timate and compensate for these uncertainties online. Furthermore, by 
learning to adjust to constantly changing surroundings, data-driven in­
telligent control techniques may continuously refine control strategies 
and demonstrate great adaptive capabilities. It is possible to signif­
icantly increase the stability and mission completion capabilities of 
UAVs in unstable environments by integrating model compensation and 
real-time adjustment systems. In order to solve the problem of exter­
nal disturbance, Jiang et al. [9] proposed an adaptive law combined 
with a backstepping sliding mode strategy. Considering the chattering 
and finite-time convergence problems of traditional integer-order sliding 
mode strategies, Labbadi et al. [10] proposed a global fractional-order 
adaptive sliding mode. To estimate and correct for external disturbances, 
Wang et al. [11] presented a control approach based on disturbance ob­
servers. Considering quadrotor UAVs with unknown models and external 
perturbations, Wang et al. [12] investigated an adaptive finite-time 
backstepping control strategy. Despite these developments, the major­
ity of disturbance assessment techniques are not always relevant in all 
operational situations and have limitations when it comes to managing 
erratic environmental circumstances.
In the actual engineering application of quadcopters, the control 
system is often required to reach a stable state within a finite time, 
while traditional control methods usually only guarantee the asymptotic 
convergence of the system. This limitation is particularly prominent in 
mission scenarios that require rapid response, such as emergency obsta­
cle avoidance, precise landing, etc. The terminal sliding mode control 
makes the system state converge within a finite time by designing a 
nonlinear sliding mode surface [13]. Nevertheless, there is a singularity 
problem that needs to be improved in combination with the nonsingular 
terminal sliding mode (NTSMC) [14]. The upper bound of the conver­
gence time of the fixed time control algorithm is independent of the 
initial state and is suitable for scenarios with uncertain initial conditions. 
The prescribed time control convergence time can be precisely utilized 
as a design parameter [15]. The finite-time observer can realize the 
finite time estimation of disturbances or uncertainties. Finite-time con­
trol techniques have become viable ways to get over the drawbacks of 
asymptotic stability methods when it comes to quadrotor UAV control’s 
convergence time. Nevertheless, the efficacy of these current finite-time 
control algorithms may be jeopardized in time-sensitive applications 
due to their poor convergence rates. Subsequently, conventional control 
frameworks sometimes lack the explicit convergence time assurances re­
quired for mission planning and safety considerations. The system can 
swiftly approach the sliding surface and stay securely fastened on it 
thanks to sliding mode control’s reaching control law.
The SMC algorithm which was initially proposed by Utkin [16] has 
been widely recognized for its robustness against matched uncertainties 
and disturbances. Nevertheless, chattering behaviors, sluggish conver­
gence speeds and instability under interference are problems associated 
with traditional SMC techniques [17]. By creating a basic first-order slid­
ing surface, traditional sliding mode control employs a sign function to 
achieve switching control. This allows the system to converge asymp­
totically to a stable state, but chattering is evident. The terminal sliding 
mode control (TSMC) achieves accurate convergence in finite time by 
using a nonlinear sliding surface. Singularity issues can be avoided with 
its enhanced non-singular terminal sliding mode (NTSMC). Through 
second-order design, the Super-Twisting algorithm which is a high-order 
sliding mode efficiently eliminates chattering by producing continuous 
control signals [18]. The integral sliding mode eliminates steady-state 
errors by introducing integral terms and is often utilized for hovering 
against constant wind disturbances [19]. The adaptive sliding mode ad­
justs the switching gain online to cope with parameter changes, which 
is suitable for battery attenuation or load change scenarios [20].
The distributed sliding mode method is applied for multi-machine 
formation collaborative control [21]. Although Huang et al. [22] pre­
sented an integral backstepping sliding mode control method that 
integrates into a conventional SMC framework and implies the ability 
to reject disturbances, its performance is still less robust and has a lower 
convergence rate than fractional-order SMC. A notable improvement 
over traditional SMC, Zhao et al. [23] also created an adaptive terminal 
sliding mode control with a nonlinear disturbance observer, although 
it still has drawbacks in terms of chattering suppression and finite-time 
stability guarantees under complex disturbances.
This paper addresses the impact of model uncertainties and stochas­
tic disturbances on system trajectory tracking. A super-twisting observer 
is designed to estimate and compensate for the disturbances. An adaptive 
fractional-order fast terminal sliding mode control (AFTSMC) approach 
is proposed to achieve precise trajectory tracking. The system stabil­
ity and finite-time convergence are proved based on Lyapunov theory. 
Combined with the fractional-order operators, the proposed control 
strategy demonstrates superior performance in chattering reduction, dy­
namic response and robustness. The main contributions of this technical 
note are exhibited as below.
1) This work constructs a new fractional-order terminal sliding sur­
face in contrast to the [17,22]. The fractional-order operator 
accelerates the state variables as they get closer to the sliding 
surface thereby increasing the system reaction speed. The non-
singular terminal sliding mode’s architecture ensures that the 
state variables converge to zero in a finite amount of time while 
reducing system chattering.
2) By accounting for parametric uncertainties and stochastic distur­
bances, this work expands the scope of application. In contrast 
to previous findings [24,25], the super-twisting sliding mode ob­
server is intended to simulate the inherent response delay and 
simultaneously realize system performance time control.
3) This paper conducts a quantitative analysis of the external distur­
bance and further optimizes the upper bound of system conver­
gence time. Simulations and hardware-in-the-loop experimental

6 . Conclusion
This work conceived an adaptive finite time control strategy for 
the quadrotor to track the desired trajectories subject to disturbances 
and uncertainties. The adaptive fractional-order sliding mode control 
was developed to provide finite-time convergence and boost system re­
silience. The super-twisting sliding mode observer was developed to 
handle external disturbances. The results showed better control accu­
racy, faster convergence and better disturbance rejection compared to 
traditional methods. The Lyapunov theory served as the foundation 
for the approval of the theoretical analysis. Furthermore, the simula­
tions illustrated the verified approach and contrasted it with the current 
findings that show its superiority. Additionally, the semi-physical ex­
perimental implementations provide experimental validation for the 
developed scheme.
Although the effectiveness of the proposed method has been val­
idated through numerical simulations and semi-physical experiments, 
several promising directions warrant further investigation. Future work 
will explore conducting flight experiments on a physical quadrotor 
UAV platform to validate the algorithm’s performance in real flight 
environments.
