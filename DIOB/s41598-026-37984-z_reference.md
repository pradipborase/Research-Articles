Abstract 
 
Unmanned Aerial Vehicles (UAVs) are increasingly deployed in complex, uncertain, 
and adversarial environments, yet they remain vulnerable to actuator faults, 
environmental disturbances, and deliberate interferences such as jamming or spoofing. 
Conventional control approaches typically address these challenges independently, 
focusing on either fault-tolerant control (FTC), disturbance rejection, or counter-UAV 
defense. This paper presents an integrated anti-interference and fault-tolerant control 
framework that unifies three complementary modules: (i) residual-based fault detection 
and isolation, (ii) adaptive extended state observer (AESO)-based disturbance 
estimation and compensation, and (iii) counter-UAV evasive maneuver strategies. The 
framework is formulated using a nonlinear UAV dynamic model and rigorously 
analyzed under Lyapunov stability theory. Simulation results demonstrate significant 
performance improvements compared with baseline controllers. The proposed method 
reduced position and attitude deviations to below 0.05 m and 0.03 deg, respectively, 
under simultaneous actuator faults and wind disturbances. Fault and disturbance 
estimation errors remained below 0.05 and 0.03 units, respectively, ensuring timely 
control reconfiguration. Furthermore, the UAV achieved a 100% success rate in 
counter-drone evasive maneuvers while maintaining trajectory stability. These results 
confirm that the integrated design provides high resilience, rapid recovery, and reliable 
performance in fault-disturbed and adversarial conditions. By bridging FTC, adaptive 
disturbance rejection, and counter-UAV defense, the proposed framework advances the 
state of the art in resilient UAV control for civilian and defense applications. 
Keywords: UAVs; fault-tolerant control; anti-jamming; fault detection and isolation 
(FDI); disturbance estimation; counter-UAVs defense; resilience

1. Introduction 
The rapid evolution of autonomous aerial systems has redefined the landscape of 
modern aviation, where resilience, adaptability, and intelligent control are now 
paramount to mission success. As UAVs transition from controlled laboratory 
environments to real-world, dynamic, and adversarial conditions, their exposure to 
unpredictable disturbances, sensor and actuator degradations, and cyber-physical 
interferences has intensified. Conventional control strategies, typically designed for 
nominal conditions, often struggle to maintain stability and performance when 
confronted with simultaneous system faults and environmental uncertainties. This 
growing operational complexity necessitates integrated, process-oriented control 
frameworks capable of detecting faults, compensating disturbances, and countering 
external interferences in real time. Against this backdrop, the present study introduces a 
unified anti-interference and fault-tolerant control approach that bridges theoretical 
advancements in adaptive estimation and robust control with practical demands of 
resilient UAV operation. 
Unmanned Aerial Vehicles (UAVs) have transitioned from niche experimental 
platforms into indispensable tools across diverse applications, including environmental 
monitoring, logistics, defense surveillance, and disaster response [1-4]. Their versatility, 
low operational cost, and capability to perform missions in hazardous environments 
make them attractive alternatives to manned aircraft. However, as UAVs grow more 
autonomous and operate in increasingly complex airspaces, ensuring their safety and 
reliability has become a pressing concern [5-8]. A primary challenge is that UAVs are 
inherently vulnerable to systemic faults, environmental disturbances, and deliberate 
adversarial interferences. Addressing these threats requires a comprehensive control 
strategy that integrates fault-tolerant mechanisms with interference mitigation [6-8]. 
 First, internal system faults in UAVs can manifest in numerous forms, such as 
actuator degradation, control surface jamming, or sensor bias and drift. For example, 
partial thrust loss in a quadrotor’s motor can destabilize flight, while erroneous 
gyroscope measurements can mislead the controller into executing erroneous 
commands [9-12]. Recent studies have increasingly demonstrated that advanced control 
schemes particularly those employing disturbance observers, adaptive mechanisms, and 
evolutionary optimization, offer substantial benefits in handling uncertainties, external

2. Literature Review 
2.1 UAV Fault-Tolerant Control  
Fault-Tolerant Control (FTC) has been a cornerstone of UAV reliability research for 
more than two decades. FTC strategies are broadly divided into passive and active 
approaches. Passive FTC relies on inherent robustness of control laws or hardware 
redundancy to sustain stability and trajectory tracking performance without explicit 
fault detection [21-23, 26, 27]. Although simple and computationally efficient, passive 
methods often increase system weight and cost, making them unsuitable for

small-scale UAVs. Active FTC, in contrast, explicitly detects and isolates faults through 
residual generation or observer-based schemes, or subsequently reconfigures the 
control law to compensate for the identified fault [26-30]. This dynamic adaptability 
makes active FTC more effective in ensuring long-term operational reliability under 
actuator or sensor malfunctions. 
For UAVs, observer-based methods, such as Kalman filters and Unknown Input 
Observers (UIOs), are widely employed because of their capability to estimate 
unmeasured states and identify abnormal system behaviors. However, most FTC 
studies address only isolated single-fault scenarios, such as actuator failure, while 
rarely address simultaneous faults coupled with external disturbances [29-33]. 
Moreover, real-time controller reconfiguration in these studies typically assumes 
reliable communication channels and sensors feedback, assumptions that fail in 
adversarial or degraded environments.  
Recent advances have introduced machine learning-based fault diagnosis 
frameworks that facilitate pattern recognition in complex fault signatures. Nevertheless, 
these data-driven methods are often constrained by the availability  of representative 
datasets and the lack of interpretability, which limits their deployment in safety-critical 
UAV operations [31-34]. The literature thus highlights a persistent gap: FTC methods 
must transition from isolated designs to integrated architecture that harmonize fault 
diagnosis, disturbance rejection and anti-interference capabilities. Without such 
integration, UAVs remain vulnerable to cascading failures and mission degradation in 
realistic and uncertain environments. 
2.2 Disturbance Estimation and Rejection  
Disturbance estimation plays an essential role in enhancing UAV robustness under 
uncertain and dynamic operating conditions. Traditional control methods rely on 
precise mathematical model models; however, UAVs are inherently operated under 
aerodynamic nonlinearities, wind gusts, and payload variations that are challenging to 
characterize accurately [32-37]. To address these modelling uncertainties Disturbance 
observers (DOs) and Extended State Observers (ESOs) have emerged as widely 
recognized and effective tools for real-time disturbance estimation. In particular, Active 
Disturbance Rejection Control (ADRC) has shown significant potential by 
compensating for lumped disturbances through the inclusion of extended system states 
[34-36]. 
In UAV applications, ESOs are capable of simultaneously estimating both the 
system states and unknown external inputs. The adaptive variants, termed as Adaptive 
Extended State Observer (AESOs), dynamically adjust observer gains to improve 
robustness against time-varying uncertainties [31-35]. Despite these advances, most 
ESO-based frameworks are validated in controlled laboratory environments and 
seldom account for adversarial or harsh external conditions. Furthermore, disturbance 
observers often face difficulties in distinguishing genuine environmental disturbances

Hybrid approaches that integrate disturbance estimation with FTC have shown

Recent advances in UAV control highlight a strong shift toward intelligent, adaptive, 
and robust methodologies capable of handling disturbances, uncertainties, and

constrained control designs, including the constrained adaptive controller introduced 
in Engineering Research Express [45], further underscore the community’s ongoing 
efforts to guarantee stability under parameter uncertainties and operational constraints. 
More recently, PSO-tuned fractional-order sliding-mode strategies for agricultural

tracking by combining optimization heuristics with high-gain robustness.

synchronous demodulation transforms and chirplet-based scaling reassignment

meshing-order-modulated fault indicators in rotating machinery and gearboxes [47,53].

architectures reported in Robotics and Autonomous Systems emphasize unified fault 
accommodation and disturbance rejection for autonomous platforms operating under 
compounded uncertainties [58]. Likewise, recent studies in IEEE Transactions on 
Intelligent Vehicles highlight the necessity of co-designing estimation, fault diagnosis, 
and control to maintain stability and performance when multiple fault and disturbance 
sources coexist [59]. Furthermore, delay-aware robust FTC strategies presented in the 
International Journal of Control demonstrate that treating faults, disturbances, and 
delays independently can lead to performance degradation in cyber–physical systems 
[60]. These works collectively underscore the need for holistic, stability-guaranteed 
control designs, motivating the integrated fault diagnosis, adaptive disturbance 
estimation, and resilient control framework proposed in this study. 
Despite these advances, most existing studies address these challenges in isolation, 
focusing primarily on either disturbance attenuation, robustness against parametric 
variations, or improved nonlinear tracking. None of the referenced works integrate 
fault diagnosis, adaptive disturbance estimation, and counter-UAV anti-interference 
strategies into a unified, stability-guaranteed control framework. The contribution of 
the present study directly fills this critical gap.

2.3 Counter-Drone Strategies 
The rapid proliferation of small UAVs has created an urgent need for counter-drone 
strategies to safeguard airspace security. Malicious or unauthorized UAVs pose diverse 
risks ranging from surveillance breaches to physical attacks on critical infrastructure. 
Traditional counter-drone defense typically employs techniques such as detection, 
jamming, spoofing, or physical interception [31-37]. However, defensive UAVs must be 
resilient against these very same adversarial measures. In this context, therefore, 
counter-drone strategies should not be viewed merely external defense mechanism but 
as intrinsically embedded components of UAV control architectures. Integrating 
anti-interference measures directly into flight control systems enhances mission 
survivability 
and 
autonomy 
under 
contested 
or 
degraded 
communication 
environments [37-41, 61]. 
Existing research on counter-drone operations has predominantly emphasized on 
external mitigation methods such as RF jamming, GNSS spoofing, or net-based 
physical capture. While these approaches are effective for neutralizing unauthorized 
UAVs in security operations, they do not enhance the survivability UAVs when 
adversarial interference directly targets control signals or navigation systems. For 
instance, GPS spoofing can deliberately misguide UAV trajectory controllers, while RF 
jamming can disrupt ground communication links between the UAV and the ground 
control station [62-65]. These forms of interference exploit vulnerabilities that are 
typically beyond the scope of conventional FTC and disturbance rejection frameworks. 
Recent research trends advocate resilient autonomy, enabling UAVs to sustain 
mission objectives under contested or adversarial conditions. Such techniques include 
trajectory deception (e.g., generating false waypoints to mislead hostile agents), 
adaptive signal filtering to counteract jamming, and evasive maneuvers modeled 
through real-time optimization models [68-70]. Despite these promising advancements, 
these current strategies remain fragmented across different research domains leading 
to a lack of unified control architecture. The integration of counter-drone defense 
within UAV FTC architectures and disturbance estimation frameworks remain largely 
unexplored, constituting a critical research gap [61-66]. Addressing this challenge 
requires a holistic approach that not only defends against external threats but also 
ensures internal resilience against fault and disturbances. Embedding counter-drone 
modules directly within the UAVs control hierarchy can enable robust, adaptive 
performance in both uncertain environments and actively contested airspaces.

4.1 System Architecture 
The proposed anti-interference and fault-tolerant control framework for UAVs 
integrates three modules, (a) self-fault diagnosis module, (b) disturbance estimation 
module and (c) counter-drone strategy module. These modules operate synergistically 
to ensure robustness, adaptability and survivability under fault and disturbance 
conditions as shown in Figure 1.  
(a) Self-Fault Diagnosis Module: 
This module implements residual-based observer design as defined in Equations 
(12), (16), (22), and (24). It identifies both both additive and multiplicative actuator 
faults (αi,βi) in real time. Once a fault is detected, the control commands are

(b) Disturbance Estimation Module:

(c) Counter-Drone Strategy Module: 
The counter-drone strategy module implements a deterministic, rule-based decision 
logic that transforms multi-modal sensor inputs into explicit threat declarations and 
corresponding evasive actions. This formal logic framework enables robust 
discrimination between genuine external threats and internal system anomalies while 
triggering appropriate, threat-specific avoidance maneuvers. 
Threat Declaration Framework 
The system employs a multi-layered threat assessment architecture that 
continuously monitors three distinct threat modalities. A threat is officially declared, 
and evasive maneuvers are activated, when any of the following conditions is satisfied 
for a minimum persistence duration threat to prevent transient false positives. 
RF Jamming Threat 
Declared upon detection of sustained degradation in the control communication 
link. This is triggered either by a drop in received signal power below a viable 
operational threshold or by the measured jamming-to-signal ratio exceeding a defined 
limit for a confirmed duration. This condition targets intentional radio frequency 
interference designed to disrupt command and control. 
GPS Spoofing Threat 
Declared upon detection of a significant, inexplicable discrepancy between the 
position solution derived from the Global Navigation Satellite System (GNSS) and the 
position propagated by the onboard Inertial Navigation System (INS). This declaration 
also requires accompanying anomalies in GNSS signal integrity flags, such as invalid 
checksums or inconsistent satellite geometry, to distinguish spoofing from mere GNSS 
signal blockage or multipath error. 
Physical Collision Threat 
Declared upon the detection of an object (via onboard vision, LiDAR, or radar) 
penetrating a predefined protected zone around the UAV. The declaration further 
requires that the object's relative velocity vector projects a future intersection point, as 
calculated by a time-to-collision (TTC) metric falling below a critical threshold. This 
condition addresses both static obstacles and dynamic adversarial drones on an 
interception course.

(AESO), Control Reconfiguration Logic, and Counter-Drone Strategy (CDS). Measured 
outputs (y) from onboard sensors feed into the FDI and AESO modules. The FDI

fault and disturbance estimates are passed to the Reconfiguration Logic, which 
computes specific control adjustments (Δu_i). Concurrently, the CDS module monitors 
for adversarial threats (jamming, spoofing, collision) and, if detected, calculates evasive 
trajectory corrections (Δx_c, Δy_c, Δz_c). These corrections modify the position 
setpoints for the Nominal Controller. The final, reconfigured control inputs (u_new) are 
sent to the UAV actuators, enabling simultaneous compensation for internal faults, 
external disturbances, and active evasion of threats.

(20–50 Hz) without exceeding allowable control latency. While our MATLAB/Simulink 
profiling shows that the full algorithm executes within 1.8–2.3 ms per cycle on an 
ARM-A57 equivalent processor, the computational load may challenge ultra-light 
microcontrollers commonly used in small quadrotors. Furthermore, although the AESO 
and FDI modules are designed to attenuate sensor noise, high-frequency IMU noise and 
intermittent GPS dropouts still influence estimation accuracy, especially during 
aggressive maneuvering or jamming-contested environments. 
Second, real-world deployment introduces practical challenges not fully captured in 
simulation. Wind turbulence can be more irregular and unstructured than the stochastic 
models used here, and urban canyon effects may degrade GPS–INS consistency, 
potentially delaying fault or spoofing detection. The counter-drone evasive strategy, 
while effective in simulation, may require additional airspace rules, collision-avoidance 
protocols, or inter-UAV communication layers to be certified for operational 
environments. Battery limitations also constrain how long aggressive avoidance 
maneuvers can be sustained without compromising mission endurance. These 
considerations highlight the need for hardware-in-the-loop testing and field validation, 
which we identify as essential future steps for transitioning the proposed architecture 
toward full real-time deployment. 
To mitigate the associated computational burden, several practical strategies can be 
adopted. First, the observer and fault-diagnosis modules can be implemented in a 
modular and event-triggered manner, allowing the AESO bandwidth adaptation and 
fault-residual evaluation to be activated only during detected transients, thereby 
reducing average computational load. Second, algorithmic optimization through 
fixed-point implementation, gain precomputation, and reduced-order observer 
formulations can significantly lower execution time without sacrificing stability. From a 
hardware perspective, the proposed framework is well suited for embedded platforms 
with floating-point support, such as ARM Cortex-A or Cortex-M7 processors, and can 
be efficiently deployed on companion computers commonly used in autonomous UAVs. 
These measures ensure that the proposed architecture remains computationally feasible 
while preserving real-time performance and robustness.

7. Conclusion 
This study proposed a comprehensive anti-interference and fault-tolerant control 
framework for UAVs, integrating residual-based fault diagnosis, adaptive disturbance 
estimation, and counter-drone evasive strategies. This approach was validated through 
extensive simulations encompassing actuator faults, environmental disturbances, and 
adversarial UAV encounters. Key contributions and findings include: 
1. Trajectory and Attitude Robustness: The UAV maintained position and attitude 
deviations below 0.05 m and 0.03 deg, respectively, even under simultaneous faults 
and disturbances. 
2. Accurate Fault and Disturbance Estimation: Residual-based observers and AESO 
achieved estimation errors below 0.05 units, enabling real-time and effective control 
reconfiguration. 
3. Counter-Drone Evasion: The UAV successfully executed evasive maneuvers 
across all test scenarios, ensuring safety and operational continuity with minimal 
trajectory deviation. 
4. Integrated Framework Advantage: Combining fault detection, disturbance 
estimation, and counter-drone strategies enhanced UAV resilience, outperforming 
conventional single-focus methods, enhancing resilience and adaptability in 
dynamic environments. 
Overall, the proposed framework delivers high-fidelity trajectory tracking, reliable 
fault detection, robust disturbance rejection, and effective threat avoidance, 
highlighting its strong potential for real-world UAV operations. Future work will focus 
on hardware-in-the-loop experiments, multi-UAV coordination, and large-scale field 
deployment to further establish the system’s scalability and operational robustness. 
Future real-time implementation will further investigate lightweight embedded 
deployment, advanced sensor-fusion denoising, and multi-baseline experimental 
validation to continue improving scalability and practical adoption.
