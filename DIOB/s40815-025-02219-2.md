A Comparative Study Between Different Fuzzy-Based Nonlinear
Controllers for UAV Coaxial Octorotor
Khaled Toudji1 · Lakhmissi Cherroun1 · Mohamed Nadour1 · Vicenç Puig2 ·
Ahmed Hafaifa1
Received: 28 August 2025 / Revised: 25 November 2025 / Accepted: 29 December 2025
© The Author(s) 2026
Abstract This paper presents and compares three fuzzy-
based nonlinear control strategies: fuzzy PD controller
(FPDC), sliding-mode PID (SMC-PID), and fuzzy sliding-
mode controller (FSMC) for the altitude and attitude regula-
tion of a six-degree-of-freedom (6-DOF) coaxial octorotor
UAV. The proposed FSMC integrates a type-1 Mamdani
fuzzy inference system in the switching law of SMC to sup-
press chattering while maintaining robustness and stability.
A full 6-DOF dynamic model is used to evaluate controller’s
performances under rectangular and circular trajectories,
with and without external wind disturbances. Quantitative
evaluation using ITAE, IAE, and control effort indices shows
that FSMC achieves the lowest tracking error and energy con-
sumption compared with FPDC and SMC-PID. The obtained
results conﬁrm that FSMC provides superior tracking accu-
racy, robustness, and energy efﬁciency, which makes it a
promising approach for real-time UAV applications.

1 Introduction
A multirotor UAV is an autonomous aerial system equipped
with more than two rotors. Several conﬁgurations (such
as birotor, trirotor, quadrotor, hexarotor, and octorotor) are

used in practice. Multirotor UAVs offer several advantages,
including vertical take-off and landing (VTOL), hovering
ability, and strong resistance to external disturbances. As a
result, they have been widely adopted in ﬁelds such as aerial
photography, precision agriculture, search and rescue opera-
tions, and military applications [1].
Among them, octorotor UAVs stand out due to their supe-
rior payload capacity since they can carry heavier loads more
efﬁciently than other multirotor types. They are categorized
into two main conﬁgurations: star and coaxial. In the star con-
ﬁguration, eight DC motors are positioned at distinct points,
whereas in the coaxial design, the motors are arranged in
pairs along the same axis. Octorotor UAVs offer numerous
beneﬁts, such as VTOL capability and stable hovering, while
also exhibiting greater resilience to environmental distur-
bances compared to other drone types. Consequently, their
use has been expanding rapidly across multiple industries [2].
Octorotor UAVs play a crucial role in various ﬁelds, partic-
ularly in research, where they contribute to advancements
in artiﬁcial intelligence, robust control systems, and the
development of intelligent control frameworks. Despite the
multidomain applications advantage, controlling a coaxial
octorotor remains a challenging task because of its nonlinear
and strongly coupled six-degree-of-freedom dynamics. The
system exhibits multiple interacting subsystems (altitude,
pitch, roll, yaw, and horizontal motion) that are sensitive to
parameter variations and external disturbances such as wind
gusts. Designing a controller that ensures accurate trajectory
tracking while maintaining robustness and energy efﬁciency
is a key research problem addressed in this work.
Octorotors,asnonlinearmulti-inputmulti-output(MIMO)
dynamic systems, exhibit signiﬁcant uncertainties that com-
plicate their control and making stability assurance a critical
challenge. To overcome these complexities, researchers have
extensively explored advanced control techniques, including
both conventional nonlinear controllers and intelligent con-
trol approaches. Additionally, hybrid control strategies that
integrate artiﬁcial intelligence with traditional methods have
gained considerable attention within the research commu-
nity for their potential to enhance system performance and
adaptability.
The control strategies for coaxial octorotors closely
resemble those of quadrotors. Consequently, the extensive
literature on quadrotor path tracking and stability provides a
strong foundation for advancing coaxial octorotor control
research [3–5,9–13]. Studies dedicated to coaxial octoro-
tor control, such as [6,8–10,13], consider dynamic models
of coaxial octorotors using the Newton–Euler method, cap-
turing both position and attitude dynamics. These models
serve as the foundation for designing controllers capable
of addressing the system inherent nonlinearities and high
coupling effects. Several control strategies have been pro-
posed for coaxial octorotors, ranging from fuzzy logic and
sliding-mode control to backstepping and hybrid approaches
incorporating neural networks. Among these, some novel
methods show signiﬁcant promise in mitigating the inher-
ent control challenges of coaxial octorotors.
Hybrid approaches have proven highly effective in over-
coming control challenges. In [3], a PID sliding-mode fuzzy
logic controller was proposed, where the PID controller
acted as a switching surface and was auto-tuned using
gradient descent techniques, while fuzzy logic minimized
chattering effects. Likewise, Reference [4] explored meta-
heuristic optimization (cuckoo optimization and particle
swarmapproaches)inanexponentialsliding-modecontroller
integrated with an adaptive fuzzy type-2 neural network.
Moreover [9], optimization methods such as genetic algo-
rithms and particle swarm optimization were employed to
ﬁne-tune parametric gains in sliding mode controllers. Sim-
ilarly, Reference [11] the combined used of fuzzy logic with
backstepping controllers, while Reference [12] implemented
a backstepping fast terminal sliding-mode controller cou-
pled with an extended inverse multi-quadratic radial basis
function neural network for disturbance observation. Further
advancementispresentedin[14]whereanadaptivebackstep-
ping sliding-mode controller was developed with optimized
gains using the Levenberg–Marquardt algorithm, ensuring
stability through Lyapunov theory. Similarly, Reference [15]
introduced a fault-tolerant fuzzy sliding-mode controller that
employed a radial basis function neural network (RBFNN) to
estimate control laws without relying on inverse dynamics.
Other hybrid approaches combine fuzzy sliding-mode
controllers with adaptive interval type-2 fuzzy logic or
integrate robust backstepping sliding-mode controllers with
adaptive RBFNNs to effectively compensate for system non-
linearities and external disturbances [7]. Additionally, Ref-
erence [16] proposed a fault-tolerant control strategy based
on adaptive global fast dynamic sliding-mode techniques,
augmented with fuzzy logic for uncertainty estimation. On
the other hand, Reference [17] proposes a novel multiple-
input–multiple-output (MIMO) super-twisting sliding-mode
controller (SMC) that uses a Multilayer Perceptron Neural
Network (MLPNN) to estimate uncertainties and distur-
bances. The stability of the closed-loop system is ensured
through Lyapunov stability theory, which also facilitates
the formulation of real-time update laws for the MLPNN
weights.
Control methodologies for quadrotors have also seen sig-
niﬁcant advancements. References [18–24] have extensively
explored robust and hybrid control techniques, including
sliding-mode control and fuzzy logic. In [18], a back prop-
agation neural network is used to adapt the sliding-mode
surface in response to external disturbances, ensuring sta-
bility through Lyapunov theory. Similarly, Reference [25]
applied a fuzzy logic-based approach for efﬁcient octorotor

Although the recent studies in the dynamic event trig-

In this paper, we consider the nonlinearities and strong
coupling effects inherent in the dynamic models of a coax-
ial octorotor by implementing and analyzing fuzzy-based
nonlinear controllers for a six-degree-of-freedom (6-DOF)
system. Theproposedcontrol frameworkis validatedthrough
simulations considering multiple desired trajectories in sim-
ulation using a MATLAB/Simulink-based dynamic model
of the coaxial octorotor. Furthermore, this paper introduces
the design and simulation of robust and adaptive control
strategies tailored speciﬁcally for coaxial octorotors. Build-
ing upon established techniques, we explore novel controller
combinations to address the unique dynamic challenges of
thesesystems,contributingtotheexpandingresearchonmul-
tirotor UAV control. Finally, a comprehensive performance
comparison of the proposed controllers regarding their effec-
tiveness and stability is presented.
The main contributions and innovations of this paper are
summarized as follows:
(1) Novel FSMC Structure: We designed a dedicated type-
1 Mamdani fuzzy logic controller (FLC) that directly
replaces the discontinuous switching term of the sliding-
mode controller (SMC). This single-input–single-output
(SISO) fuzzy inference system takes the sliding surface
as its input and outputs a continuous control signal,
thereby eliminating chattering without introducing a
boundary layer that could compromise robustness. This
structure provides a new smooth-switching mechanism
speciﬁcally tailored for the nonlinear 6-DOF coaxial
octorotor dynamics.
(2) ComprehensiveComparativeStudy:Thepaperpresents
a thorough comparative analysis of three distinct control
strategies: fuzzy PD controller (FPDC), sliding-mode
PID (SMC-PID), and the proposed fuzzy sliding-mode
controller (FSMC). The controllers are evaluated not
only in terms of tracking performance (ITAE and IAE

2 Coaxial Octorotor Dynamic Modeling
The dynamic model of the considered octorotor is based
on the models presented in [21,29]. The coaxial octorotor
dynamics is the same as the quadrotor, where the coaxial
octorotor is better than the star octorotor in stability and size.
In addition, a classical octorotor needs the addition of four
arms that are taller than the other ones to ensure the separa-
tion between the used rotors. Figure1 shows an example of
a coaxial octorotor, which is an Onyx Star.
2.1 Coaxial Octorotor Conﬁguration
The coaxial octorotor is a multirotor UAV that has eight
rotors. The coaxial octorotor can be considered a quadro-
Fig. 1 Onyx Star coaxial octorotor
tor, but in each corner, there are two coaxial rotors, and each
one has an opposite direction of rotation. The dynamics of
the octorotor are derived using two inertia frames [Xe, Ye,
Ze] and a body frame Fb [Xb, Yb, Zb] to describe the rotation
and the movement of the coaxial octorotor. Also, this UAV
has six degrees of freedom because it has three movement
axis: X, Y, and Z, and three rotations about the X, Y, and
Z axis called, respectively, pitch, roll, and yaw [30]. Fig-
ure2 describes the conﬁguration and frames used to model
this UAV. This conﬁguration gives the coaxial octorotor the
ability of vertical landing and hovering.
2.2 Coaxial Octorotor Dynamics
The dynamic modeling of the octorotor leads to a MIMO
nonlinear system with high coupling which is derived using
Newton–Euler method following the below hypothesis to
obtain the dynamic model [21,29,31,32]:
• The octorotor is supposed as a rigid body and symmetri-
cal.
• The center of mass of the octorotor and the origin of the
body frame are supposed to coincide.
• The rotors are supposed rigid.
• The lift and drag forces are proportional to the square of
the angular velocity.
The octorotor vehicle has six degrees of freedom (DOF)
because it can do a vertical movement in x, y axis and an
horizontal motion in z axis. In addition, it can roll and pitch
about x, y and yaw in z axis. These movements are done in
the body frame Fb which is aligned to the center of mass of
the octorotor. For that, in order to describe these movements
in the inertia F frame, we need to transform these move-
ments from the Fe to Fb. This transformation is done using
the following transformation matrix T [10,30,32]:

3 The Proposed Controllers
The coaxial octorotor is a high-coupled MIMO nonlinear
system with uncertainties and external disturbances during
motion. To ensure an effective guidance system, a stable and
robust controller is required. In this section, the proposed
control structures using different controller types are illus-
trated and discussed. We have used different techniques (such
as SMC and fuzzy Logic) to guide and stabilize the coaxial
octorotor.
3.1 Overall Control Structure
The overall control architecture of the coaxial octorotor is
organized into four main subsystems: altitude (z), position
(X and Y), and attitude [pitch (θ), roll (ϕ), and yaw (ψ)].
The altitude and yaw motions are each regulated by ded-
icated controllers, whereas the pitch and roll subsystems
form the inner attitude-control loop, with the X−Y transla-
tional motion forming the outer position-control loop. Each

structure shown in Fig.3. This ﬁgure presents the generalized
control framework for all proposed fuzzy-based nonlin-

3.3 Fuzzy Proportional–Derivative Controller (FPDC)
The second control strategy is a fuzzy logic controller (FLC)
for the altitude, roll, pitch, and yaw subsystems. This is a
model-free approach that relies on expert knowledge.
Each FLC has two inputs: the error (e) and the derivative
of the error (de). The output is the control action.
• The input e uses ﬁve trapezoidal membership functions:
Negative Big (NB), Negative (N), Near Zero (NZ), Pos-
itive (P), and Positive Big (PB), as shown in Fig. 5.
• The input de uses three Gaussian membership functions:
Negative (N), Zero (Z), and Positive (P), as shown in Fig.
6.
• The output uses ﬁve membership functions: Go Much
Down (GDM), Go Down (GD), Stay (S), Go Up (GU),
and Go Up Much (GUM), as shown in Fig. 7.
• The crisp output is inferred using the centroid method.

– Discussion on the Innovations of the Proposed FSMC
The proposed fuzzy sliding-mode controller (FSMC)
introduces several structural and theoretical innovations
compared with previously developed hybrid control schemes
such as classical fuzzy SMC and adaptive fuzzy–neural con-
trollers.
Unlike many existing methods in which fuzzy logic
merely adjusts switching gains or tunes SMC parameters, the
proposed FSMC replaces the discontinuous switching term
[Ks ·sign(S)] entirely with a continuous fuzzy inference sig-
nal Kf(S).
Thismodiﬁcationremovestheneedforanartiﬁcialbound-
ary layer that can weaken robustness and ensures a smooth
control signal that effectively eliminates chattering.
Structurally,theFSMCadoptsasingle-inputsingle-output
(SISO) fuzzy logic framework using only the sliding surface
S as input.
This conﬁguration simpliﬁes computation and facilitates
real-time implementation for the 6-DOF coaxial octorotor,

the smallest Integral of Absolute Error (IAE) and Integral
of Time-weighted Absolute Error (ITAE) indices, conﬁrm-
ing superior tracking accuracy and faster stabilization. It
also demonstrates the lowest control effort compared with
FPDC and SMC-PID, which translates to reduced energy
consumption. In the presence of external wind disturbances,
FSMC maintains trajectory tracking with minimal deviation,
proving its robustness and adaptability. In contrast, FPDC
provides acceptable performance with simpler structure and
lower computational demand, making it suitable for low-cost
systems where extreme precision is not required. SMC-PID
maintains strong robustness to disturbances but generates
larger oscillations and higher energy usage due to the dis-
continuous switching term. These comparative ﬁndings show
that FSMC achieves the most balanced performance in terms
of accuracy, robustness, and energy efﬁciency.
Overall, the FSMC structure offers a promising solution
for the control of coaxial octorotor UAVs, combining the
advantages of fuzzy inference and sliding-mode control. Its
ability to ensure smooth response, reduced chattering, and
reliable disturbance rejection conﬁrms its suitability for real-
time implementation in autonomous ﬂight applications.
Future work will focus on adaptive tuning of fuzzy and
sliding parameters using optimization and machine learning
algorithms. Additional research will also include experi-
mental validation on a real coaxial octorotor platform and
extension of the control design to handle actuator faults and
sensor noise for practical ﬁeld deployment.
