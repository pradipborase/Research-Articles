Abstract
This work proposes a disturbance observer (DOB)-based fault-tolerant control (FTC) approach for a quadcopter subject to total
rotor failure. The main goal is to develop a robust control strategy capable of maintaining partial Ô¨āight control and stabilizing
the quadcopter even after a complete failure of one rotor. This is a major challenge, as losing one rotor breaks the thrust
symmetry, leading to instability. The proposed approach joins two key components: a primary axis controller that enables the
quadrotor to regulate both its spatial position and inclination, and a DOB designed to estimate and compensate for disturbances
caused by rotor failures and cross-coupling effects. Leveraging nested control loops and quadratic programming optimization,
the control signals are dynamically allocated in response to real-time angular acceleration feedback, thereby reducing reliance
on an accurate plant model. The method is shown in high-Ô¨Ādelity MATLAB/Simulink simulations to stabilize the quadcopter
post-failure, with comparative analysis indicating high performance over a used estimator in a prior investigation.

2.3 Main objectives and methodology
The primary objective is to reconÔ¨Āgure the control system
immediately upon detecting a motor failure, ensuring sub-
optimal tracking of reference trajectories. However, in the
event of a sudden and complete rotor failure, perfect trajec-
tory tracking becomes impossible as losing one rotor in a
quadrotor forces the sacriÔ¨Āce of a degree of freedom (the
roll). The proposed approach involves generating, when one
unknown rotor is completely fault, a Ô¨Āctitious control signal
u1 = [Fp ŌĄp ŌĄq]T via the three remaining torque rotors to
maintain position tracking (x, y, z) even if the drone experi-
ences rotation around its axis.
To achieve this goal, Ô¨Ārstly, a precise fault detection and
amplitude estimation should be performed using a dedicated
observer. In particular, a high-gain disturbance observer is
selected to reconstruct abrupt rotor failure. Subsequently,
an adaptive control reconÔ¨Āguration will be implemented to
compensate for the failure. In this case, nested loops should
be used to maintain Euler angles and normalized accelera-
tion around their Ô¨Āctive trajectories. In addition, quadratic
programming-based dynamic control allocation is adopted
to avoid switching between safety and faulty modes.

4.1 Global structure of control
The complete control scheme is illustrated in Fig. 3 and
consists of four nested loops combined with a disturbance
observer. The outermost loop is responsible for position
tracking and produces the normalized acceleration n3, which
serves as the input to the subsequent attitude controller. The
angular rate controller, receiving the virtual angular rate ref-

angular rate, outputs the desired control signal ud. Before
reaching the allocation loop, each signal is adjusted using the
rotor fault estimate. Finally, the allocation loop computes the
desired torques to be applied to the quadrotor model.
