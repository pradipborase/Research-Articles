Abstract
To address the requirements for multi-channel decoupling and high-precision control in
quadrotor UAV systems, this paper proposes a novel intelligent controller (TD3-ADRC)
which integrates Active Disturbance Rejection Control (ADRC) with the Twin Delayed Deep
Deterministic Policy Gradient (TD3) algorithm. Firstly, the dynamic model of the quadrotor
is established. Secondly, a parameterized tanh function is introduced and applied to design
the tracking differentiator, extended state observer, and nonlinear feedback control law.
Then, the TD3 learning mechanism is incorporated to automatically learn and optimize
controller parameters, thereby significantly enhancing the system’s disturbance rejection
capability. Finally, simulation studies comparing conventional PID, ADRC, DDPG and
the proposed TD3-ADRC algorithms are conducted in Simulink. In addition, a bench
test system is developed using the PX4 flight controller. Experimental results show that,
under complex environmental conditions, the proposed TD3-ADRC controller outperforms
both conventional PID and linear ADRC methods in terms of reliability and adaptability,
validating the effectiveness of the proposed control approach.

1. Introduction
In recent years, unmanned aerial vehicles (UAVs) have been widely employed in both
military and civilian applications. Compared to fixed-wing aircraft, rotary-wing UAVs
are more suitable for operations in confined spaces, such as urban areas, mountainous
regions, or forests. However, this also introduces increased complexity in the control of
rotary-wing UAVs. The control systems of rotary-wing UAVs exhibit strong coupling and
underactuation, and their performance significantly influences the flight quality of the
aircraft. In addition, due to the relatively low mass of quadrotor aircraft, they are inevitably
subjected to external disturbances such as wind gusts during normal flight. The key to
ensuring safe flight and mission accomplishment for quadrotors lies in the availability of
high-performance controllers that guarantee stability and reliability in complex environ-
ments. In recent years, various intelligent learning algorithms have garnered significant
attention as effective tools for enhancing the robustness of nonlinear systems. Many re-
searchers have leveraged the superior approximation capabilities of intelligent learning
methods to design attitude tracking control strategies for quadrotors.
Currently, several control theories are commonly applied to such aircrafts, including
the following:
(1) The PID controller [1–4], as one of the most widely used control methods in engi-
neering applications, is characterized by its simplicity, broad applicability, and stability.
It remains the most mainstream control strategy for multi-rotor UAVs on the market. Qi
Naiming [5] adopted an active disturbance rejection PID control approach by incorporating
a tracking differentiator, resolving the inherent trade-off between rapid response and over-
shoot in conventional PID control. Qiu Bobo [6] designed a dual-loop controller for cruise
altitude tracking, effectively solving the attack angle tracking problem. Nigatu [7] proposed
an adaptive PID control method based on neural network-fuzzy fusion, which improves
the control accuracy of unmanned aerial vehicles (UAVs) under complex dynamics.
(2) Fuzzy control [8–14] is a methodology based on fuzzy logic reasoning, which
possesses strong nonlinear processing capabilities and is often applied in systems with com-
plex structures or significant uncertainties. Aurelia [15] designed a fuzzy gain-scheduling
controller, which adjusts the position and height control gains of quadrotors in real time
via fuzzy rules and maintains stability under variable operating conditions. Jin Rui [16]
proposed a variable universe fuzzy PID control algorithm and applied it to the control of a
large dual-rotor UAV.
(3) Deep learning-based intelligent algorithms [17–25] exhibit low dependency on
system models, thereby offering strong generality and broad applicability to control prob-
lems across different types of systems. Serhat [26] proposed a PD parameter prediction
method based on reinforcement learning, which realizes gain self-adaptation of quadrotor
UAVs in dynamic environments, reduces the dependence on manual parameter tuning,
and improves trajectory tracking accuracy. Rysdyk [27] developed a neural network-based
compensation mechanism to address wind disturbance rejection in UAVs. Lv [28] et al. pro-
posed an adaptive tracking control algorithm for hypersonic flight vehicles that integrates
model-based control with the DQN algorithm, improving tracking performance in complex
environments through dynamic optimization of control parameters. Timmerman [29] et al.
adopted the PPO algorithm to train an adaptive gain scheduling strategy, realizing online
dynamic adjustment of the gains of the quadcopter’s cascaded feedback controller.

critical for error feedback and the smoothness of control signals. The fal function employs
a piecewise nonlinear form: it uses linear amplification for small errors and a power form
for large errors to avoid oscillations that may arise from linear error feedback. The galn
function improves the discontinuity problem of the fal function near the origin, but the
function still has abrupt change points. Compared with the fal function, the tanh function
exhibits lower gain when the error is large, thereby enhancing the stability of the system;
whereas it has a higher gain than the other two functions when the error is small, which
helps to improve the control accuracy and response speed. Meanwhile, the tanh function
is continuous and smooth over the entire interval, without the abrupt change problem
existing in the other two functions. This smoothness demonstrates the superiority of the
proposed nonlinear function in control performance, helping to suppress oscillations in the
small-error region and further enhancing system stability and response accuracy.

4. Design of Intelligent Active Disturbance Rejection Controller
(TD3-ADRC)
Although the tanh function mitigates certain discontinuity issues inherent in the
traditional fal function, its overall performance remains highly dependent on the precise
tuning of controller parameters. In conventional controller design, parameter selection
and optimization often rely heavily on manual experience and extensive trial-and-error,
which not only increases the complexity and workload of the debugging process but also
hinders the controller’s adaptability to different system models or operating conditions.
This limitation becomes particularly pronounced in highly uncertain or strongly nonlinear
systems, where fixed parameters can significantly restrict control performance.
To address this challenge, this paper introduces the Twin Delayed Deep Determinis-
tic Policy Gradient (TD3) deep reinforcement learning algorithm to optimize the ADRC
framework. By integrating deep learning techniques, the proposed approach enables the
ADRC to self-optimize without relying on complex manual tuning. This method not only
reduces the cost of human intervention but also enhances the system’s responsiveness
and accuracy under various disturbances. Consequently, it not only improves the per-
formance of traditional ADRC but also achieves a more efficient and intelligent control
optimization process.
4.1. TD3 Deep Reinforcement Learning Algorithm
TD3 is a deep reinforcement learning algorithm proposed by McGill University [36] as
an improvement over the Deep Deterministic Policy Gradient (DDPG) algorithm. Designed
primarily for continuous control problems, TD3 addresses the overestimation bias preva-
lent in DDPG by introducing a twin Q-network architecture and a delayed policy update
mechanism. These enhancements significantly improve learning stability and policy per-
formance. Compared with mainstream reinforcement learning algorithms such as DDPG,
PPO, and SAC, its core advantages are lower Q-value overestimation bias, more stable

4.2. Design of Intelligent Active Disturbance Rejection Controller
In the TD3 algorithm, two primary networks are employed: the Actor Network and
the Critic Network. The Actor Network outputs continuous control actions based on the
state feedback received from the environment. Subsequently, the Critic Network evaluates
the value of these actions in conjunction with the current state, providing essential feedback
for policy optimization.
During the continuous interaction between the agent and the environment, the Critic
Network processes the estimated rewards that it generates and the actual rewards obtained
from the environment. It computes the reward error and uses this to adjust its weights
and biases, thereby improving the accuracy of its value estimates. Meanwhile, the Actor
Network updates its parameters using the policy gradient method, orienting its updates
toward directions that maximize action value. Through iterative learning and optimization
of both neural networks, the policy gradually converges to its optimal form.

7. Conclusions
The numerical simulation results demonstrate that the parameterized tanh function
exhibits more stable performance, higher robustness, and greater reliability compared
to the traditional nonlinear fal function within the active disturbance rejection control
(ADRC) framework. It effectively mitigates the control discontinuity issues associated with
the conventional fal function. After introducing random disturbances, the output curves
remain smooth without significant high-frequency oscillations, verifying the considerable
effectiveness of the proposed method in noise suppression.
Furthermore, the proposed TD3-ADRC method addresses attitude tracking for the
quadrotor UAV under wind and external environmental disturbances. By utilizing the
TD3 algorithm to train the parameters of the control law and designing an appropriate
reward function, an intelligent disturbance rejection controller is developed. Simulation
analysis indicates that, based on a comparison of the Integral of Absolute Error (IAE) and
Root Mean Square Error (RMSE) for attitude tracking, the proposed TD3-ADRC achieves a
37.28% performance improvement over traditional ADRC.
Finally, experiments were conducted on a three-degrees-of-freedom (3-DOF) bench test
based on the PX4 flight controller. Analysis of the experimental data from the three methods
(PID, LADRC, TD3-ADRC) and comparison of their RMSE values show that TD3-ADRC
possesses significant advantages when dealing with complex attitude control tasks in
challenging environments.
