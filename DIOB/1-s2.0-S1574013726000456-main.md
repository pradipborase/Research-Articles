A B S T R A C T
Machine Learning (ML) has broadened the operational capability of Unmanned Aerial Vehicles (UAVs) across 
various fields. But these aerial services still face limitations due to factors like changing environments, on­
board computing limits, and secure connections. The current literature lacks a comprehensive analysis of the 
advancements in the domain and ongoing challenges that need to be addressed. To fill this gap, we conducted a 
mixed-method review of 9148 associated articles that focus on the usage of ML in UAV systems from the Scopus 
database. Our study analyzed the country collaboration and document co-citation networks to understand the 
overall growth trajectory and associated themes. These findings helped in categorizing UAV research into 5 op­
erational areas. These include navigation and control, communication networks, task scheduling and resource 
management, safe and secure operations, and Drone-As-A-Service (DaaS). The findings also point to the existing 
barriers that hinder the smooth operations of these systems. The results also identify emerging opportunities like 
multi-UAV systems, energy-efficient models, contextually aware orchestration, and human-in-the-loop services. 
These insights will serve as a one-point reference for researchers and industry experts to advance ML-powered 
UAV services.
1 . Introduction
In the last decade, Unmanned Aerial Vehicles (UAVs) have been 
globally adopted as a mobile service platform providing vision and 
visibility. Initially, this aerial entity was utilized particularly for mil­
itary and surveillance purposes. However, their role has expanded 
beyond mapping and space interaction due to their adaptable, flexible, 
and remotely maneuverable design. As of now, they perform seamless 
operations across aerial photography, last-mile delivery, agriculture, in­
frastructure inspection, etc. As a result, UAVs have been recognized 
as tools that promote knowledge creation, strategic cooperation, and 
economic viability [1]. These UAVs can be categorized according to 
perspectives and contexts based on their size, weight, flight endurance, 
wing loading, achievable altitude, engine type, and utility. This helps in 
contextualizing their usage in specific tasks, environmental conditions, 
and operational constraints that provide tailored services [2]. Learning-
based technologies such as machine learning (ML) have been widely 
integrated across diverse sectors, utilizing their applicability across data 
analysis, automatic decision-making, and robust situational awareness. 
As such, UAV operations have also been optimized using ML models to 
interpret visual data, optimize flight paths, and provide remote percep­
tion and sensing more efficiently. Notably, the availability of trainable 
data and precise miniaturized sensors has played a key role in boosting 
their service utility [3]. But these self-governing entities require care­
ful attention for an effective and dependable functioning. Problems like 
limited onboard power and computation hinder their practical viabil­
ity in solutions requiring mission-specific and real-time decision-making 
efficacy [4–8]. To provide dependable UAV automation, current re­
search needs to actively explore the integration of ML-based adaptive 
algorithms [9]. The current aerial systems need to deal with various 
control-loss problems that arise due to GPS signal loss, dynamic ob­
stacles, and unpredictable conditions in surrounding environments. The 
traditionally adopted control solutions lead to lower precision and sta­
bility under these constraints. This happens due to their dependence 
on fixed flight paths and associated static rules [8,10]. Current ML ap­
proaches like proximal policy optimization (PPO) and deep-Q-networks 
(DQN) assist in navigating these changing flight paths. These methods 
use feedback from the environment to continuously learn control needs. 
They provide these systems with the capability to have optimal flight 
patterns, path selection, and stabilized control during obstacle avoid­
ance in dynamic environments [11–13]. Convolutional Neural Networks 
(CNNs) are also helping in enhancing UAV’s innate perception capabili­
ties through object detection, hazard recognition, and precision landing

UAV swarms while ensuring data privacy and low bandwidth usage

identification, and adaptive response mechanisms. Approaches such 
as Random Forest (RF), Support Vector Machines (SVM), and autoen­

failures, sensor malfunctions, or GPS spoofing, etc.). These proactive 
data-sensitive learners help in detecting real-time unauthorized access 
attempts and suspicious network activity during critical missions [7,24]. 
Additionally, optimization algorithms are crucially effective in boost­
ing the performance of these mission-aligned solutions by balancing 
workloads and energy-efficient task assignment [5]. Overall, research 
efforts within energy-efficient designs, adaptive control strategies, and 
swarm intelligence will be crucial for driving research advancements in 
ML-driven UAV systems.

3 . Research question
To address the identified research gaps in the aerial-automation do­
main, this study analyzed the literature corpus as per the following 
research questions.
1) What are the temporal dynamics driving the evolution of ML-
assisted UAV systems?
2) How do the knowledge patterns across ML-assisted UAVs struc­
ture international collaboration and research’s global dissemina­
tion?
3) What intellectual concerns and limitations need to be addressed 
within the current knowledge framework?
4) What are the key future research directives and knowledge 
implications that will drive further research efforts?
5) What are the research implications and thrust themes shaping 
advancements in the ML-assisted UAV’s realm?
4 . Motivation for the study
The growth of ML-assisted UAV systems has expanded interdisci­
plinary literature, including control theory, wireless communications, 
distributed and edge computing, artificial intelligence, and service-
oriented architectures. Although this growth reflects the maturation of 
intelligent drone technologies, it is also contributing to the fragmen­
tation of the UAV research landscape. Many technical advances are 
investigated in isolation, rather than in conjunction with an overall 
aerial automation ecosystem. As stated earlier, most of the literature 
has focused on discrete operational aspects of UAV systems, including 
trajectory planning, obstacle avoidance, swarm coordination, and UAV 
communication. However, they provide limited information about how 
ML techniques influence UAV operation across these different domains. 
This leads to an incomplete view of the overall organizational struc­
ture of the field, both temporally and globally. Thus, the researcher’s 
and practitioner’s ability to define the research paths, emergent the­
matic relationships, and system-level challenges of the research field 
has been restricted. In addition, the architectural advancements made 
in learning models present new challenges for scalability, interoperabil­
ity, and real-time deployment. To resolve these problems, a methodical 
and data-driven synthesis is required that identifies established research 
themes from emerging themes and reveals the identified gaps in re­
search. Currently, the inefficiency of research studies to quantify the 
evolution of knowledge and objectify the collaboration dynamics pre­
vents objective assessment of the research progress. Therefore, this study 
addresses the aforementioned goals by providing a system-wide, holistic 
view of ML-driven UAV development and defining the limitations of the 
intellectual base and potential thematic thrusts of future UAV research.
5 . Major contributions of the study
The present study offers several key contributions that advance the 
understanding of ML-assisted UAV research:
1) The study investigated large-scale and high-quality ML-UAV lit­
erature (1994-2024), providing comprehensive coverage.
2) The advancements in ML-based UAV research are explored using 
a mixed-method approach, combining scientometric and content 
analysis to highlight key innovation drivers and leading research 
hubs worldwide.
3) System-level science mapping, including country collaboration 
and document co-citation analysis, identifies leading research 
hubs, knowledge dissemination patterns, and research themes, 
providing a coherent structural framework.
4) Evidence-based identification of research gaps, challenges, and 
future directions provides actionable insights for both researchers 
and practitioners.
6 . Research methodology
6.1 . Analysis method
This study utilized the mixed-method approach by integrating scien­
tometric and qualitative content analysis to analyze the advancements 
within ML-based UAV research. Initially, research data were analyzed 
using the scientometric method to map the intellectual, social, and con­
ceptual structure of the literature. As such, various science mapping 
techniques, such as publication trends, country collaboration networks, 
and document co-citation analysis, were performed to quantify the 
knowledge progression and research structure. The results and patterns 
observed First, data were analyzed using a scientometric approach to 
map the intellectual, social, and conceptual structure of the literature. 
Research context has been explored using publication trends, coun­
try collaboration networks, and document co-citation analysis. These 
quantitative results objectively identify influential entities, dominant re­
search clusters, and emerging research fronts. Subsequently, qualitative 
content analysis is systematically applied to interpret and contextualize 
the patterns revealed by the scientometric findings. The clusters, burst 
references, and collaboration structures identified through scientometric 
mapping directly inform the thematic synthesis, enabling in-depth exam­
ination of technological advancements, methodological limitations, and 
future research directions. Table 2 explicitly illustrates how the outputs 
of the scientometric analysis are linked to and enriched by qualita­
tive content analysis, ensuring methodological coherence and analytical 
rigor.
6.1.1 . Data source and retrieval
We selected Scopus for extracting the current literature out of the 
various available bibliographic databases. Scopus provides extensive 
coverage of literature and frequently updates the citation metadata. 
This platform ensures that all of the listed articles are peer-reviewed 
and thus are a valid references for further studies and interpretations. 
We only considered a single database so that we could prevent any 
ambiguity during the integration of studies across multiple databases.

7.2 . Research development
The present section explores the literature across three growth phases 
that examine key trends in research dissemination and increasing diver­
sity. These influential studies encapsulate seminal breakthroughs and 
foundational theories that have shaped future research directions [35]. 
As such, these trends are summarized as follows:
7.2.1 . Initial phase (1994–2007)
During the early years from 1994–2007, the research shifted from 
classical algorithms to neural networks for nonlinear control and au­
tonomous decision-making. Neural networks made it possible to ap­
proximate nonlinear dynamics, optimize motion paths, and perform 
better than traditional PID controllers in noisy and uncertain situations 
[36]. Aerodynamic modeling improved with neural network-assisted 
surface-mounted sensors. These sensors made it possible to estimate 
flight parameters like airspeed, angle of attack, and sideslip angle in real 
time. This led to improvements in handling the stabilization and con­
trol of UAVs [37]. Controllers based on Multi-Layer Perceptrons (MLPs), 
Radial Basis Functions (RBFs), and Self-Organizing Maps (SOMs) showed 
flexibility and real-time learning abilities. They provided flight stabil­
ity in changing environments citesutton1997neural, cho2006modeling, 
Nikolos2007. RL-based learning algorithms also helped in managing 
navigation by allowing UAVs to learn the best flight paths through on­
going interactions with their environment. This cut down the reliance 
on fixed waypoints. At the same time, bio-inspired machine learning 
methods developed alongside natural flying techniques helped stabi­
lize robotic systems using visual input, coordinate swarms, manage 
flight altitude, and create energy-efficient routes [38]. As the learn­
ing algorithms continued to grow, the emergence of DL like Grossberg 
Neural Networks (GNNs) proved useful during collision avoidance, en­
vironmental perception, and detection of objects in real-time [39]. 
Additionally, approaches like Extreme Learning Machines (ELM) en­
hanced fault detection and terrain modeling [40]. ML-based workflows 
also began using other information input from multi-modal sensors, 
such as LiDAR (Light Detection and Ranging), GPS (Global Positioning 
System), and digital cameras, creating significant improvements in the 
accuracy of locating and maintaining UAV’s (unmanned aerial vehicles) 
positions and stability while in flight. Collectively, these advancements 
depict the foundational phase of ML-assisted UAV research, during 
which learning-based control, perception, and sensing capabilities were 
established. Although the application scope remained relatively narrow, 
this period laid the groundwork for subsequent expansions into large-
scale coordination, communication, and resource-aware UAV systems.
7.2.2 . Maturation phase (2008–2017)
The ML-based research across UAV operations between 2008 and 
2017 focused on swarm networking and formation flight, including 
aerial refueling, advanced significantly. But the learning paradigms still 
faced hurdles such as data redundancy, inconsistent communication, 
and inefficient resource utilization which drained performance gains. 
To address these issues, techniques such as in-network data processing 
were employed to filter low-quality data, while methods like control-
based Echo State Networks were utilized for user mobility prediction 
and content request modeling. These advancements resulted in data-
efficient swarm coordination and data caching [41,42]. Subsequently, 
flight control methods used NNs-based architectures to support au­
tonomous landing, indoor navigation, task execution, and trajectory 
tracking. These controllers addressed nonlinear effects arising from tur­
bulence, blade flapping, friction, modeling uncertainties, and partial 
system failures. Simultaneously, estimation techniques based on learn­
ing, usually in combination with Lyapunov or backstepping control 
frameworks, enabled real-time compensation for unmodeled aerody­
namics. These methods ensured the stability of trajectory tracking. NN-
assisted fractional-order PID controllers improved disturbance rejection 
and trajectory tracking performance in the presence of wind disturbance, 
battery disturbance, and model mismatch. These approaches utilized 
adaptive NNs to counteract compensation errors and external distur­
bances while preserving stability guarantees [43,44]. Research attention 
evolved from single-agent stabilization to cooperative multi-agent con­
trol. Learning-based cooperative frameworks enabled leader–follower 
coordination and formation maintenance [45]. Advanced synchroniza­
tion techniques, including 𝑝th-moment exponential synchronization in 
stochastic delayed NNs with Markovian switching, allowed UAV swarms 
to maintain coordinated behavior despite delays and environmental un­
certainty [46]. Fuzzy Neural Networks (FNNs), especially Type-2 FNNs,

7.2.3 . Growing phase (2018 onward)
From 2018 onward, research on ML-assisted UAV systems has ad­
vanced through intelligent communication, decentralized learning, and 
energy-aware autonomy. These UAVs are adopted as aerial base stations, 
mobile relays, and communication nodes that improve network cover­
age, capacity, and spectral efficiency in remote and high-demand areas 
[62]. The research utilized algorithms like FL and NNs to handle the 
stringent latency and privacy needs. This ensured the community had 
decentralized intelligence at the network edges. It facilitated efficient 
learning while preserving data locality [23,63]. As UAVs continued to 
be integrated within the computing infrastructures deployed at the edge. 
Thus, the researchers thoroughly explored the usage of ML for task 
scheduling, resource allocation, and trajectory planning. For example, 
algorithms like CNNs and liquid state machines have been helpful in 
predicting the content requests. They also allow for automating the 
caching and resource allocation decisions on these devices [64,65]. 
By dynamically adjusting resource usage to changes in network con­
ditions, energy availability, and user demand, RL architectures further 
supported computation offloading [66]. Fault tolerance and perfor­
mance efficiency were major priorities for UAV control frameworks 
during this phase. Using NNs for nonlinear fault estimation and con­
vex optimization for adaptive control updates in multi-UAV systems, 
fixed-time and fault-tolerant control strategies were created [67]. During 
this era, intelligent human interaction (HI) mechanisms also emerged. 
For instance, DL-based hand gesture recognition is one example. This 
technology allowed for intuitive, hands-free UAV navigation by using 
spatiotemporal cues and reinforcement learning-based adaptation [68]. 
Q-learning-enhanced visual servoing methods improved UAV motion 
control. They achieved this through fuzzy-adaptive control, synthetic 
color and contour-based feature extraction, and decoupled servoing 
gains, which tackled long-standing issues in image-based visual servoing 
[69]. Energy management also became a critical focus for research along 
with control and navigation. This factor is very essential for supporting 
long-duration UAV operations and improving communication coverage. 
Researchers explored the mobile charging stations as practical options 
for outdoor UAV recharging. To keep up with the charging schedules of 
these UAVs, they adopted DL-assisted auction methods. This included 
optimal second-price auctions for allocating dynamic charging slots 
[70]. Complementary studies also examined state-of-charge (SOC) esti­
mation with LSTM models [71], energy-efficient navigation strategies 
[6], CNN-based prediction of steering angles and collision probabili­
ties [72], and optimized trajectory planning within energy limits [8]. 
Additionally, ML models facilitated operations in adversarial and safety-
critical settings. By detecting motor temperature anomalies, RL-based 
anomaly detection frameworks enabled safe UAV landings [73]. The 
Q-learning and hill-climbing techniques improved relay performance 
under smart jamming attacks [7]. Furthermore, the ML-assisted multi-
agent optimization improved resource allocation in vehicular and UAV 
relay networks [74,75]. However, using DL more frequently revealed 
new security weaknesses. Adversarial attacks aimed at regression-based 
navigation and control models were found to disrupt UAV flight be­
havior. These challenges required adversarial training and knowledge 
distillation techniques to improve the robustness and resilience of these 
systems [76]. As such, it is evident that recent research has increasingly 
focused on adopting integrated system-level frameworks that jointly 
consider energy-efficient navigation, adaptive task scheduling, multi-
UAV deployment, and intelligent target recognition. These approaches 
aim to balance communication coverage, energy use, connectivity, and 
fairness across different operating environments. This demonstrates a 
move toward complete and scalable UAV system design [9,77,78].

[117]. The emergence of the Covid-19 pandemic in the last decade has 
focused the attention of researchers and industry on this sector. It has 
served as a catalyst for exploring the operational utility of UAVs in 
remote logistics operations, monitoring applications, and autonomous 
systems deployments [118].

8.3 . RQ3: what intellectual concerns and limitations need to be addressed 
within the current knowledge framework?
UAVs have to face considerable operational constraints due to 
their computational and energy limitations. This has led to a trade-
off in performance across ML architectures. The DL approaches re­
quire higher computational capabilities, which limit the application 
efficiency of these entities. Furthermore, the UAV swarms are hin­
dered by the overhead associated with the decentralized learning, 
communication latency, and dynamic task allocation [124]. Non-ideal 
components like amplifiers, phase noise, and in-quadrature-phase im­
balance lead to residual hardware impairments in these vehicles. This 
degrades the communication system making it challenging for mod­
els to accurately optimize the system functionality. Thus, our learning 
models need to robustly handle these imperfections while adapting 
to the changing conditions in real-time. This training regime is still 
hindered by the requirement of high-quality labeled data for ML 
models. They should be adapted to capture this diversified data un­
der varying conditions such as interference, fading, and hardware
impairments.
The challenge is to ensure that enough representative data is col­
lected, labeled accurately, and used to train ML models capable of 
generalizing well in real-world situations [125]. One of the main issues 
in addressing state-of-the-art challenges is the lack of realistic, high-
fidelity simulation environments that accurately replicate real-world 
conditions. Although the current ML-based approaches are promising, 
they often struggle with generalization, leading to performance degra­
dation when applied to unseen scenarios. Notably, UAV operational 
domains, including navigation and control, communication and net­
working, task scheduling and resource allocation, secure operations, and 
DaaS, do not evolve in isolation within practical systems. Decisions re­
lated to navigation and control directly influence communication link 
stability and latency, which subsequently constrain task scheduling and 
resource allocation under energy and bandwidth limitations. Scheduling 
policies on their part impact the computational offloading, missed tim­
ing, and service continuity, especially in multi-UAV and edge-enabled 
contexts. The basis of all these layers is to ensure that control signals, 
data exchange, and cooperative behavior are not faulty and are ad­
versarially resistant to interference by malicious actors through secure 
UAV operations. On a more abstract level, DaaS encloses these interde­
pendencies with integrated services where the capabilities of UAV are 
provided as a unified service that demands coordinated responses be­
tween control, networking, scheduling, and security interfaces to satisfy 
the quality-of-service specifications. Table 4 provides a summary of the 
functional interactions between the five categories and the necessity of 
system-level ML frameworks in comparison to domain-specific solutions.
8.3.1 . Navigation & control
Trajectory planning and optimization. 
UAV trajectory design must 
jointly balance kinematic limits (speed, endurance, maneuverability) 
with mission goals such as logistics routing, traffic surveillance, or emer­
gency response. Despite progress, achieving real-time, energy-efficient, 
and collision-free navigation remains difficult under stochastic, par­
tially observable conditions. Path discretization accelerates convergence 
but reduces spatial precision, while joint mobility–power optimization 
remains NP-hard and is often confined to 2D simplifications [126]. 
Hybrid frameworks combining solar or wireless power transfer (WPT), 
RIS-assisted communication, and edge offloading improve endurance 
and connectivity but impose heavy coupling among trajectory, energy, 
and computation variables. QoE-aware, task-coupled learning schemes 
further integrate trajectory with scheduling and offloading decisions, 
enabling delay- and energy-efficient operation under dynamic user mo­
bility [82,97]. Multi-agent and hierarchical DRL (e.g., MADDPG, HDRL) 
have helped in providing coordination and scalability, but they still 
suffer from reward sparsity, instability, and sensitivity to dynamic chan­
nel states. Decentralized swarm learning has demonstrated over 90% 
success and < 1% collision rates in controlled scenarios, yet its per­
formance degrades under uncertain environments and limited onboard 
computation. Other challenges, like reward sparsity, coarse action dis­
cretization, and high-dimensional policy spaces, restrict the scalability 
in dense aerial corridors. In these scenarios, the collision probability can 
increase up to 30% without adaptive avoidance [19]. Aside from these 
issues, factors like initialization bias, manual hyperparameter tuning, 
and restricted caching capacity also lead to degradation [127]. Thus, it

Collision avoidance. 
For real-time aerial navigation, the research is 
needed to consider the ever-changing requirements. The dynamic be­
havior of vehicles, pedestrians, or parallel UAVs makes the feature-based 
methods inefficient due to their reliance on predefined patterns. In ad­
dition, maintaining system constraints like input limits and velocity 
restrictions remains a significant challenge. The present solutions of­
ten approximate the actuator constraints or use compensators. But the 
improper consideration of real-world actuator non-linearity degrades 
optimal performance. The obstacle avoidance algorithms that consider 
the flowing properties also struggle with real-time computation within 
dynamic scenarios. The limited capabilities of sensing which are likely 
raised due to narrow FOV or fusion drifts diminish the viability of cur­
rent solutions. Advanced stereo vision systems like S-MSCKF are also 
able to achieve only 0.80–0.83 matching accuracy under non-constant 
conditions. This is mainly due to the errors in residual pose and depth 
estimation [129]. These issues are further exacerbated by lineariza­
tion assumptions, Gaussian models, and drifts during fast maneuvers. 
Techniques like trajectory modification and start-time batching are in­
efficient in UAV swarms. They cause convoluted flight paths, endless 
loops, and excessive launch delays, which are incurred due to the higher 
cost of computation[130]. The hierarchical dependence of swarms on 
leader nodes and communication delays is another area of concern. 
This underscores the urgency of focusing on distributed autonomous 
architectures and multi-leader resilience in these swarms.
Energy efficiency and power management. 
In UAVs, energy constraints 
play a decisive role in optimizing the trajectory, execution, and deliv­
ery of the missions, especially under a strict set of size, weight, and 
power (SWaP) constraints [131]. Energy usage depends on the environ­
ment, payloads, flight dynamics, and the length of the mission, where 
propulsion and continuous communication particularly utilize the ma­
jority of the energy power. Traditional controls, such as Sliding Mode 
Control, provide robustness, but they have unstable endurance due 
to chattering. ML-based solutions, including adaptive switching laws, 
NN approximators, and RL frameworks such as DDPG, MADDPG, their 
multi-agent versions, and multi-armed bandits, offer better solutions to 
energy-aware trajectory planning, power allocation, and task schedul­
ing. Generally, decentralized RL helps improve energy efficiency by 
7–46% over traditional RL methods [132]. However, challenges remain 
in the decentralized multi-agent learning, including communication 
overhead, interference, convergence with limited onboard computa­
tional resources, and redundant experience training, all of which may 
mitigate possible energy efficiency improvements. Multi-band, UAV-to-
UAV communication, and trajectory awareness provide more trade-offs. 
Trajectory-aware, multi-band UAV-to-UAV communication also intro­
duces trade-offs. While sub-6 𝐺𝐻𝑧 bands have better energy efficiency 
(𝜂𝑒≈0.15–0.07 bits/Joule) because of lower path loss, mmWave bands 
(28–60 𝐺𝐻𝑧) have superior spectral efficiency at the cost of signifi­
cantly lower energy efficiency (10−3–10−4 bits/Joule). Mobility, altitude 
variations, and multipath fading further complicate real-time power 
adaptation, necessitating predictive ML-based approaches that are close 
to real-world energy consumption [133]. Solar-, RF-, mechanical-, and 
thermal-based energy harvesting can help lessen endurance limits, 
although combining these sources with ML-based scheduling is chal­
lenging in densely populated and multi-agent networks. The use of 
energy-aware task allocation is essential to prevent the UAVs from ex­
ceeding the established energy budgets and at the same time align the 
mission target, the network performance, and the reliability of delivery.
Real-time adaptation in dynamic environments. 
The feature-sparse re­
gions (e.g., deserts, open water, forests) make it challenging for swarm 
networks to maintain global localization and coordinated control. They 
quickly need to adapt to the UAV-SMD positioning, adversarial targets, 
jitter, and disruption caused by surrounding environments like wind or 
airspace constraints. The nonlinear multi-agent systems are required 
to adjust to the actuator saturation and full-state constraints during 
real-world uncertainties for formation tracking control. The existing 
adaptive strategies like fuzzy logic and model-based controllers have 
to be tuned manually and are often disrupted by dynamic variations 
[134]. These existing methods are helpful in addressing the fixed for­
mation, but they need to dynamically adapt formation reconfiguration 
for multi-target tracking, obstacle avoidance, and swarm operations. 
They should also jointly prioritize the multi-objective optimization of 
key factors like energy efficiency, stability, and response time. There is 
a need for fault-tolerant sensor fusion, as methods considering feature-
sparse localization can fail in ingenuity crash[135]. The robustness of 
approaches like DRL can fail with wind disturbances. Thus, a deeper 
exploration of multi-agent policies and 3D jitter-aware trajectory opti­
mization needs to be considered. The computational demands invoke the 
need for inference and control loops in swarms for real-time adaptation 
[136].
Multi-entity coordination. 
The coordination between different UAVs 
under partial observability, dynamic topology, and high-dimensional 
state spaces remains a challenge for ML-driven control. Learning policies 
suffer from sparse rewards, poor generalization, and unstable conver­
gence as swarm size or environmental complexity increases, limiting 
scalability and cooperation [90]. Traditional heuristic and graph-based 
solvers lack adaptability, while DRL methods face training collapse and 
overfitting under real-world uncertainty. The need for policy coordi­
nation among numerous UAVs in a distributed multi-agent system is 
a major concern. Centralized critic methods and unique policy net­
works for each agent are not practically feasible due to the exponential 
growth of joint state action. The graph theory-based path planning 
or RL techniques often fail to handle real-time variations and energy 
constraints. The lack of proper spatial memory in UAV swarms pre­
vents them from storing visited information. In parallel, the digital 
twin-based frameworks are effectively limiting the sim-to-real gap, but 
they still face scaling limitations across heterogeneous UAVs [101,102]. 
Event-triggered control can enhance efficiency but poses stability issues.
Learning-based control and decision-making. 
The existing ML-based con­
trol systems are often required to have an initial admissible control 
policy. But the uncertain conditions associated with the UAV pose ma­
jor limitations. The traditional NNs typically utilize general activation 
functions like Sigmoid, Tanh, and ReLU which lead to low accuracy 
in trajectory prediction. These models suffer from poor generalization 
in noisy environments. The advanced versions of these learners, such 
as DNN or DDPG models, are unable to adapt to changing UAVs and 
users. They need to be retrained with a change in the network size. 
The random initialization of DNN actors also results in invalid actions 
and training failures. In parallel, RL has been explored as a promising 
alternative. But its dependence on the persistency of excitation (PE) con­
dition poses a challenge [137]. It requires the introduction of probing 
noise or larger training datasets which hinder real-time deployment. The 
large state and action spaces in traditional RL make it difficult to effi­
ciently find optimal policies. In the advanced RL techniques like DDPG 
or PPO, they struggle with sparse rewards and overestimation errors. 
The inherent exploration–exploitation trade-off in these algorithms leads 
to suboptimal convergence [97,137]. The architectural advancements 
such as dueling or double Q-networks, though, improve the stability 
but are computationally expensive for onboard execution [126]. The 
absence of well-structured data makes it difficult to accurately learn 
Q-values. This limits the capability of Deep Q-learning and similar ap­
proaches in finding the optimal path. The current learning paradigms

Localization and precision landing challenges.

8.3.2 . Communication & networking challenges
Signal enhancement and RIS integration. 
The combination of UAVs with 
RIS helps in improving signal propagation characteristics, network ca­
pacity, and overall energy efficiency. This gain is governed by 3D 
beamforming and adaptive LoS optimization within these aerial net­
works [97,139]. However, this integration introduces a new set of 
challenges, where channel tracking, phase-shift optimization, and real-
time adaptation are major concerns. The stringent latency and stability 
requirements of these systems call for more robust edge and cloud 
offloading mechanisms. This situation is compounded when Doppler 
shifts, interference patterns, and imperfect CSI degrade the system’s 
reliability. The joint optimization of trajectory, phase shifts, and user 
associations is another non-convex, high-dimensional problem requiring 
attention. DL frameworks like DDPG and hierarchical DRL subtly allow 
joint beamforming and trajectory control but still falter. These methods 
tend to suffer from slow convergence, sensitivity to changing channel 
estimations, and associated computational overhead with their training 
itself. Notably, models like LSTM and GRU offer gains in CSI estimation, 
however, error accumulation over time and generalization to dynamic 
topologies inhibit their performance. Likewise, FL ensures data privacy 
over reduced communication load, but it faces challenges due to diver­
gence and heterogeneous data imbalance within distinct UAVs [140]. 
Thus, lightweight, adaptive learning schemes capable of maintaining re­
liable RIS–UAV connectivity under real-time, high-mobility conditions 
are required.
UAV deployment and mobile edge computing. 
Deploying UAVs as mo­
bile edge servers enhances dynamic QoS, computation offloading, and 
LoS connectivity, forming drone cells in ultra-dense 5G/6G networks. 
However, practical deployment faces persistent challenges from inter­
ference, limited energy, trajectory-resource coupling, and regulatory 
constraints. The current solutions are confined to simplified single-user 
or static setups that overlook the multi-agent, decentralized coordina­
tion required for real-time service continuity. To address these issues, 
multi-agent DRL frameworks (e.g., MADDPG, MATD3) jointly optimize 
3D trajectories, offloading, and computational resource allocation, yet 
suffer from slow convergence, reward sparsity, and scalability bottle­
necks in heterogeneous UAV networks [141]. Decentralized schemes, 
such as Reward-Based Multi-Agent Learning (RMAL), mitigate commu­
nication overhead but risk policy divergence under partial observability. 
The current game-theoretic approaches, like stackelberg or auction-
based models, are useful in handling resolving multi-provider resource 
conflicts. But the network size leads to an exponential increase in 
computation requirements [142,143].
Relay selection and cooperative control. 
The dynamic topologies and 
fluctuating channel states make the task of relay selection in cooper­
ative UAV networks. The current selection mechanisms have concerns 
related to transparency, trust, and suboptimal decision-making. which 
lead to inefficient communication between users and UAVs. The tech­
niques based on auction mechanisms and game-theoretic models are 
inefficient in adapting to dynamic network conditions, mobility in vehi­
cles, and UAV preferences. Although learning approaches like DQN are 
used which can negate the need for Q-table storage during cooperative 
communications. But the DNN training poses computational overhead 
along with slow convergence in real-time scenarios. The centralized 
model continues to struggle during scaled implementations. Though 
parallel decentralized learners can provide robustness to network non-
stationarity, they still have to face coordination instability and reward 
interference across agents [144]. The NNs, like GNNs, can capture tem­
poral relationships to reduce delays, but they need to be frequently 
retrained to handle topology shifts [145]. The privacy-centered ap­
proaches like Federated DQN can provide near-centralized accuracy. But 
the heterogeneity between UAV resources, partial observability, and ad­
versarial CSI variations limit its performance. The IRS and MEC-assisted 
networks often require joint optimization of trajectory, user power, and 
IRS phases. This creates non-convex, high-dimensional problems, urging 
the exploration of lightweight and adaptive frameworks [96].
Dynamic positioning and handover management. 
The real-time manage­
ment of real-time positioning and handover (HO) operations in dense 
aerial-terrestrial networks is a persistent issue. This arises due to the 
associated high mobility, interference, and unpredictable densities of 
users. The current solutions based on clustering approaches like K-means 
suffer from topology variations. Supervised models (MLP, SVM, RF) of­
fer alternatives for detecting HO failures and fine-tuning parameters 
like hysteresis margins and time-to-trigger thresholds. However, their 
performance varies proportionally with the availability of labeled data 
and manually configured static threshold values. Similarly, unsuper­
vised learning effectively learns mobility and load patterns but tends to 
struggle during varying topology changes, user speeds, and interference 
conditions. DL approaches like DQ, DDQN, PPO, AC-DRL, etc. can adapt 
to mobility patterns and time-varying conditions improving spectral ef­
ficiency and reducing overall HO management. But still, these solutions 
falter during non-stationary swarms and non-continuous connectivity. 
The ping-pong HO, link failures, and excessive signaling overhead across 
the 3D mobile landscape are the major inhibitors [146]. Multi-Agent 
Twin Delayed DDPG (MATD3) has shown promise in addressing the 
aforementioned limitations through an attention mechanism. It effi­
ciently manages state–action spaces whose dimensions shift as UAV and 
vehicle densities change dynamically. This leads to scalable, stable, and 
efficient decision-making capabilities, especially in Internet of Vehicles 
(IoV) applications. However, achieving real-time adaptability, robust 
interference management, multi-UAV coordination, and generalization 
across heterogeneous mobility patterns is still a significant challenge 
[131].

inference mitigation, trajectory control, and power allocation need to be 
jointly addressed for efficient resource and power management. They 
have a major influence on determining the overall coverage, latency, 
and energy efficiency of the solutions. Traditional DRL models, such as

need to consider the heterogeneous demands of users and varying chan­

routing delays, interference, cumulative queuing, and high-dimensional 
non-convex phase optimization must be actively considered during these 
communications. The joint optimization of trajectory, power, and RIS

8.3.3 . Task scheduling & resource allocation
Energy allocation and trajectory planning. 
Limited onboard energy stor­
age and caching capacity do not allow UAVs to sustain content coverage 
and computational operations during prolonged mission periods. This 
leads to a strong necessity for real-time and joint optimization of 
non-independent variables, such as the trajectory planning, energy dis­
tributions, caching policies, and computational resource distributions, 
which are highly nonconvex, nonlinear optimization problems that 
cannot be addressed using the conventional heuristic or analytical tech­
niques. ML approaches, particularly DRL and MARL, provide adaptive 
control over the continuous decision variables and allow coordinated 
decision-making among UAVs. This leads to concurrent optimization of 
energy efficiency, latency, task offloading, and caching based on dy­
namically changing network conditions. However, significant problems 
persist, especially the scheduling of heterogeneous tasks, computation-
communication trade-offs, and the support of user mobility, interference 
behavior, and the changing energy supply. Cooperative schedules and 
resource sharing among several UAVs with simultaneous differentiated 
content coverage and quality-of-service (QoS) add another layer of com­
plexity. Furthermore, the scalability and convergence of DRL/MARL 
systems in high-density networked settings under real-time operational 
conditions are still unsolved research questions. These issues point to­
ward a need for an integrated, end-to-end, robust ML-based system 
that optimizes energy consumption, trajectory design, and scheduling 
decisions [127].
Caching strategies and device-to-device communication. 
Limited onboard 
and user-device storage limits caching in aerial-terrestrial networks. It 
makes it difficult to satisfy dynamic content demands in high-traffic 
areas. The caching efficiency is further diminished by uneven spatial 
content distribution and poor inter-UAV coordination. This makes the 
joint caching, trajectory planning, and spectrum allocation optimization 
a challenging multi-objective task. The MARL frameworks allow UAVs 
to adaptively coordinate caching, offloading, and trajectories across 
heterogeneous environments. But it still has to manage user mobil­
ity from pedestrians to fast cars, which introduces frequent offloading 
failures and link interruptions. This deteriorates QoS and resource uti­
lization. The ML-based predictive and hybrid offloading strategies are 
helpful in addressing these issues. They allow for proactive adjust­
ment of UAV positions and resource allocations for efficient content 
delivery. But the current research still needs to overcome challenges 
in order to achieve real-time adaptation with various mobility pat­
terns and coordinate caching and offloading across multi-UAV systems
[13,149].
Multi-domain resource management. 
The traditional communication-
centric frameworks usually ignore dependencies within flight dynamics, 
energy limits, and cooperative formation control. This underscores the 
need for joint optimization of trajectory, communication, and computa­
tion requirements for multi-domain resource management. The parallel 
model compression methods like pruning and quantization can lower 
computational overhead on edge devices. But it is still challenging 
to maintain a balance between latency, accuracy, and energy effi­
ciency within real-time constraints. Current research should consider 
dynamic network conditions, heterogeneous devices, and energy-aware 
task scheduling during diverse operational domains [141].
8.3.4 . Reliable & secure UAV operations
Sensor vulnerabilities and attack detection. 
UAV sensor systems are vul­
nerable to a wide range of threats, such as GPS spoofing, tampering 
with inertial sensors (including gyroscopes and accelerometers), barom­
eter manipulation, and coordinated attacks targeting multiple sensors 
simultaneously. The massive amounts of high-dimensional sensor data 
produced during flight exacerbate these vulnerabilities by placing a 
heavy computational burden on real-time monitoring systems. Due to 
issues with feature extraction, robustness against adversarial behav­
ior, and practical deployment constraints, traditional detection and 
classification models frequently fail to maintain accuracy under such cir­
cumstances. The intrinsically dynamic operating environment of UAVs, 
where sensor attacks may happen suddenly and without predictable pat­
terns, is a major challenge in real-time attack detection. It is still a 
significant research challenge to develop classification strategies that 
can quickly detect such attacks while preventing extra latency that 
might compromise flight stability. In this case, extracting pertinent data 
from heterogeneous sensor streams, such as gyroscopes, accelerometers, 
barometers, GPS signals, and control-related parameters, requires ef­
ficient dimensionality reduction and feature selection. Along with the 
increasing computational overhead, the excessive sensor dimensionality 
can hinder the detection performance, which reduces the overall efficacy 
of real-time security measures.

related features are all necessary to improve model performance. The 
class imbalances and small datasets still cause the degradation of the 
model’s accuracies [150]. The data collection and attack simulation are 
crucial for the training and validation of the models. This is further un­

Communication vulnerabilities and privacy concerns.

privacy of data being transmitted between UAVs. In order to protect

networks that are decentralized. The lack of well-defined protocols to 
initiate the swarm will create serious problems regarding swarm coor­
dination, task assignment efficiency, and swarm resilience when new 
UAVs are introduced.
8.3.5 . DaaS
Scalability, security, and economic challenges in DaaS. 
Multi-tenant or­
chestration demands in UAVs require dynamic allocation of flight 
time, spectrum, and computing resources between tenants for the real-
time, latency-sensitive QoS requirements of transportation and moni­
toring apps, thereby limiting the scalability of data services (DaaS). 
The absence of standardized APIs, unified data schemas, and inter­
operability protocols across UAV platforms limits the cross-provider 
coordination and smooth integration. The increasing use of multi-
tenant shared DaaS infrastructures increases the vulnerability to se­
curity and privacy, specifically model poisoning, adversarial interfer­
ence, and data leakage that affect mission-critical operations. While 
lightweight blockchain technologies and FL technologies provide an 
increase in auditability and privacy, their requirement for communi­
cation and synchronization overhead limits their ability to perform 
in real time. Localized decision-making for time-critical missions, 
such as inspection, surveillance, logistics, and emergency response, 
remains one of the primary challenges of intermittent connectivity; 
even minor delays in making inferences may compromise task suc­
cess. Additionally, developing sustainable DaaS requires the creation 
of new economic frameworks to provide incentives to tenants, assess 
cost-per-inference, and attribute liability so all tenants in complex data-
intensive UAV environments receive an equal share of resources and
services.
Object detection and tracking. 
The accurate object detection and track­
ing from UAV inputs are constrained by the surrounding variability 
and geometric distortions from oblique viewing angles. In parallel, the 
onboard computational limitations, occlusions, dynamic motion, and 
cluttered backgrounds hinder stable target identification across frames. 
DL-based detectors and trackers outperform classical models (e.g., SVMs, 
random forests) in robustness and precision but depend heavily on large 
annotated UAV datasets, which are scarce and often limited to coarse 
bounding boxes. Object detection performance is hindered by domain 
shifts between different types of view perspectives (ground and aerial 
views). It also suffers due to small object sizes, adverse weather, and 
complex and cluttered environments. Multi-scale and attention-based 
convolutional neural network (CNN) architectures can improve the abil­
ity of CNNs to detect small or partially occluded targets. However, these 
network types add additional computational requirements, which make 
it difficult to achieve adequate real-time performance when operating 
under severe size, weight, and power (SWAP) constraints. Lightweight 
CNN models such as Tiny-YOLO, MobileNet Variants, and Pruned CNNs 
have demonstrated the ability to achieve nearly real-time object detec­
tion capabilities on embedded graphics processing units (GPUs) and field 
programmable gate arrays (FPGAs). RL and MARL planning algorithms 
increase the ability of UAVs to perform adaptive rerouting, coopera­
tive target tracking, and collision avoidance during fast and dynamic 
multi-target operations. When combined with 3D (Dynamic Object) 
Segmentation, simultaneous localization and mapping (SLAM) also
provide the ability to create maps and localize in areas that have lit­
tle or no GPS coverage. There have been rapid advancements in UAV 
technologies, however, cross-domain generalization, energy-efficient 
model compression/retraining, real-time adaptive model deployment 
on edge computing devices, and long-term re-identification of tar­
gets remain key challenges that must be successfully addressed be­
fore true scalable, autonomous UAV perception capabilities can be
achieved.
Real-time processing constraints. 
The transition from simulations of 
UAVs to real-time deployment represents a pivotal step toward es­
tablishing completely autonomous and learning-based aerial systems. 
The current real-time perception and control capabilities of UAVs are 
constrained by the amount of computation, memory, and energy avail­
able on board; therefore, UAVs must execute deep learning models 
under a strict requirements of latency and endurance. To meet these
performance demands, a UAV will have to make trade-offs between 
complex deep learning architectures, which provide greater levels of 
accuracy but lower frame rates, versus lightweight deep learning ar­
chitectures (e.g., YOLO and MobileNetV3) as well as compression 
techniques (e.g., quantization, pruning, and distillation), which increase 
speed but may reduce some accuracy. Continuous inference accelerates 
battery depletion, emphasizing the need for energy-efficient accelerators 
and edge-AI hardware. Deploying models on embedded platforms (e.g., 
Jetson, Pixhawk) introduces software–hardware integration challenges 
for 3D localization, semantic reasoning, and graph-based situational 
awareness. Learning-based DaaS operations amplify these issues through 
multi-tenant orchestration across onboard, edge, and cloud layers, re­
quiring joint optimization of latency, reliability, and energy considering 
UAV payload, flight endurance, and communication constraints. RL and 
MARL frameworks often assume ideal connectivity, yet real-world band­
width limitations lead to delayed coordination and degraded swarm 
efficiency. DL models frequently exceed the SWAP budgets and therefore 
require aggressive compression methods and partial offloading, which 
results in increased latencies and reliance on the availability of stable 
networks. Additionally, differences between synthetic and real-world 
datasets limit the generalizability of the models, resulting in a need 
for federated retraining, hybrid co-training, and sim-to-real adaptation 
through digital twins. Furthermore, interpretability and certification

Learning-driven adaptive flight control. 
Next generation UAV control 
needs to move beyond 2D path based assumptions, slow convergence 
and fixed learning latencies by combining RL with MPC for immediate 
and online trajectory optimization. Both multi-objective optimization 
and PINNs can be used for improving energy efficiency, latency, and 
stability in response to dynamic airspace. Neural Architecture Search 
(NAS) and approximate computing can be used to further push the 
computational burden of DL frameworks, whereas graph-based and 
transformer architectures enhance long-range dependency modeling for 
accurate path prediction and coordination in the face of partial ob­
servability. For more robust non-linear learning, focusing on emerging 
quantum-inspired ML paradigms for high-dimensional, non-convex de­
cision spaces (quantum RL, tensor-based optimization) is essential for 
policy convergence and swarm coordination. When integrated with dig­
ital twin frameworks, these methodologies allow real-time learning, 
predictive maintenance strategies, and proactive mission planning that 
increase resilience to traffic congestion, environmental variability, and 
communication latency. This necessitates hierarchical or curriculum-
based learning (e.g., ACEMSL) to improve coordination and policy 
convergence under sparse rewards [154] Limited computation and 
energy restrict the use of large ML models, requiring lightweight archi­
tectures (e.g., pruned or MobileNet-based variants) with low overhead 
(≤1.14 GFLOPs).
Multi-modal perception and predictive control. 
Fundamental problems 
such as sensor noise, narrow field of view, and poor obstacle gener­

thermal, radar, and RGB data with recurrent convolutional attention 
and predictive depth estimation networks. This type of integration will 
improve spatio-temporal context-aware situations and reduce the fluc­
tuation cost of collisions in dynamic environments. Future research can 
concentrate on improving the design of adaptive event-triggered control 
and self-supervised depth learning to manage obstacles with varying en­
vironmental densities without using predefined maps. For high-speed or 
unstable environments, anticipative control is supported by combining 
the collision avoidance network with trajectory prediction. To ensure 
true real-world validation, the performance metrics like scalability, in­
ference latency, and robustness should be measured under real-world 
dynamics and resource limitations.

deviations during missions. In parallel, we can explore federated pol­

can maximize endurance without the need for extra communication

8.4.2 . Communication & networking
The practical and computational issues with current systems, such 
as dynamic channel conditions, limited onboard computation, energy 
constraints, scalability, and coordination between multiple UAVs and 
RIS units, must be specifically addressed in future UAV communication 
research [85,125,126,131,139,155–157].
Signal enhancement and channel dynamics under high mobility. 
Real-time 
prediction and adaptation are challenging in UAV communication due 
to rapidly changing LoS/NLoS conditions, Doppler shifts, and imperfect 
CSI. Future work should focus on hybrid ML frameworks that utilize TL 
for adaptive and predictive channel estimation. Real-world training and 
validation of UAV-IRS-assisted communication systems in diverse condi­
tions (e.g., disaster zones, smart cities, industrial corridors) will be vital 
to improving generalization. Hybrid DL architectures can jointly model 
UAV position, altitude, and orientation to map them onto channel pa­
rameters, enabling continuous link adaptation. Similarly, Simultaneous 
Navigation and Radio Mapping (SNARM) frameworks and radio map 
reconstruction through clustering and regression can enhance dynamic 
link prediction and real-time radio coverage modeling.
RIS-aided connectivity and cooperative beamforming. 
The learning meth­
ods like hierarchical DRL and FL can preserve privacy and scalability 
by separating short-term RIS phase control from long-term trajectory 
planning. Future studies should create multi-RIS coordination mecha­
nisms using techniques like dynamic segmentation, Dinkelbach-based 
convex optimization, and soft-Q learning. This will help UAVs to dynam­
ically control RIS configurations for better coverage and lower energy 
consumption. RL-based approaches like D3QN and the like can en­
sure multi-UAV–multi-RIS optimization, which improves reliability in 
dense environments by adjusting beamforming and power allocation in 
response to fluctuating network loads and interference.
UAV deployment, mobile edge computing, and resource offloading. 
Future 
systems should employ MADRL frameworks to jointly optimize 3D UAV 
trajectories, task offloading, and energy-aware computation scheduling. 
Game-theoretic frameworks can coordinate multiple service providers, 
while hierarchical edge–cloud ML architectures can allocate real-time 
control to edge nodes and high-level inference to cloud servers, mini­
mizing latency and computational burden. FL-enabled task distribution 
further ensures privacy-preserving training across UAV swarms without 
raw data exchange, reducing bandwidth overhead and communication 
delays.

under uncertain CSI, while federated DQN can preserve near-centralized 
performance while guaranteeing privacy. In order to increase resilience 
to adversarial conditions and guarantee transparent relay selection in 
heterogeneous UAV–IRS–MEC environments, future work should design 
robust cooperative protocols incorporating contrastive learning and 
domain adaptation.

8.4.3 . Task scheduling & resource allocation
The research should continue to explore hierarchical and multi-
agent RL for coordinated decentralized control. The adaptive feedback 
loops and dynamic state-space abstraction can make the current systems 
more responsive and robust. This can be especially helpful during in­
terference, partial observability, and unpredictable wireless conditions 
[82,131,131,149,158].
Computation offloading and fair resource distribution. 
As discussed ear­
lier, various factors like endurance, latency, stability, consumption of 
energy, computation, and communication resources should be jointly 
considered during optimization across UAV operations. While energy-
aware reward shaping speeds up policy convergence and improves 
robustness in dense UAV swarms, distributed learning-supported hybrid 
energy harvesting and cooperative caching can significantly extend mis­
sion duration. Moreover, we can implement multi-user, latency-aware 
edge offloading that simultaneously optimizes trajectories, ground ter­
minal placement, and RIS phase shifts. This can provide the systems with 
low-latency, equitable resource allocation. As such, offloading and task 
failures, which are incurred due to congestion and weak links, can be 
reduced. Semantic-aware UAV-MEC (SMEC) architectures reduce trans­
mission overhead by offloading only essential information, improving 
energy efficiency and communication reliability. Additionally, expert 
demonstration-based pretraining and imitation learning can enhance 
convergence stability in DRL-driven co-optimization under dynamic and 
heterogeneous UAV networks.
Caching, offloading, and mobility-aware coordination. 
The current sys­
tems are needed to overcome inefficiencies in caching and offloading 
failures due to user mobility and uneven content distribution. As such, 
they can use predictive caching and hybrid offloading strategies to 
address these challenges. These strategies blend model-based insights 
with online learning. Multi-agent cooperative learning frameworks can 
manage caching and offloading choices among UAVs while considering 
mobility, interference, and changes in bandwidth. The GNNs and similar 
models can be helpful in efficiently caching resources through content 
popularity models and user preference predictions. The combination of 
cache placement, trajectory planning, and spectrum allocation can al­
low these UAVs to maintain stable QoS during high demand and rapid 
fluctuations.
Multi-domain resource management and system scalability. 
The com­
bined optimization of computation, communication, and trajectory is 
a critical challenge for these systems. Future studies can effectively ex­
plore architectures like MARL or FRL for handling high-dimensional and 
multi-agent interactions in real-time. They can use model compression 
and lightweight inference through pruning, quantization, NAS, etc. to 
ensure deployment on energy-constrained UAVs. For dynamic multi-
domain applications, we can look into distributed scheduling functions 
and hierarchical coalition formations. Thus, it will be helpful in scaling 
and improving the energy efficiency of the systems. It can also enhance 
the operational reliability across large UAV fleets.
8.4.4 . Safe & secure operations
Sensor-aware and real-time attack detection. 
The onboard sensors on 
UAVs are vulnerable to IMU tampering, GPS spoofing, barometer-based 
attacks, coordinated multi-sensor denial, etc. Thus, the present research 
needs to focus on developing a real-time in-flight attack detection mech­
anism. This system must handle a variety of complex sensor data. We 
need lightweight learning models to balance detection accuracy with 
processing speed. This approach will help in avoiding the delays of rely­
ing on offline or cloud-based processing during operations. Architectures 
that integrate multiple sensors (e.g., GPS, IMU, barometer) along with 
control signals provide an alternative way to address the limitations of 
single-sensor detection methods. This can provide a greater level of resis­
tance to collaborative attacks and reduce false positive detection rates 
caused by background environmental noise. We can further combine 
statistical change detection and ML classifiers for timely and accurate

Data quality, robustness, and adversarial resilience. 
The learning perfor­
mance of ML models is dependent on the amount and quality of input 
data along with the variations in data. These systems have to continu­
ously suffer from class imbalance, unlabeled data, and correlated sensor 
features. Thus, the ongoing research must prioritize systematic simu­
lation of diverse attacks, expansion of datasets, and validation under 
diverse flight conditions. It is also crucial to ensure resilience against 
adversity caused by sensor perturbations. The existing proposed ML so­
lutions are still inefficient against adversarial manipulations. Therefore, 
we should actively integrate robustness-aware feature selection and 
adversarial training techniques. Combining uncertainty-aware learning 
with confidence estimation can help reduce the risk of misclassification 
linked to unclear and noisy sensor inputs.
Decentralized trust management and secure coordination. 
Dynamic and 
decentralized UAV swarms, where network topology keeps constantly 
changing, expose the centralized trust models and static node evaluation 
protocols to exploitation. Future systems must use adaptive, decen­
tralized trust frameworks to address these vulnerabilities. As network 
conditions change, such frameworks should be able to continuously eval­
uate relay behavior, confirm data consistency, and track node reliability. 
The blockchain-based trust mechanisms help in providing transparency 
and resistance to tampering but also introduce considerable challenges 
related to computation and scalability. This requires the development of 
lightweight blockchain solutions, efficient smart contract execution, and 
implementation of selective trust logging. Integration of these decentral­
ized ledgers with ML-based trust assessment can help in improving relay 
selection reliability without losing real-time performance.
Secure communication, privacy preservation, and swarm integrity. 
The 
increased adoption of UAV systems across diverse areas provides the 
adversaries with a number of ways to exploit the communication sys­
tem. These can range from interference-based attacks to privacy leakage 
across the D2D and U2U communications. Thus, the research must focus 
on improving privacy-centric protocols. It will be particularly beneficial 
in decentralized swarms where compromised relay nodes can act as con­
duits for spreading malicious activity. FL can be a viable solution for 
training cooperative security models and establishing trust and reliabil­
ity without requiring sensor data or flight data to be sent and stored in a 
central location. The research can explore ML-enabled intrusion detec­
tion to create dynamic intermediate countermeasures at the physical and 
network layers. It can provide adaptive beamforming, RIS phase config­
uration, and secure rerouting under imperfect CSI conditions. Security 
policies based on RL and similar approaches can further refine these re­
sponses by learning effective strategies for mitigation in uncertain and 
hostile environments.
Explainability, autonomy, and deployability. 
Security solutions should 
continue to ensure transparent operations for reliable autonomy. By 
combining Explainable AI (XAI) within attack detection and response 
solutions, the operators can have a reliable understanding of UAV 
behavior deviations. These systems can further use rule-based protec­
tions for clearer accountability and trust confidence. However, practical 
deployments need to account for payload limits, wind sensitivity, com­
munication latency, and battery capacity. As another direction, these 
energy-aware architectures have to be validated and benchmarked 
against real-time flight experiments and standardized UAV datasets. 
Onboard hardware and firmware can be integrated with lightweight 
security intelligence to provide continuous protection at the lowest 
operational level.
8.4.5 . Drone as a service
DaaS ecosystems need to configure multi-UAV coordination, real-
time perception, computational constraints, and economic sustainability 
for reliable systems [79,94,95,133,138,159].
Multi-tenant coordination and standardization. 
The joint optimization 
of computation, communication, and energy efficiency through SLA-
aware orchestration frameworks within heterogeneous UAV fleets must 
be the main focus of DaaS-oriented systems. Without requiring com­
plete state awareness, decentralized MARL and graph-based control 
mechanisms can enable scalable, data-driven cooperation, guaranteeing 
coordination under sporadic connectivity. Standardized DaaS interfaces, 
including unified APIs, data formats, and communication protocols, are 
essential to ensure interoperability across vendors and service layers. 
The standardization of benchmarking protocols is critical for improv­
ing transparency and reproducibility in experimentation. Therefore, it 
is necessary to create and adopt a set of statistical reporting stan­
dards for evaluating the reliability and reproducibility of results. The 
research should ensure the reporting of primary performance metrics 
like ID-switch rate, mean average precision, mission completion rate, 
multi-objective tracking accuracy, and energy efficiency. In addition to 
this, they should also include the associated operational metadata, such 
as one’s computational footprint, sensor configuration, and parameters 
associated with the altitude of each flight.
Real-time resource allocation and edge optimization. 
Hybrid edge-cloud 
coordination will be essential for the dynamic distribution of workload 
through onboard systems and the cloud. This can ensure an effective 
use of resources within the multi-tenant environments. Methods such as 
Successive Convex Approximation (SCA), Inverse Soft Q-Learning, and 
Dinkelbach-Based Conditions can be beneficial in determining the op­
timal allocation of resources and how users associate themselves with 
resources within this environment. Computational power and latency 
reduction can be guaranteed by energy-conscious neural architectures 
with intelligent on-chip scheduling. Service systems also require the in­
tegration of task-aware NNs with adaptive properties, which adapt their 
structure as per the evolving mission needs.
Robust perception and domain-generalized tracking. 
For cross-domain 
perception and stable long-term tracking, research needs to adopt 
lightweight domain-adaptive vision pipelines. Architectures that priori­
tize integrating multi-modal data with multi-scale feature integration, 
self-supervised learning, and few-shot learning will be essential for 
making learning systems more robust during adverse environmental 
scenarios. Hybrid convolution–transformer and sparse convolutional 
networks with adaptive self-attention, reinforced by RL-based dynamic 
anchor-frame selection, can strengthen UAV perception with low com­
putational overhead. In parallel, modeling temporal structures and 
contextual reasoning can help during re-identification and handling of 
occlusion. Uncertainty estimation can help ensure safety during critical 
missions. Cross-domain generalization should leverage domain random­
ization, transfer learning, and large pre-trained vision models (e.g., 
ViT, CLIP, DINO) for viewpoint-invariant aerial perception. Other tech­
niques, like knowledge distillation, dynamic selection of features, and 
parameter tuning, can also be explored. These can support edge-efficient 
real-time deployment within dynamic environments.
Holistic integration and real-world validation. 
Future studies should 
focus on developing service-oriented DaaS architectures that include 
adaptive autonomy, decentralized trust mechanisms, and multi-sensor 
resilience as intelligent decision frameworks. Real-world validation un­
der adversarial, urban, and dynamic mobility conditions is essential to 
assess latency, accuracy, and robustness trade-offs. Thus, we need to 
focus on establishing standardized datasets, digital twins, and large-
scale testbeds. It will help in bridging the gap between prototypes

8.5 . RQ5: what are the research implications and thrust themes shaping 
advancement in the ML-assisted UAVs realm?
Section 7.3.3, and more particularly, Supplementary Figs. 3 & 4, 
helps in understanding the apparent directional shift within the domain. 
Recent studies reflect growing attention toward multi-agent intelligence, 
secure wireless communication, and sustainable UAV technologies. As 
such, future thrust implications can be structured around these areas. 
Centrally, learning models need to be optimized for efficient running 
on edge devices. Techniques such as inverse soft-Q learning, SCA, adap­
tive self-attention, and hybrid/self-supervised RL models can enhance 
UAV perception, decision-making, and tracking, especially in dynamic, 
data-limited environments. Parallel advancements in transformer archi­
tectures, temporal modeling, extreme ML pipelines, quantum-based and 
knowledge distillation approaches should be considered. To improve the 
performance across small object detection, segmentation, and semantic 
interpretation, research should tweak YOLO variants, vision transform­
ers, Mask R-CNN, GNNs, and the like. At the same time, MARL adoption 
with PPO and soft-actor-critic has potential for adaptive control and 
autonomous navigation. Addressing communication-related challenges 
while maintaining energy-efficient scalability requires further explo­
ration. FL approaches, mobile edge computing, and RIS technologies 
constitute areas where academia needs to strengthen its attention. At 
the same time, system reliability and cyber resilience can be enhanced 
by digital twins and robust authentication procedures, like lightweight 
post-quantum cryptography. Future research also needs to consider 
sustainable coordinated operations alongside human-UAV interaction 
through multi-modal and language-integrated frameworks [79,94,95].
9 . Conclusion
The study used scientometric and content analysis to investigate 
the changing role of ML in UAV operations. The findings revealed 
three distinct developmental phases that shaped the research landscape. 
Following 2017, there has been a notable increase in publications and 
citations, which is indicative of growing scholarly interest. These devel­
opments have been driven by advancements in ML, the IoT, networking 
technologies (5G/6G), sensor miniaturization, and growing practical 
utility due to market demand and supportive policies. Strong funding 
policies and research ecosystems have supported major contributors 
like China and the United States of America, and promoting partner­
ships with countries like South Korea, India, Switzerland, Germany, and 
Israel will speed up UAV innovation worldwide. Our findings revealed 
five major research themes: navigation and control, communication and 
networking, task scheduling and resource allocation, secure and reliable 
UAV operations, and drone as a service. Future research should actively 
expand the standardized datasets to include instances with diverse envi­
ronmental conditions and security threats. Along with these, hardware 
innovations and interdisciplinary collaboration are required to truly cap­
ture the ML advancements. Technologies like 6G, quantum computing, 
and edge AI hold the promise for solving the existing hindrances such as 
real-time mission constraints and security threats. Simultaneously, the 
interpretability and explainability of UAV operational workflows must 
be given priority by the ML models. Both technical and non-technical 
user groups will benefit from increased comprehension, accountability, 
and validation as a result of these technologies’ increased transparency, 
reliability, and verifiability in decision-making. The interpretability of 
the model must also be given top priority in the research’s operational 
workflow in order to fully comprehend and validate decisions. These 
intellectual insights provide a structured knowledge framework for re­
searchers and policymakers to address UAV operational challenges and 
develop concise future research trajectories.
