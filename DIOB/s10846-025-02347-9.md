Abstract
Vertical Flight Ducted-Fan Quadrotors (VFDFQs) offer enhanced safety and lower maintenance compared to conventional 
quadrotors but present distinct control challenges, including higher sensitivity to disturbances and model uncertainties. 
While Sliding Mode Control (SMC) is a promising solution for its robustness, its application to VFDFQs is hindered by 
the chattering phenomenon, which causes severe oscillations and poses a risk to actuators. This paper bridges this gap by 
proposing a novel barrier-function-based adaptive global super-twisting sliding mode controller. The core of our method lies 
in integrating an adaptive continuous barrier function into a global super-twisting structure. This combination guarantees 
finite-time convergence of the sliding surface, provides robust stabilization against lumped uncertainties, and critically, 
eliminates control chattering by ensuring a continuous control signal. Extensive simulations under various disturbance 
scenarios validate the proposed controller's performance. The results demonstrate a significant improvement over standard 
SMC and adaptive SMC, with the proposed method reducing convergence time by approximately 50% and 18%, respectively, 
and lowering overshoot by about 80% and 5%, respectively. These quantified outcomes highlight the controller's superior 
transient performance and practical viability for VFDFQ applications.

This research utilizes a vertical flight unmanned aerial 
vehicles that resembles a quadrotor but has different struc-
ture than conventional quadrotors. Instead of four motors 
with propellers, this flying robot employs four fans to direct 
the airflow into hollow rings. The special design of these 
rings allows the air blown by the fans to accelerate through 
a circular opening and pass over a sloped surface shaped 
like an airfoil, significantly enhancing the airflow. The air at 
the rear, around, and in front of the rings is drawn into the 
main airflow due to aerodynamic processes and aligns with 
it. Ultimately, this results in amplifying the airflow into the 
rings and increasing the resulting thrust force. Due to the 
greater safety of ducted-fan quadrotors compared to con-
ventional quadrotors, the use of this type of quadrotors is 
more desirable, especially in some applications and places. 
However, in ducted-fan quadrotors, more power consump-
tion is needed to have same maneuverability of quadrotors. 
Although the quadrotor mechanism with a ducted-fan is 
simple to operate, it needs a complex control system to deal 
with problems such as parameter uncertainties, unmodeled 
dynamics, and disturbances such as wind, to achieve long 
flight capability like other unmanned aerial vehicles.
Ducted-fans, also known as air multiplier fans invented 
by James Dyson in October 2009, are generally more pow-
erful and quieter than traditional bladed fans [8]. Although 
they come at a higher price, they offer significant advan-
tages, including high efficiency, low weight, minimal wear, 
and enhanced safety due to the absence of blades. These fans 
gained attention in 2010 due to their unique features and 
were widely introduced in the global market, particularly for 
home applications [2, 8, 9]. Figure 1 illustrates the typical 
aerodynamical structure of a ducted-fan and their application 
for VFDFQs, demonstrating how they generate lift force to 
propel quadrotors. Thrust and angular torques are generated 
as four control signals by varying the speed of the four rotors 
in the ducted-fan quadrotor as conventional quadrotors, but 
the need to reduce power consumption causes to increase 
importance of control performance especially in terms of 
control signal quality and preventing output fluctuations.
The complex, underactuated, and highly nonlinear 
dynamics of quadrotors, compounded by model uncertain-
ties and external disturbances, necessitate the design of high-
performance robust controllers [9–11]. Among various non-
linear control strategies, Sliding Mode Control (SMC) has 
proven to be a highly appealing choice due to its inherent 
robustness and stability properties [12, 13]. The fundamental 
principle of SMC is to drive the system states onto a pre-
defined sliding manifold and maintain them on it, thereby 
ensuring the desired closed-loop performance [14]. In [15], 
an adaptive sliding mode control strategy is presented for 
attitude and altitude stabilization of a quadrotor unmanned 
aerial vehicle (UAV), where the input saturation is taken 
into consideration.
However, the application of conventional first-order 
SMC is hampered by the well-known 'chattering' phe-
nomenon. Chattering is characterized by high-frequency, 
finite-amplitude oscillations in the control signal, caused 
by the discontinuous sign function used to counteract 
uncertainties [14]. This is particularly detrimental to UAV 
actuators, leading to premature wear and potential instabil-
ity. To mitigate chattering, several advanced SMC variants 
have been developed. Higher-Order SMCs (HOSMCs), 
such as the Super-Twisting Algorithm (STA), retain the 
robustness of standard SMC while producing a continuous 
control signal, thereby significantly reducing chattering 
[16–18]. The Super-Twisting SMC (STSMC), a special

Parallel developments have focused on adaptive tech-
niques to handle uncertainties with unknown bounds. For 
instance, [21] combined integral backstepping with adap-
tive terminal SMC for quadrotor attitude control, while 
[22] employed a fractional-order SMC to attenuate wind 
disturbances. Recently, the integration of adaptive barrier 
functions with SMC has emerged as a powerful method 
to dynamically adjust control gains, ensuring robustness 
while minimizing overestimation [23, 24]. For example, 
[23] designed a finite-time nonsingular adaptive SMC with 
a barrier function for a conventional quadcopter, demon-
strating improved disturbance rejection. Recent research 
shows that combining STA with adaptive barrier functions 
can yield superior performance with smooth control sig-
nals, as seen in applications like mobile manipulators [25], 
parallel robots [26] and motor drives [27].
Beyond the sliding mode paradigm, other powerful 
nonlinear and intelligent control strategies have been suc-
cessfully applied to UAVs [28, 29]. Intelligent controllers 
leveraging fuzzy logic systems or neural networks have 
demonstrated remarkable capabilities in handling system 
uncertainties and nonlinearities without requiring precise 
mathematical models [30, 31] and fixed-wing UAVs [32].
Despite these advancements, the control of Ducted-fan 
Quadrotors (VFDFQs) remains a less explored domain. 
The unique aerodynamics and higher power requirements 
of VFDFQs demand controllers that are not only robust but 
also exceptionally smooth to prevent mechanical stress. 
The work in [33] represents a step in this direction, apply-
ing an adaptive SMC to a ducted-fan quadrotor. However, 
a significant gap remains in developing a unified control 
framework for VFDFQs that simultaneously guarantees 
three key properties: (1) finite-time convergence for rapid 
transient response, (2) robust stability against significant 
model uncertainties and external disturbances, and (3) 
the complete elimination of control chattering for smooth 
actuator operation. While methods like the saturation func-
tion [17, 34] can reduce chattering, they introduce a fixed 
boundary layer that compromises robustness and leads to 
steady-state error. In parallel to model-based robust con-
trol strategies, significant advances have been made in 
model-free and artificial intelligence (AI)-based optimal 
control laws. These approaches, such as reinforcement 
learning and bio-inspired optimization, do not require an 
explicit dynamic model and can learn optimal control poli-
cies directly from data, making them highly appealing for 
systems with complex, poorly modeled dynamics [35, 36].
To bridge this gap, this paper proposes a novel bar-
rier-function-based adaptive global super-twisting sliding 
mode controller for VFDFQs. The main contributions of 
this work are as follows:
•	 A novel hybrid control design that synergistically com-
bines the finite-time convergence and chattering attenu-
ation of the global super-twisting algorithm with the 
robust adaptation capabilities of a continuous barrier 
function. This specific combination is distinct from 
prior work: unlike the standard STA, our method incor-
porates a global sliding surface to eliminate the reaching 
phase; and unlike the barrier function SMC in [23], our 
design leverages the continuous control structure of STA 
to achieve chattering suppression without resorting to a 
fixed boundary layer or saturation function, which can 
compromise robustness. This controller is specifically 
tailored for the high-order, underactuated VFDFQ sys-
tem.
•	 A rigorous stability analysis using Lyapunov theory, 
formally proving finite-time convergence of the sliding 
surface and robust stabilization of the closed-loop system 
in the presence of bounded lumped uncertainties.
•	 A comprehensive simulation study demonstrating that the 
proposed controller achieves a significant performance 
improvement, quantified by a 50% and 18% reduction in 
convergence time compared to standard SMC and adap-
tive SMC, respectively, alongside an 80% reduction in 
overshoot compared to standard SMC, while generating 
smooth, chatter-free control signals.

tion 2 describes the dynamic model of the ducted-fan quad-
rotor. The proposed control design is detailed in Section 3. 
Its performance is assessed in Section 4. Finally, some con-
cluding remarks are given in Section 5.

To evaluate the model of a quadrotor, the quadrotor's speed 
and acceleration should be measured or estimated in the body 
coordinates and translated into the ground coordinates using 
a certain transformation matrix [37]. To this aim and to avoid 
complicating the modeling process, the equations of motion 
are established under the following mild assumptions: (1) The 
ground is considered flat in an inertial space, (2) The structure 
of the body is symmetrical, (3) The inertia matrix of the sys-
tem is constant, (4) The weight of the system is constant, (5) 
The body is rigid, and (6) The center of mass of the system 
coincides with the center of the body frame. Furthermore, to 
create a simple inverse model that can be easily incorporated 
in control algorithms, it is essential to simplify the dynamics 
of the quadrotor to some extent. The quadrotor dynamic equa-
tions can be simplified according to the following considera-
tions: (i) The rotational terms exhibit specific characteristics 
due to the interactions arising from the coupling of angular 
velocities including Coriolis and gyroscopic effects. However, 
since the primary objective is to maintain a stable flight pos-
ture for the quadrotor, it can be assumed that its motion is 
close to a steady flight state. Consequently, angular changes, 
particularly around the x and y axes, can be treated as small, 
allowing us to simplify the associated equations. (ii) The angu-
lar accelerations ( ̇p, ̇q, ̇r) determined in the body frame do not 
correspond directly to the Euler angle accelerations 
( ̈𝜑, ̈𝜃, ̈𝜓)
 
calculated with respect to the ground frame. The transforma-
tion matrix TA defines the relationship between the angular 
velocities in the ground frame and those in the body frame 
attached to the quadrotor. While this matrix approaches the 
identity matrix during steady flight, the angular acceleration 
equations are still directly referenced to the Euler angular 
accelerations. The effects related to the above considerations 
and their impact on the dynamics of a quadrotor have been 
extensively studied in [16, 38].

The overall structure of the proposed control system for 
the VFDFQ is summarized in the block diagram shown in 
Fig. 4. The controller consists of an inner loop for attitude 
and altitude control, which implements the proposed BF-
AGSTMC law (Eq. 42), and an outer loop for position con-
trol, which generates the desired roll and pitch angles. The 
adaptation mechanism, governed by Eqs. (43–45), dynami-
cally adjusts the control gain based on the sliding variable 
S(t) to ensure robustness and chattering suppression.
Remark 1: The proposed control law is derived in continu-
ous time, but its practical implementation on a digital flight 
controller requires addressing discrete-time effects. For a suc-
cessful real-world application, the following should be consid-
ered: (i) A sufficiently high sampling frequency (e.g., 100 Hz 
to 1 kHz) should be used to capture the system dynamics. (ii) 
The integral action in the auxiliary control law (see the term 
ki ∫‖S(t)‖dt in Eq. 36) requires an anti-windup mechanism 
(e.g., conditional integration) to prevent performance degra-
dation when control signals saturate. (iii) The adaptive laws 
(Eqs. 33, 44, 46) should incorporate a gradient limiter or a 
maximum update rate per sampling period to prevent overly 
aggressive gain increases triggered by sensor noise, enhancing 
robustness in a digital environment. Addressing these imple-
mentation details is a vital step towards the experimental vali-
dation of the proposed controller.
Remark 2 (Parameter Tuning): A sensitivity analysis 
of the proposed controller reveals the impact of key control-
ler parameters on the performance of the closed-loop sys-
tem. The sliding parameter γ (from Eq. 26) primarily affects 
the response speed; increasing γ reduces settling time but 
increases control effort and can induce overshoot. The bar-
rier function boundary ε (from Eq. 43) governs the trade-off 
between steady-state accuracy and chattering suppression; 
a smaller ε yields smoother control and higher accuracy but 
may slightly slow convergence. The parameters used in this 
study (e.g., 훾= 3, 휀b = 0.1 ) were selected to balance fast 
finite-time convergence with smooth, chatter-free control 
signals, demonstrating the controller's tunability for differ-
ent performance objectives.
Remark 3 (Adaptation Procedure): The overall adapta-
tion logic for the gain ̂Γj(t) ensures a two-phase operation: 
a rapid initial adaptation phase to drive the system state 
towards the sliding surface, followed by a smooth, con-
tinuous phase that maintains the state within a predefined 
boundary (ε) without chattering. Intuitively, this process 
works as follows: Initially, the error | Sj(t) | is large, and the 
gain ̂Γaj(t) increases according to a power law (Eq. 46) to 
ensure a fast, finite-time reaching phase. Once the error 
enters the neighborhood of the sliding surface (|Sj(t)|< ε), the 
adaptation switches to the barrier function ̂Γpsb(t) (Eq. 47). 
This function is small when the error is small, providing 
a smooth control signal, but can grow rapidly if the error 
attempts to leave the boundary layer, thus providing the 
necessary robustness to keep the state near the desired equi-
librium. This combination yields the desired performance 
of fast convergence, chattering elimination, and sustained 
robustness.

5  Conclusions
This paper proposed a barrier function-based adaptive 
super-twisting sliding mode control approach for Verti-
cal Flight Ducted-Fan Quadrotors. The aim is to guarantee 
fast finite-time convergence and robust stabilization of the 
VFDFQ, even under significant model uncertainties and 
external disturbances. The robustness is formally proven 
via Lyapunov analysis, showing stability despite bounded 
lumped uncertainties, while the finite-time convergence of 
the sliding surface ensures a rapid transient performance. 
The controller's novelty lies in the specific integration of 
a global sliding surface, the super-twisting algorithm's 
continuous control law, and an adaptive continuous bar-
rier function. This synthesis differentiates it from: (i) The 
foundational Super-Twisting work in [19], by incorporating 
a global sliding surface and an adaptive barrier gain for 
enhanced performance. (ii) The barrier function SMC of 
[16], by utilizing the STA's inherent continuity to eliminate 
chattering, avoiding the robustness trade-offs of discontinu-
ous control with a boundary layer. Performance analysis of 
the VFDFQ system under various scenarios showed that 
the proposed controller improved the convergence time 
by about 50% and 18% compared to sliding mode control-
ler the adaptive sliding mode controller, respectively. In 
particular, the reduction of overshoot compared to SMC 
is about 80% and compared to ASMC is about 5%. Based 
on these results, it can be concluded that the use of the 
adaptive continuous barrier function in the proposed con-
troller has led to the ducted-fan quadrotor system moving 
more accurately to the desired trajectory and following 
lower overshoot and undershoot values ​at a more appropri-
ate time. Especially, this controller has performed better 
in removing the effect of disturbance and having control 
signals with better implementation capability. Real-world 
applications present additional challenges, such as actua-
tor saturation limits, communication delays, sensor noise, 
and truly “unseen” uncertainties whose behavior may not 
conform to the bounded assumption used in our design. 
Furthermore, the high-frequency dynamics of the physical 
actuators, which are ideal in simulation, can lead to unex-
pected interactions with the control law in practice. In the 
continuation of this research, the following suggestions can 
be mentioned: To control this quadrotor, other non-linear 
control methods can be used and the results of their per-
formance can be compared with the controller designed in 
this research. In the design of the controller, it is assumed 
that the system attitude and position signals are fully avail-
able. However, in practice, due to noise or malfunctions in 
the sensor, correct information may not be available, which 
necessitates the use of an observer. Due to the need for more

adaptation and the need for convergence to appropriate val-

the designed controller in this research be implemented on a
