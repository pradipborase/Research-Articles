Disturbance-observer-based Finite-time Following Control With Wind
Resistance Capability for Multiple GPS-denied UAVs
Chih-Lyang Hwang* ‚Ė†, Cheng-Yi Li, and Chen-Wei Wu
Abstract: In this work, a disturbance-observer-based finite-time following control with wind resistance capability
(DO-FTFC-WRC) is designed to rapidly perform an assigned following of multi-UAV in a GPS-denied region, e.g.,
warehouse, library, factory. The inner-loop PID control in each UAV can improve its control behavior. Without the
original nonlinear complex dynamics, its outer loop is modeled by a second-order system with unknown distur-
bances, including sensor noises, system uncertainties, turbulence caused by ground effect, or extra wind gusts. The
desired 3D pose for the leader is assumed to be a virtual leader. Subsequently, followers are one-by-one to execute
a time-varying formation by adjusting their relative poses. To realize the task in the presence of disturbances, the
suggested DO-FTFC-WRC includes i) equivalent control to tackle the newly designed following task and compen-
sate the estimated disturbance, and ii) switching control with time-varying gain to deal with extra uncertainties for
enhancing robust performance. Finally, the simulations and experiments as compared with the nonlinear finite-time
following control or the inner-loop PID control with/without extra wind condition for a representative forward,
180 degrees clockwise turning, then backward motions validate the effectiveness and superiority of the proposed
DO-FTFC-WRC.
Keywords: Disturbance observer, finite-time following control, inner-loop PID control, Lyapunov stability theory,
multi-UAV.

INTRODUCTION
Due to the autonomous operation, communication /pro-
cessing capabilities, and mobility, unmanned aerial ve-
hicles (UAVs) are envisaged in many applications [1-3].
However, they must have the GPS feedback for naviga-
tion. On the contrary, the navigation in the GPS-denied
region, e.g., warehouse, library, will be a challenge. It is
known that the optimization of UAV flight path to ensure
the accuracy of target surveillance or security is impor-
tant. Nevertheless, the surveillance or secure performance
of a single UAV is limited or inefficient when some ob-
structions (e.g., pillars or pipeline in the build, shelves
for inventory or book) appear in the flight path [5-9].
Hence, a multi-UAV cooperative optimization model or
the coverage path planning in wireless networks (e.g.,
WiFi network) can improve surveillance or secure effi-
ciency when obstructions of different scales appear [9-11].
Moreover, through the artificial intelligence of things, a
well-organized group of UAVs outperforms a single large
UAV in terms of device scalability, maintenance, and ex-
pense [12].
Due to the effective and efficient ability for perform-
ing complex and challenging tasks, e.g., a wide range of
security and remote monitoring applications, disaster res-
cue, communication cooperation among vehicles, the for-
mation and cooperation of multi-UAV are extensively in-
vestigated [10-29]. In [10], at the expense of the energy
consumption and transmission unreliability, a multi-UAV
cooperative path optimization model is developed to ad-
just the surveillance location of each UAV. In [11], a fuzzy
fixed time following control for multi-UAV with input
fault and saturation is designed. In [12], an AIoT-based
navigation and formation control by combining deep re-
inforcement learning reduces the collision risk of obsta-
cles and other UAVs. In [13], a multi-UAV networked
system under Wiener and Poisson random fluctuations is
achieved by stochastic robust team tracking control. In
[14], a composite adaptive disturbance observer-based de-
centralized fractional-order fault-tolerant control, incor-
porating fractional-order sliding-mode surfaces, nonlinear
disturbance observers, fuzzy wavelet neural networks, and
robust controllers, achieves the attitude tracking control of
networked UAVs. In [15], a distributed command force is
first synthesized in the position loop by an immersion and
invariance adaptation strategy for the exponential mass es-

dynamics of formation control [19], controller designs
separated by position and attitude subsystems [22], model
prediction control [24], PC-based time-varying formation
control [26], fire patrol planning [29]. It is also known that
finite-time tracking control of time-varying pose is better
than that using asymptotical tracking control [30-32].
Most theoretical studies [11,13-20,22-24,27] consider
the nonlinear complex dynamics of each UAV for the de-
sign of formation control. Due to the limitation in edge
computing, their implemented performances will be de-
teriorated or difficult to achieve. Since the indoor UAV
can‚Äôt have a GPS for the localization and then navigation,
Lidar range finder [33] and optical sensor [34] in this ar-
ticle substitute for GPS to achieve a closed-loop feedback
system. It also can use a multimodal UAV 3D trajectory
exposure system to achieve a closed-loop system in the
GPS-denied region [35]. Since optical sensor uses visual
features on the ground to obtain the estimated 2D veloc-
ity, a uniform ground has adverse effect for the estimation.
In contrast, some patterns on the ground improve the po-
sitioning accuracy. On the other hand, Lidar range finder
can achieve an accurate and fast response for the height
of UAV. Finally, the EKF-fused Lidar range finder/optical
sensor and IMU for the real-time localization of UAV is
accomplished and verified by distributive UWB network
[30]. The corresponding 3D position error is better than
that using low frequency of mechanical antenna average
position error 0.45 m in 10 m √ó 10 m region [35]. It con-
firms the effectiveness of our Lidar range finder and op-
tical sensor to achieve the positioning in GPS-defined re-
gion.
After the above construction, the UAV with PID control
first improves or simplifies the system dynamic behavior
[11,36]. Nevertheless, its robust performance caused by
input fault and constraint, extra wind gusts, or other un-
certainties is often poor. To this end, outer control loop
feedback by its pose and velocity in this loop is applied
to design its acceleration command so that the 3D pose
following control of multi-UAV ameliorates the forma-
tion in the presence of the above-mentioned disturbances
(or uncertainties). At first, the connected following error
for the present UAV and following UAV is designed. The
nonlinear filtering following error dynamics of each UAV
includes linear dynamic part to shape the frequency re-
sponse, and its fractional exponent to obtain a finite-time
following ability to null following error [16,31,32]. To
deal with the disturbances brought about by system un-
certainties or extra wind gusts, a disturbance observer is
designed to compensate and attenuate their adverse ef-
fect [14,19,20]. It doesn‚Äôt need the assumption of bounded
time-derivative of disturbance [37] such that they can con-
tain high-frequency components.

CONCLUSIONS AND FUTURE STUDIES
In this paper, a disturbance-observer-based finite-time
following control with wind resistance capability (DO-
FTFC-WRC) for the GPS-denied multiple UAVs with sen-
sory noises, input fault/constraint, extra wind condition,
and other uncertainties, is designed to perform a time-
varying 3D pose‚Äôs following task. Due to the inner-loop
PID control with the quaternion for coordinate transfor-
mation and the EKF fused Lidar range finder/optical sen-
sor and IMU in each UAV, the nonlinear complex dynam-
ics of the original UAV is not required, and the control
behavior of UAV is improved. Under these circumstances,
its outer loop is modeled as a second-order system with the
above unknown dynamic uncertainties. Not only does the
nonlinear switching gain in switching control augments as
its connected following error is near zero to attain its quick
finite-time following ability, but also the above dynamic
uncertainties are online estimated by disturbance observer
and then compensated by equivalent control. We summa-
rize that the average position errors with and without ex-
tra wind conditions of the DO-FTFC-WRC are 49.1% and
36.7% better than those of LL-PID-FC. Likewise, the av-
