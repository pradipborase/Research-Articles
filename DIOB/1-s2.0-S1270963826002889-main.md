Sliding mode control (SMC) is one of the most popular nonlinear 
control methods which applies robustness in quadcopter UAV systems, 
especially when uncertainties are present [7]. The approach provides 
stability and precise tracking by forcing system states onto a given slid-
ing manifold. Under sliding mode control, the closed-loop dynamics ex-
hibit strong robustness to matched parameter variations and external 
disturbances, though performance may still be inпђВuenced by unmatched 
uncertainties-a property particularly important to cope with real-world 
conditions. Intrinsic robustness in SMC makes the technique very ideal 
for quadcopter systems that will tend to be subjected to external distur-
bances, including wind gusts, payload пђВuctuations, and sensor noises. 
An integrator-supported sliding mode control method is constructed for 
quadcopter dynamics robust stabilization under uncertainty conditions 
[8,9]. By obliterating the reaching phase, the method forces robustness 
at the very beginning, resulting in sensational closed-loop performance 
improvements [10]. However, it should be noted that the integral slid-
ing mode control method does not decrease the system order, i.e., the 
parameters and state of the whole system are all involved in sliding 
mode. The property may impact the control performance, particularly 
on parametric variation sensitive systems and dynamic perturbation. To 
alleviate high frequency chattering issue of SMC, higher-order sliding 
mode approaches such as the Super-Twisting Sliding Mode Control (ST-
SMC) have been proposed [11]. ST-SMC improves convergence speed 
and reduces chattering compared to classical SMC, but it still requires 
accurate system modeling and struggles under strong nonlinearities and 
noisy measurements.
Fuzzy logic control is another technique that has been used very 
widely in quadcopter systems. Fuzzy logic controllers (FLCs) use lin-
guistic rules to address uncertainties and nonlinearities, which are most 
appropriately addressed in uncertain and complex environments. Fuzzy 
logic blended with sliding mode control, or Fuzzy Sliding Mode Con-
trol (FSMC), has been proven to minimize chattering and enhance ro-
bustness [12,13]. This hybrid method integrates the advantages of both 
methods to oпђАer a пђВexible, adaptive control method for quadcopter sys-
tems. Event-triggered control is a next-generation approach that min-
imizes the computational burden and communication loads in control 
systems. In event-triggered control strategies, control updates are done 
only when necessary; hence, eпђГciency is optimized and resource us-
age is minimized. Event-triggered control approaches have been used 
for quadcopter systems and demonstrated superior improvements in 
performance and resource usage [14,15]. For instance, event-triggered 
MPC has been formulated to reduce the computational burden with op-
timal control performance. Hybrid Control approaches have also been 
investigated for quadcopter systems, wherein two or more control ap-
proaches are hybridized. For instance, combining sliding mode con-
trol with adaptive control or fuzzy logic control can beneпђБt from the 
strengths of both and result in enhanced robustness and performance 
[16,17]. Switched Control techniques, which alternate among various 
control laws depending on system behavior, have also been investigated 
in the case of quadcopter systems. These techniques are very eпђАective 
for controlling multiple operating mode systems or systems with large 
dynamics variations. Switched control techniques have been shown to 
oпђАer robust and adaptive control performance in quadcopter systems
[18,19].
In addition, backstepping control has also been of concern due to 
its systematic design process and capacity to manage nonlinear systems 
with unmatched uncertainties. Through recursive control law design for 
subsystems, backstepping control provides stability and tracking perfor-
mance to the system. When merged with sliding mode control, backstep-
ping methods have proven to improve robustness and eliminate chatter-
ing in quadcopter systems [20,21]. It is especially eпђАective in managing 
the underactuated dynamics of quadcopters.
To further enhance output feedback control methodologiesвАЩ ro-
bustness against uncertainties as well as disturbances from outside 
sources, observer-based methods have grown to become highlighted 
ones. Observer-based techniques estimate such uncertain disturbances 
using observers and then controllers get reconпђБgured for maintaining 
the stability of the system, as outlined in previous literature [22вАУ24]. 
Those observer-based methodologies have proven eпђГcient in forcing 
the state variables of a vehicle towards desired state variables. The 
techniques of observer-based sliding mode control proposed in [25] 
demonstrate chattering suppression while improving robustness to un-
certainties. In LevantвАЩs work [26], a high-order robust exact diпђАeren-
tiator with constant gains was designed within the framework of an 
output-feedback controller to estimate the derivative states, assuming 
that the system position is bounded. Unfortunately, the execution of 
this controller may be questionable in practice as systems are often sub-
ject to high levels of noise and/or fast dynamic пђВuctuations. Another 
eпђГcient approach that enhances the robust performance of control sys-
tems is the Disturbance Observer-Based Control (DOBC). DOBC meth-
ods estimate and counteract disturbances in real-time to provide steady, 
accurate control performance. DOBC methods have been implemented 
successfully on quadcopter systems and provided enhanced disturbance 
rejection and track accuracy [27,28]. Nonlinear disturbance observers, 
for example, have been employed for external disturbance estimation 
and cancellation to achieve accurate control of quadcopter dynamics. 
Several research papers have incorporated the use of neural networks 
in controller design to enhance robustness in approximating near un-
known nonlinearities and disturbances [29,30]. When combined with 
observers, these methods based on neural networks have been found to 
be extremely eпђГcient in enhancing robustness of control systems. By 
approximating complex terms successfully, these methods bring about 
more robust and precise control systems.
Reinforcement Learning (RL) is now becoming increasingly popular 
for controlling quadcopter systems. RL controllers learn the best policies 
via experience while exploring the environment, hence with very high 
adaptability to evolving conditions. Deep Reinforcement Learning (DRL) 
and Q-learning methods have been used for quadcopter systems, which 
exhibited deep performance and adaptability improvements [31,32]. In 
addition to neural networks, adaptive control methods have extensively 
been studied to cope with uncertainties in quadcopters. Adaptive control 
methods tune controller parameters online depending on system perfor-
mance, providing robustness against model uncertainty and external dis-
turbances. For example, Model Reference Adaptive Control (MRAC) and 
Lyapunov-based adaptive control have been successfully implemented 
in quadcopter systems, providing enhanced tracking performance and 
disturbance rejection [33,34]. They are especially appropriate for use 
when system dynamics are not yet well established or undergo extreme 
пђВuctuations. Robust Control techniques, e.g., H-inпђБnity control and рЭЬЗ-
synthesis, have been attempted for quadcopters. These methods design 
controllers oпђАering the best performance under uncertainties and dis-
turbances. Robust control techniques have been found to achieve high 
robust stability guarantees and disturbance rejection in quadcopter sys-
tems [35,36]. For example, H-inпђБnity control has been applied to de-
sign controllers, leading to the least disturbance eпђАect on system per-
formance with assured robust stability and performance.
Predictive control approaches, such as Model Predictive Control 
(MPC), have also been proposed for quadcopter systems. MPC applies 
to пђБnite-horizon optimal control optimization of the control input in the 
presence of system constraints and, therefore, пђБnds applications in those 
situations where stringent trajectory tracking and avoidance of obstacles 
are required. Robustness and adaptivity features were implemented in 
MPC during recent advancements to respond against uncertainties and 
disturbances eпђАectively [37,38]. For instance, eпђАective MPC methods 
have been developed to account for bounded model uncertainties and 
disturbances to provide stable and accurate control performance under 
stringent constraints. Nonlinear Model Predictive Control (NMPC) is an-
other sophisticated control method that has been implemented for quad-
copter systems. NMPC predicts the systemвАЩs future states based on its 
nonlinear model and determines the optimal control inputs by solving a 
constrained пђБnite-time optimal control problem. The approach is known 
to yield very good tracking results and is robust to uncertainties [39,40].

Although sliding mode and observer-based control strategies for the

open. Classical SMCs induce chattering, which has adverse eпђАects on 
the quality of the output [43], high-frequency actuator vibration, and

uncertainties and disturbances. Neural network-based adaptive control

tors based on high-gain observers or robust diпђАerentiators are very sen-
sitive to noise and perform poorly in the presence of strong disturbances. 
Furthermore, fractional-order calculus applications in sliding mode con-
trol are still in their infancy, especially for quadcopter systems that 
require greater robustness and пђВexibility. Finally, most adaptive and 
observer-based schemes do not exhibit пђБnite-time convergence, which 
is crucial for drone operations in real time, as they require fast and pre-
cise control. The drawbacks of these techniques motivate us to propose a 
novel framework based on fractional-order calculus, NN adaptation, and 
robust diпђАerentiators, which provides a uniпђБed solution for quadcopter 
UAV control in the presence of unknown perturbations.
1. Novel NA-FOSTSMC framework: A Neuro-Adaptive Fractional-
Order Super-Twisting Sliding Mode Control (NA-FOSTSMC) frame-

cal SMC controllers that suпђАer from poor robustness and chattering, 
and standard ST-SMC approaches that rely heavily on accurate mod-

пђБnite-time convergence and enhanced disturbance rejection.
2. Integration of DRNAM for adaptability: A Deep Residual Neural

dependence on precise system models.
3. Development of FOST-SMD diпђАerentiator: A Fractional-Order 
Super-Twisting Sliding Mode DiпђАerentiator (FOST-SMD) is proposed 
to estimate velocity states based on the noisy position measure-
ments. Traditional diпђАerentiators are sensitive to noise and amplify 
it, whereas the fractional-order-based approach provides dependable 
feedback in the presence of uncertainties and enhances the robust-
ness of the solution when applied to UAVs.
Besides the methodological contributions, the proposed approach 
has some practical merits. The use of fractional calculus and super-

the closed-loop system and to eliminate chattering in the presence of 
matched uncertainties and external disturbances. In addition, a strict

the proposed approach are further validated through high-пђБdelity MAT-

5.  Conclusion
This paper proposed a fractional-order super-twisting sliding mode 
control for quadcopter trajectory tracking under uncertainties. By refor-
mulating the system into an input-decoupled form and integrating frac-
tional calculus with super-twisting design, пђБnite-time convergence and 
chattering suppression were achieved. A deep residual neural network 
with attention mechanisms estimated unknown nonlinearities, reducing 
model dependence, while a fractional-order super-twisting diпђАerentia-
tor ensured accurate velocity estimation from position data. Analytical 
stability was proven via Lyapunov theory, with tracking errors converg-
ing to bounded regions. Quantitative metrics (IAE, ISCI) also veriпђБed the 
reduction in error and control magnitude, and the simulations showed 
faster convergence, a smaller steady-state error, and less overshoot com-
pared with traditional methods. Though there are still limitations in 
computing complexity, data dependency, and parameter tuning, future 
research will focus on developing lightweight models, adaptive strate-
gies, and hardware-in-the-loop veriпђБcation to improve real-time appli-
cability.
