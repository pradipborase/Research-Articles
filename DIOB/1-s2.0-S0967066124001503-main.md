A B S T R A C T
Disturbance observer-based control is widely employed in control systems. It demonstrates the ability to
estimate intricate disturbances for subsequent measurement, thereby enabling their compensation in the
output of existing controllers. Nevertheless, disturbance observer-based control shows limited robustness when
utilizing a fixed nominal model. Simultaneously, the fixed time constant of the Q-filter prevents the securement
of filtering performance across various flight speed areas. This study aims to enhance robustness in both open-
and closed-loop disturbance observer-based control by carefully selecting the Q-filter time constant through a
comprehensive transfer function analysis. The transfer function characterizing the quadrotor unmanned aerial
vehicle is derived from highly reliable dynamic modeling obtained through wind-tunnel testing, with the
nominal model computed by the flight speed. We propose variable disturbance observer-based control by using
a nominal model according to flight speed and selecting the Q-filter of disturbance observer-based control by
considering the characteristics of the dynamic model, disturbance, and sensor noise. Numerical simulations and
flight tests are conducted to compare the controller’s performance, focusing specifically on attitude tracking
during a round-trip flight with a slung load.
1. Introduction
Small Unmanned Aerial Vehicles (UAVs) are becoming more ad-
vanced with new sensor and microcomputer technologies, leading to
research efforts to optimize their control and utilization. With their
unique ability to hover, fly, and land in confined spaces, Quadrotor
UAVs have diverse applications in safety, risk management, environ-
mental monitoring, infrastructure management, and agriculture (An-
weiler & Piwowarski, 2017; Ghazbi, Aghli, Alimohammadi, & Akbari,
2016; Gupte, Mohandas, & Conrad, 2012; Tezza & Andujar, 2019).
Disturbance rejection is essential in aerial system design for flight
operations. Robust control are often employed to achieve effective
disturbance rejection, but they can lead to trade-offs in nominal per-
formance. Passive anti-disturbance control is frequently used when it
is not feasible to measure disturbances directly. However, adjusting
controllers for robustness can decrease performance. Striking a balance
between tracking and disturbance rejection remains a challenging task.
Quadrotors require robust attitude control that can sustain the desired
attitude despite uncertainties. Linear and nonlinear techniques like lin-
ear quadratic regulators (LQR) (Dharmawan, Priyambodo, et al., 2017),
model predictive control (MPC) (Bui, Van Nguyen, & Phung, 2023; He,
Wen, Stojanovic, Liu, & Luan, 2022), adaptive sliding mode control
(ASMC) (Qiu, Zhang, Jing, & Gao, 2021), and backstepping control
(BC) (Kim, Cho, & Jung, 2021) can address this challenge. Adaptive

Disturbance observer-based control (DOBC) is a widely known and
influential robust control technique considering model uncertainty fac-
tors. DOBC is an effective strategy for improving control system perfor-
mance by accurately estimating and compensating for external distur-
bances. Due to its versatility and effectiveness, this technique is widely
used in diverse fields, including robotics, automotive, and aerospace.
DOBC’s straightforward structure makes it a preferred choice among
control engineers and researchers (Chen, Yang, Guo, & Li, 2015). DOBC
uses a disturbance observer (DO) to estimate disturbances caused by ex-
ternal perturbations and uncertainties in the plant. These disturbances

DOs leverage the inverse of the nominal model to emulate a nomi-

The study on the impact of the Q-filter time constant of DOBC was
detailed in Ref. Lee and Jung (2016). After conducting simulations
and experiments, the characteristics of sensor noise and complementary
behaviors had been explained. The selection of the time constant
determined the disturbance bandwidth. The results were presented,
considering the Q-filter time constant about the control system’s sam-
pling interval and the natural frequency of the plant. In this study,
a fixed nominal model and time constant were employed. The DOBC
algorithm strategy is well-suited for a stable system with unchanged
dynamic characteristics. The nominal model estimates the system’s
state in terms of disturbance. As the system’s state changes, the nominal
model must adapt accordingly. Additionally, the time constant of the
Q-filter needs to be set to an appropriate cutoff frequency.
Extended state observer (ESO) is a technique for estimating a dy-
namic system’s state and external noise. It has been found to improve
control performance by estimating the system state and external inter-
ference. In Ref. Hosseini-Pishrobat and Keighobadi (2019), ESO with a
nonlinear gain function was designed to estimate gyroscope dynamics’
speed and disturbance vector through the measured position signal
to satisfy robust performance. Ref. Hosseini-Pishrobat, Keighobadi,
Oveisi, Nestorović, et al. (2018) approximated and compensated for
unstructured disturbances by considering the mismatch between the
primary plant and the nominal mathematical model. ESO is applied in
the framework of disturbance rejection control to handle a wide range
of uncertainties. However, applying ESO in real-time poses challenges
due to its sensitivity to the initial conditions of the estimated state,
difficulties in processing nonlinear systems, and high computational
demands.
A recent study examined the integration of DOBC with a robust
internal-loop compensator (RIC) to create a comprehensive framework
for disturbance compensation (Kim & Chung, 2003). The RIC plays a
crucial role in enhancing control performance by addressing uncertain-
ties and disturbances within the internal loop of the control system.
Combining DOBC with RIC further improves the control system’s dis-
turbance rejection capabilities. Notably, the robustness of DOBC is
maintained by separating the Q-filter from the mixed-sensitivity op-
timization problem, which strengthens the overall robustness of the
control system. In the DOBC, selecting a suitable nominal model and
determining the time constant for the Q-filter is essential. This is crucial
for ensuring system performance and robustness.
Some previous studies assumed that the plant and nominal models
were the same or that the moment of inertia (MOI) was constant about
all axes (Mokhtari, Cherki, & Braham, 2017; Wang & Su, 2015). While
these assumptions are practical, they may only partially capture the
subtle aspects of the nominal model. Many studies commonly employ
a uniform time constant for all plant states. However, this approach
can lead to performance degradation and reduced robustness, mainly
when significant differences exist between the nominal model and
the actual system. Therefore, selecting the appropriate time constant
necessitates thoroughly evaluating the system’s dynamics, disturbance
characteristics, and performance standards. In Ref. Kong and Tomizuka
(2013), the nominal model parameters were optimized in the discrete-
time domain to enhance the stability robustness of DOB. Since the
closed pole of the DOB was assumed to be subject to uncertainty, the
magnitude of the maximum uncertainty was determined as a measure
of robustness. The optimal parameters of the nominal model were

5. Conclusion
Our research introduced a novel approach called Variable Distur-
bance Observer-Based Control (VDOBC) to address the limitations asso-
ciated with disturbance observer-based control (DOBC) methods when
applied to Unmanned Aerial Vehicles (UAVs). VDOBC dynamically ad-
justed the nominal model and time constant, seamlessly integrating into
the flight control computer. Our study demonstrated that VDOBC sur-
passed DOBC in enhancing attitude tracking and improving disturbance
estimation accuracy. Utilizing a linearized dynamic model based on
wind-tunnel testing as the nominal model, VDOBC effectively estimated
disturbances, such as model uncertainty or slung load moment, and
compensated for them in the controller. However, it is essential to
note that system hardware failure or structural vibrations may impose
certain limitations on performance. In fixed systems such as manipula-
tors, whose dynamic characteristics remain stable over time or where
the tolerance of disturbance is higher than the system’s limits, VDOBC
has its limitations. It cannot guarantee control performance in such
cases. Despite the limitation of knowledge of general linear models,

such as gain scheduling, simply comprehending usual linear parts en-
ables achieving superior performance compared to conventional DOBC.
Under the assumption of these minimal limitations, VDOBC exhibits
potential for broader applications beyond UAVs. Its adaptability can be
extended to ground robots and unmanned vehicles, thereby enhancing
the robustness of these systems by preserving the adaptable traits of the
time-varying model.
