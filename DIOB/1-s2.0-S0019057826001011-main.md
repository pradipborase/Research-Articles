A B S T R A C T
This study proposes a novel neural network-based active disturbance rejection control (ADRC) scheme, enhanced 
with the adaptive moment estimation (ADAM) optimizer for precise trajectory tracking of quadrotor UAVs under 
disturbances. A cascaded ADRC architecture is designed for attitude control, featuring an inner loop that enables 
rapid attitude response and an outer loop that handles low-frequency disturbances and uncertainties. This study 
implements the online adaptive tuning of extended state observer (ESO) parameters via a radial basis function 
neural network (RBFNN), which dynamically adjusts observer gains as disturbances evolve. The integration of 
the ADAM optimizer accelerates RBFNN training compared to traditional backpropagation by leveraging gradient 
moment estimations for adaptive learning rates. This approach enables real-time rejection of time-varying disВӯ
turbances and eliminates the need for manual parameter recalibration. The theoretical stability of the proposed 
system is rigorously proven using Lyapunov analysis. Hardware-in-the-loop experiments validate the superior 
performance of the proposed scheme in three scenarios.
1 . Introduction
In recent years, quadrotor UAVs have gained widespread applicaВӯ
tion in both civilian and military fields due to their ability to hover 
freely and perform vertical takeoffs and landings. They are capable of 
performing various tasks in fields such as agriculture, rescue, inspecВӯ
tion, and aerial photography, which has attracted significant attention 
from researchers. A UAV is a six-degree-of-freedom system, including 
three position degrees of freedom and three attitude degrees of freeВӯ
dom. However, it has only four control inputs, making it a typical 
underactuated system. Its multivariable, nonlinear, and strongly couВӯ
pled nature, along with its sensitivity to disturbances, increases the 
difficulty of designing trajectory tracking controllers [1вҖ“3]. Traditional 
proportional-integral-derivative (PID) control was widely used initially 
in industrial applications [4,5]. However, when dealing with complex 
and nonlinear control systems, the performance of PID control may not 
meet expectations. In modern control theory, methods such as fuzzy 
control [6,7], sliding mode control (SMC) [8], and active disturbance 
rejection control have been proposed and widely applied [9,10]. Fuzzy

used for its excellent robustness and adaptability to uncertainties and

Active disturbance rejection control is a practical control technology 
proposed by Han [11] that does not rely on the precise mathematical 
model of the controlled object. The ADRC technique can compensate for 
the difficulty of solving nonlinear problems and reduce the dependence 
on accurate mathematical models. Various uncertainties such as interВӯ
nal and external disturbances in the controlled object are considered 
total disturbances. The extended state observer [12] enables real-time 
disturbance estimation and compensation, achieving active disturbance 
rejection. Due to the large number of control parameters of ADRC and 
the difficulty of manually adjusting the parameters, the particle swarm 
optimization method can adjust the parameters of ADRC to improve the 
dynamic performance of the controlled system [13]. Gao [14] linearized 
the main components of nonlinear ADRC and proposed linear active 
disturbance rejection control (LADRC). This approach simplifies state 
feedback for ESO design and parameter tuning issues, and simplifies the 
adjustment of controller bandwidth, observer bandwidth, and interferВӯ
ence compensation. Its contribution greatly simplifies the structure and 
parameterization of ADRC and facilitates engineering applications.
As a result, many researchers have applied ADRC to quadrotor UAV 
control systems [15вҖ“17]. Although ADRC can effectively suppress disВӯ
turbances, the original controller parameters may become unsuitable 
under the combined effects of load variations and wind disturbances. 
Specifically, the three main parameters in the linear extended state obВӯ
server (LESO) not only influence the performance of the controller but 
also affect its stability. Given their powerful nonlinear fitting capability, 
numerous studies have integrated neural networks with ESOs to enable 
adaptive adjustment of controller parameters. In [18], an RBFNN was 
employed for the online tuning of ADRC parameters, significantly enВӯ
hancing control precision. Building on this, [19] introduced a cascaded 
ADRC scheme that replaced the conventional ESO with an RBFNN-based 
observer, effectively tackling disturbance control challenges in multi-
input multi-output systems. This improved observer simplified ADRC 
parameter tuning while boosting the controllerвҖҷs adaptability. Further 
advancing this approach, [20] leveraged an adaptive RBF neural netВӯ
work to estimate unknown total disturbances, substantially improving 
the control systemвҖҷs robustness.
Although neural networks can achieve online parameter self-tuning 
for ADRC, there has been limited research on improving the online trainВӯ
ing process of neural networks. The training process of a neural network 
primarily involves updating the weights of neuron connections. Ensuring 
fast convergence of the network while avoiding local optima has alВӯ
ways been a focus of research. This study employs the ADAM optimizer 
[21,22] for online neural network training. Traditional RBF neural netВӯ
works [23] rely on backpropagation with a fixed learning rate, which 
restricts adaptability. This limitation leads to a slower online training 
speed for LADRC parameters, negatively affecting the systemвҖҷs stability 
and responsiveness. By combining the ADAM algorithm with the RBF 
neural network, the speed of network training can be improved. ADAM 
dynamically adapts per-parameter learning rates by leveraging both 
first-order and second-order moment estimates of the gradients. In [24], 
ADAM was combined with a neural network-based PID to enhance the 
tracking capability of a remotely controlled robot, demonstrating its poВӯ
tential to optimize neuro-controllers. Although ADAM has been widely 
applied in neural networks (NNs), its application in ADRC-RBFNN sysВӯ
tems remains limited. In this study, a neural network-based ADRC for 
quadrotor UAVs is proposed, utilizing ADAM for optimization. The main 
contributions of this study are summarized as follows:
1) Unlike related works in [25], the proposed controller is impleВӯ
mented in a cascaded form for attitude control. Cascaded LADRC 
employs a hierarchical structure (i.e., inner-loop and outer-loop 
control) to optimize control performance. The inner loop rapidly 
responds to attitude changes, while the outer loop effectively hanВӯ
dles low-frequency disturbances and uncertainties. This layered 
design enhances the systemвҖҷs response speed and stability.
2) The integration of the ADAM optimization algorithm with the 
RBF neural network and ADRC is an original contribution of this 
study. Comparative experiments validate the advantages of this 
approach.
3) The developed controller can update its parameters online withВӯ
out requiring complex computations or pre-training.
The remainder of this study is structured as follows. Section 2 introВӯ
duces the system model of the UAV with disturbances. Section 3 presents 
the design of ADRC for each channel and its stability proof. Section 4 
details the RBF neural network structure and its optimization using the 
ADAM algorithm. Section 5 presents the experimental results. Section 6 
provides the conclusion of this study.

6 . Conclusion
This paper proposes a novel active disturbance rejection control 
scheme, termed ADRC-RBF-ADAM, to address the challenge of high-
precision quadrotor UAV trajectory tracking under complex disturВӯ
bances. The core innovation lies in integrating the ADAM optimizer to 
accelerate the online tuning process of an RBF neural network. This 
approach effectively overcomes the critical bottlenecks of slow conВӯ
vergence and parameter oscillation inherent in traditional methods. 
Hardware-in-the-loop experiments provide compelling evidence that the 
proposed method not only doubles the convergence speed of the LESO 
parameters but also reduces trajectory tracking error by over 40% comВӯ
pared to other advanced controllers, such as SMC and fuzzy control. 
Although the scheme entails a higher computational cost, its superior 
disturbance rejection and tracking accuracy validate its effectiveness in 
complex dynamic environments. Future integration with model predicВӯ
tive control is a promising direction. The superior online learning rate 
and estimation precision of our RBF-ADAM method allow for the acВӯ
tive rejection of complex disturbances. This alleviates the dependency 
of model predictive control on high-fidelity models, thereby facilitating 
robust optimization on a simpler plant model.
