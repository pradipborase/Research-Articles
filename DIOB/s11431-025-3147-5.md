Abstract
A finite-time adaptive sliding mode controller is designed to improve the trajectory tracking capability of a 
quadrotor unmanned aerial vehicle (UAV) in the presence of model uncertainties and unmodeled external disturbances. By 
applying adaptive nonsingular fast terminal sliding mode control (ANFTSMC) to the quadrotor UAV system, rapid response, 
finite-time convergence, and high-precision steady-state control are achieved under uncertainties and external disturbances, 
while also avoiding singularities and reducing the effect of chattering. Furthermore, the adaptive strategy is designed to 
estimate the upper bounds of model uncertainties and external disturbances without requiring prior bounded information. The 
finite-time convergence capability of the system is rigorously analyzed using the Lyapunov stability framework. Extensive 
simulation studies and experimental validations collectively demonstrate the efficacy of the designed control scheme. Notably, 
stable flight in the presence of wind disturbances and control of the contact force in experiments with wind turbine blades 
underscore the applicability of the strategy for achieving reliable quadrotor UAV operations in complex environments.

Introduction  
Unmanned aerial vehicles (UAVs) are increasingly deployed 
in both civilian and military applications, owing to their 
adaptability, low operational costs, and proficiency in con­
ducting missions in high-risk environments where human 
presence is either unviable or poses significant dangers. 
Quadrotors have emerged as the most popular choice among 
various types of UAVs, given their simple mechanical 
structure, high maneuverability, and vertical takeoff and 
landing capabilities, which make them ideal for a wide range 
of applications, such as aerial surveillance [1], environ­
mental monitoring [2], remote sensing data collection [3], 
and structure inspection [4]. Nevertheless, quadrotor UAVs 
are complex systems because of their underactuated design 
and nonlinear, coupled dynamics. Moreover, environmental 
disturbances such as atmospheric turbulence and wind gusts, 
combined with dynamic parameter uncertainties, may lead to 
flight trajectory deviation or system instability [5,6]. 
Significant research efforts have been devoted to addres­
sing the challenges associated with trajectory tracking in 
quadrotor UAVs. Despite the efficacy of traditional linear 
control strategies, including proportional-integral-derivative 
(PID) and linear quadratic regulator (LQR) techniques, for

systems with linear dynamics, maintaining stability becomes 
problematic for quadrotors operating outside their designated 
domains, especially when tracking nonlinear target trajec­

monstrated efficacy in quadrotor applications, including 
backstepping-based approaches [8] and fuzzy logic control 
(FLC) [9]. However, these approaches often rely on accurate 
system models and lack adaptability in dynamic environ­
ments [10]. Controlling quadrotor UAVs under external 
disturbances is further complicated by challenges such as in- 
flight vibrations, strongly coupled nonlinear system dy­
namics, and unexpected external disturbances (e.g., wind 
gusts, environmental contact forces, or sudden load chan­
ges). Additionally, UAVs must be able to operate in real-time 
with rapid response capabilities [11]. Sliding mode control 
(SMC) is distinguished by its inherent disturbance rejection 
capabilities against nonlinear structures, unmodeled dy­
namics, and external perturbations, making it fairly effective 
in maintaining the stability of quadrotor UAVs across various 
operating conditions. The relatively straightforward design 
of SMC further facilitates its implementation in complex 
UAV dynamics [12,13]. 
To enhance the disturbance rejection capability of the 
system, a sufficiently large fixed switching gain has been 
employed in traditional SMC to account for the maximum 
possible magnitude of model uncertainties and external 
perturbations [14]. However, in real-world implementations, 
the bounds of uncertainties and disturbances cannot be pre­
determined [15,16]. Moreover, overestimating the control 
gain may lead to chattering phenomena, thereby adversely 
affecting the system performance [17]. Adaptive SMC 
(ASMC) has emerged as an alternative strategy for dyna­
mically tuning the control gain based on real-time estimation 
of uncertainties and external disturbances. Although ASMC 
designs reported in refs. [18,19] reduced chattering without 
requiring disturbance bounds, they still exhibited a slow 
perturbation response, limiting the convergence rate and 
steady-state accuracy. 
To improve the convergence rate and realize finite-time 
convergence, which are critical requirements for time-sen­
sitive quadrotor tasks, researchers developed terminal SMC 
(TSMC) by incorporating a terminal attractor-based ap­
proach [20]. Recent advances have integrated TSMC with 
complementary techniques to address specific challenges. 
For instance, Najafi et al. [21] and Alattas et al. [22] com­
bined barrier functions with finite-time SMC to handle state 
constraints and actuator faults, whereas the convergence 
speed was regulated using quantized predefined-time control 
[23]. However, these methods often produce conservative or 
aggressive control inputs, compromising the transient per­
formance, especially near constraint boundaries or pre­
defined settling times. Neural network-based approaches 
[24–26] have been developed to tackle complex un­
certainties, but their inherent approximation lag hinders 
timely compensation for fast-varying disturbances (e.g., 
sudden wind gusts), which are commonly encountered dur­
ing quadrotor operations. A prior study [27] demonstrated 
that TSMC coupled with adaptive mechanisms enables 
stable attitude control despite actuator saturation effects. 
Nevertheless, the performance of TSMC is degraded in the 
event of large deviations from equilibrium. To address this 
issue, fast terminal SMC (FTSMC) was developed, which 
accelerates the error convergence by employing supple­
mentary sliding surface terms. Despite this improvement, 
FTSMC faces singularity issues when states approach the 
origin. This has been resolved by optimizing the design of 
the sliding surface using nonsingular FTSMC (NFTSMC) 
[28]. Further, a previous study [29] introduced an adaptive 
NFTSMC (ANFTSMC) for robust quadrotor tracking. 
However, the adaptive parameters failed to decrease with 
diminishing disturbances, leading to overestimation [30]. 
Based on the aforementioned discussions, in this study, an 
ANFTSMC framework is developed for enhancing the tra­
jectory tracking of quadrotor UAVs in the event of para­
metric uncertainties and environmental disturbances. As the 
foundational work, a complete dynamical model accounting 
for both system modeling uncertainties and environmental 
interference factors is established. The detailed design of the 
proposed ANFTSMC is presented, in which a nonsingular 
fast terminal sliding surface is integrated with adaptive laws 
to compensate for external disturbances and system un­
certainties. It is demonstrated that the adaptive laws enable 
the sliding mode variable to converge to and remain within a 
bounded region of the sliding manifold in finite time. The 
proposed controller is compared with existing approaches 
via simulation studies, demonstrating both its theoretical 
validity and performance advantages. Finally, its favorable 
tracking performance is further demonstrated through com­
parative experiments under wind disturbances. The system is 
also successfully applied in controlling the contact force on 
wind turbine blades. The primary contributions of this study 
are as follows. 
(1) A finite-time adaptive control approach is proposed to 
achieve rapid response, finite-time convergence, and high- 
precision steady-state control of a quadrotor UAV with 
model uncertainties and external disturbances. 
(2) An adaptive strategy is designed to estimate the bounds 
of model uncertainties and external disturbances, without 
requiring prior knowledge of the bounds. 
(3) The effectiveness of the proposed control scheme is 
validated in real-world test flights of the quadrotor under 
wind disturbances and is further verified in scenarios in­
volving physical interaction and regulation of the contact 
force on a wind turbine blade.

6  
Conclusion  
This study presented an adaptive, nonsingular, fast terminal 
SMC (ANFTSMC) strategy for a quadrotor UAV, effectively 
addressing trajectory tracking challenges in the presence of 
model uncertainties and external disturbances. The proposed 
control law integrates the NFTSMC concept, ensuring finite- 
time convergence and avoiding singularities. By employing 
an adaptive parameter-tuning mechanism, the strategy 
eliminates the need for prior knowledge of uncertainty 
bounds and reduces chattering effects. The superiority of the 
control strategy was validated through extensive simulations 
and experimental comparisons under wind disturbance and 
was demonstrated in a force control application during 
contact with a wind turbine blade. The results reveal that the 
control method effectively achieves rapid convergence, 
minimizes steady-state error, and exhibits strong robustness, 
highlighting its advanced performance and practical applic­
ability in real-world scenarios. Based on the present work, 
future research will focus primarily on the following two 
aspects. First, the explicit integration of actuator saturation 
constraints into the control design will be pursued to enhance 
the robustness when operating at physical limits. Second, the 
framework will be extended to manage the full dynamics of 
aerial manipulators for more complex physical interaction 
tasks beyond simplified impedance control.
