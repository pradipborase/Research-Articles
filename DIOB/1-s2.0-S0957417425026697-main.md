Roll-pitch estimation algorithms are widely used in modern devices such as GNSS-aided inertial navigation sys-
tems (GNSS/INS), attitude and heading reference systems (AHRS), and inertial measurement units (IMU). These 
algorithms provide real-time data for communication and mechatronic closed-loop systems, where fast and high-
quality measurements are essential. This work proposes a strategy for achieving real-time, high-precision roll-
pitch estimations for land and air applications by employing sensor fusion algorithms and enhanced discrete 
generalized proportional-integral (GPI) observers. Compared to other state-of-the-art algorithms, our strategy 
demonstrated superior performance. Experimental results highlight the eп¬Җectiveness of recursiveness, active 
disturbance rejection (ADR), and optimal state estimation features. For rough environments, the algorithm is 
parametrized using a Bayesian Optimization strategy. The proposed algorithm can be utilized to generate input 
signals for onboard hard real-time systems, such as trajectory generators, target trackers, and stabilized platforms.

2.  The federated Kalman п¬Ғlter (FKF) for sensor fusion
Sensor fusion algorithms are essential in applications requiring high-
precision measurements. When a single sensor cannot provide a reli-
able response, a sensor array can generate multiple signals that can be 
weighted or averaged. In this context, two prominent approaches have 
been utilized: machine learning and Kalman п¬Ғltering. The latter is pre-
ferred for hard real-time applications due to its recursive nature, adapt-
ability, п¬Ӯexibility and fast convergence. Additionally, it does not require 
massive datasets or training steps for standard applications.
The п¬Ғlter proposed by Carlson in Carlson (1988b),Carlson (1988a) 
was designed to achieve a globally optimal estimation using measure-
ments from a sensor array. A set of independent Kalman п¬Ғlters processes 
data simultaneously, while a master п¬Ғlter aggregates the local results. 
If a sensor fails, only the corresponding local п¬Ғlter is aп¬Җected, making 
the algorithm fault-tolerant. Due to these features, this algorithm was 
named the Federated Kalman Filter (FKF).
In general, the Kalman п¬Ғlter approach is deterministic, recursive and 
easy to implement. However, the designer must have special careful 
when implementing vectors and matrixes operations. Nowadays sev-
eral software suits oп¬Җer rapid codiп¬Ғcation for several programming lan-
guages, leading to fast prototyping and easy systems integration.
In Muri et al. (2020), Harald et al. classiп¬Ғed sensor fusion conп¬Ғgu-
rations into three categories:
1. Cooperative conп¬Ғguration: All sensors monitor the same parameter 
of the physical process and are of the same type.
2. Complementary conп¬Ғguration: All sensors monitor the same param-
eter of the physical process but are of diп¬Җerent types.
3. Competitive conп¬Ғguration: Sensors monitor diп¬Җerent parameters of 
the same physical process.

3.  Discrete GPI observers (DGPIO)
In previous work (SГЎnchez-RamГӯrez et al., 2020), we presented a set 
of discrete GPI observers for visual tracking applications. By incorporat-
ing interacting multiple models (IMM) and active disturbance rejection 
features, we achieved high-precision estimations.
The basic DGPIO employs the Euler method for the discrete integral 
reconstructors. In this work, we modiп¬Ғed the integration strategy by 
replacing the Euler method with the well-known trapezoidal rule. By 
utilizing a more accurate numerical integration method, we improved 
the performance of the integral reconstructors and, consequently, the 
precision of the DGPIO. However, this modiп¬Ғcation altered the observer 
dynamics, potentially leading to system instability. As a result, a new 
proof of convergence and stability was necessary.
Before introducing the improved algorithm, a fundamental second-

8.2.  Rough environments
In real world scenarios, unexpected disturbances can cause signif-
icant issues, including oscillations in acquisition signals, sensor satu-
ration, and even sensor failure. These disturbances often arise from 
electromagnetic interference, extreme weather conditions, or undesired 
high-frequency vibration sources.
While electrical problems can typically be resolved through rigorous 
hardware testingвҖ“such as industrial or military certiп¬ҒcationsвҖ“addressing 
signal processing challenges requires careful optimization and tuning of 
various algorithms.
In this section, we focus on the problem of hard-real-time multi-
sensor fusion under harsh environmental conditions. To achieve robust 
performance, we propose using modern Bayesian optimization tech-
niques for the automatic tuning of the improved discrete GPI observers. 
In our experiments, a controlled vibration tester generated oscillations 
in multiple axes with frequencies ranging from 10 to 100 Hz. Subse-
quently, we tuned the DGPIO gains (see Appendix A) and applied them 
in challenging real-world scenarios: a drone maneuvering at high alti-
tude under strong wind conditions and a unmmaned surface vehicle on 
the sea.
8.2.1.  Autotuning in rough environment using Bayesian optimization.
A real-time autotuning procedure involves setting up to a controlled 
environment where resource consumption can be monitored. While ex-
periments provide useful information for the learning model, they also 
place stress on the hardware, and multiple iterations can be costly.
In this subsection, we describe the real-time autotuning process used 
to optimize the FKF-IDGPIO algorithm. The primary goal is to train a 
learning model and determine the optimal the best parameters for state 
estimation under extreme conditions.
A typical rough environment for orientation measurement includes 
undesired vibrations and high-speed maneuvers. These issues can lead 
to sensor saturation, reading errors, inaccuracies and the ampliп¬Ғcation 
of unwanted signals.
To replicate a similar scenario, we used the LDS device shown in 
Fig. 24. This device is used in the automotive industry to test a wide 
variety of components against persistent vibrations.
The hardware setup is shown in Fig. 25 which consists of the LDS 
oscillator, air compressor, electric ampliп¬Ғer, power supply and conп¬Ғgu-
ration software. We generated oscillations along several axes using fre-
quencies in the range of 10 to 100 Hz. In each iteration, signals were col-
lected for 40 s, which is a reasonable period of time to let Kalman п¬Ғlters 
and DGPIOвҖҷs to converge. The complete learning experiment consisted 
of 40 iterations, lasting half an hour. Figs. 26 and 27 present examples 
of generated vibrations for roll and pitch angles, respectively. Here we 
compare the disturbed outputs of the sensor array with the outputs of 
the high precision reference sensor. This controlled experiment demon-
strates how high-frequency vibrations introduce undesired noisy signals 
in the sensor array, while optimal state observers can eп¬Җectively miti-
gate these disturbances.
Table 6 presents the experimental results of the real time autotun-
ing procedure. We display the Bayesian Optimization results of the last 
10 iterations. For the FKF-DGPIO algorithm, the best results for the ob-
jective function was found in the 39-th iteration. For the FKF-IDGPIO 
algorithm, the best results were obtained in the 36-th iteration. As ex-

9.  Conclusion
In this work, we presented an onboard high-precision roll-pitch es-
timation algorithm for hard real-time applications in land, air and mar-
itime vehicles. Our proposed strategy integrates two key state estima-
tion designs: a new discrete GPI observer based on the trapezoidal rule 
for numerical integration and a federated Kalman п¬Ғlter with an active 
disturbance rejection feature.
We enhanced the precision of the discrete GPI observer by employing 
a more accurate numerical integration method. However, this improve-
ment altered the dynamics of the observers, requiring us to establish 
a new proof of convergence and stability. Subsequently, a set of im-
proved DGPIOs was incorporated into a federated structure to achieve 
high-precision global estimations.
To validate the convergence of the algorithms and parameterization, 
we developed a low-latency scenario simulating a sinusoidal vehicle 
pitch motion. Numerical results demonstrated the eп¬Җectiveness of the 
proposed strategy.
For experimental validation, we developed a portable, real-time roll-
pitch estimation prototype consisting of three devices for sensor fusion 
and a reference sensor for comparative analysis. The prototype was 
tested in two typical real-world scenarios: driving a land vehicle on a 
rough road and controlling a high-speed maneuvering drone.
In both scenarios, the algorithms behavior varied according to the 
angular velocity of the vehicle. Generally, the KF algorithm exhibited 
signiп¬Ғcant oп¬Җsets and frequent error overshoots. The FKF and FKF-
DGPIO algorithms showed reduced oп¬Җset levels but still experienced 
several error overshoots. The proposed FKF-IDGPIO strategy delivered 
the best results, with minimal oп¬Җset values and low-amplitude error 
overshoots. To validate these п¬Ғndings, root mean square errors were 
evaluated during the experiments.
To expand the research, we improved the proposed algorithm us-
ing state-of-the-art machine learning algorithms. We implemented a 
Bayesian Optimization strategy to automatically tune the gains of DG-
PIOвҖҷs in a disturbed, but controlled scenario. For comparison study, the 
proposed algorithm was compared with other state-of-the-art sensor fu-
sion strategies. As a п¬Ғnal test, we used the optimized algorithms in two 
harsh real-world scenarios: a drone maneuvering at high altitude and 
an unmmaned surface vehicle on the sea. These experimentes validated 
the eп¬Җectiveness of the proposed method.
In summary, the proposed strategy demonstrated three critical char-
acteristics: recursiveness for low latency and computational eп¬ғciency, 
high-precision integral reconstructors for the discrete GPI observers, and 
sensor fusion for optimal state estimation. This approach is well-suited 
for onboard hard real-time applications requiring high-precision esti-
mations, such as stabilized platforms, target trackers, guided weapons, 
GNSS-aided inertial navigation systems, and other modern systems.
Further research is focused to explore more complicated scenarios 
such as high-speed maneuvering aircrafts or missiles, roll-pitch car dy-
namics during catastrophic events and submarine environments. Novel 
research in sensor fusion using artiп¬Ғcial intelligence has to be consid-
ered to improve the performance.
