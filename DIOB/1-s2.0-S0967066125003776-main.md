A B S T R A C T
Unmanned aerial vehicles (UAVs) inspecting hydropower penstocks face significant stability challenges due to 
complex wind fields, including mean winds, shear effects, and turbulence. This paper proposes a Multimodal 
Adaptive Anti-Disturbance Composite Control (MAADC) framework to enhance trajectory tracking accuracy 
and disturbance rejection in such environments. The MAADC integrates three core innovations: (1) a 
Multimodal Adaptive State Compensation Observer (MASCO) for real-time wind disturbance estimation using 
dual adaptive mechanisms, (2) a Frequency-Domain Wind Disturbance Decomposer (FDWDD) to decouple 
low- and high-frequency wind components via spectral separation, and (3) a Fast Nonsingular Terminal 
Sliding Mode Controller (FNTSMC) combining feedforward compensation and adaptive feedback for robust 
tracking. Experiments in both indoor pipelines and actual hydropower penstocks validate MAADC’s superiority 
over existing methods. Results show a 58.2% reduction in position RMSE compared to ADRC and 63.9% 
improvement over AFONFTSMC in turbulent conditions. The framework effectively mitigates wind-induced 
deviations, achieving sub-0.17 m RMSE in trajectory tracking under dynamic wind shear and turbulence, 
proving its viability for industrial inspection applications.

considered is the inspection of penstocks, which are a critical com-

their nature, are susceptible to defects such as cracks, corrosion, and 
rust during long-term operation, making routine inspection an essential 
part of hydropower system maintenance. However, traditional manual 
inspection methods remain inefficient and pose considerable safety 
risks. Owing to their agility and high-resolution sensing capabilities,

1.2. Review of related works
To address these challenges, a number of research studies have pro-
posed various control methods for UAVs operating in wind-affected in-
dustrial environments. These methods can be broadly classified into dis-
turbance observer-based methods, robust control strategies, and adap-
tive learning-based approaches. Each category offers specific advan-
tages, together with a number of limitations, depending on the distur-
bance types and environmental constraints.
1.2.1. Disturbance estimation and observer-based methods
Observer-based techniques have become fundamental in address-
ing aerodynamic uncertainties encountered with UAVs. The extended 
state observer (ESO), pioneered by Han (Jingqing, 1995), revolution-
ized disturbance estimation by treating unmodeled dynamics and ex-
ternal perturbations as augmented states. Chen, Ballance, Gawthrop, 
and O’Reilly (2000) further refined this approach for robotic systems, 
enabling decoupled estimation of inertial and external disturbances. 
Recent advancements have focused on improving convergence speed 
and adaptability. For instance, Wang, He, Zhou, Gao, and Zong (2024) 
proposed a finite-time event-triggered observer for quadrotor forma-
tions. However, such methods often assume slow-varying disturbances 
and exhibit degraded performance under high-frequency turbulence, as 
highlighted in the research study by Sun, Deng, Guo, Wu, and You 
(2024), where persistent chattering was observed in vertical take-off 
and landing (VTOL) UAVs under dynamic airflow. Model predictive 
control (MPC) strategies, such as that proposed by Zhang, Yuan, and 
Chin (2018), leverage airflow dynamics for wind tunnel regulation but 
require precise flow models, which presents a limitation in that these 
strategies cannot be used reliably on semi-enclosed penstocks, where 
empirical turbulence spectra deviate from theoretical predictions.
1.2.2. Robust control strategies
Sliding mode control (SMC) and its variants remain central to robust 
UAV control owing to their inherent insensitivity to matched uncer-
tainties. For example, Jesionowski, Shtessel, and Plestan (2023) pro-
posed adaptive switching SMC to suppress chattering, whereas Nguyen, 
Oh, and Moon (2022) integrated nonsingular terminal SMC with inte-
gral sliding surfaces to ensure finite-time convergence. However, these 
methods often have not been validated in environments with com-
pound disturbances. In roll channels, Ghadiri, Khodadadi, and Hazareh 
(2023) observed up to 22% overshoot caused by rotor wake torque 
delay, demonstrating the limitations of traditional designs under tightly 
coupled force–torque dynamics.
Fortunately, recent extensions have improved the performance of 
SMCs in specific aspects. For example, disturbance observer-based fast 
terminal SMC (Sun et al., 2025) effectively compensates for actua-
tor faults and bounded disturbances, whereas adaptive backstepping 
SMC (Nettari, Kurt, & Labbadi, 2022) handles parametric uncertainties 
with reduced chattering. Similarly, adaptive nonsingular fast termi-
nal SMC (Mechali, Xu, Huang, Shi, & Xie, 2021) achieves accurate 
tracking via online estimation. However, these methods were typically 
developed under simplified conditions and have not accounted for 
penstock-specific phenomena such as chimney-effect—induced airflow 
or complex wind shear patterns. Adaptive SMC with input saturation 
compensation (Shao, Sun, Yao, Liu, & Wu, 2022) can reduce track-
ing error by over 30%, yet ignores the interaction between actuator 
limits and multimodal disturbances, which is crucial to account for in 
narrow-space, low-speed inspection scenarios.
Although robust controllers offer strong theoretical guarantees, 
they often fail to address the full spectrum of multimodal wind dis-
turbances in confined, turbulent environments such as hydropower 
penstocks. This gap highlights the need for hierarchical and multi-
modal disturbance-aware control architectures, which is explored in 
the derivation of the proposed MAADC framework.
1.2.3. Adaptive and learning-based approaches
To address the complex aerodynamic uncertainties encountered 
with UAVs, several research studies have explored the use of adaptive 
and learning-based controllers. Among them, neural network (NN)-
based adaptive control schemes provide strong approximation capabil-
ities and online adaptability. Double-loop architectures incorporating 
adaptive NNs in both position and attitude channels (Lopez-Sanchez, 
Perez-Alcocer, & Moreno-Valenzuela, 2023) eliminate the need for 
offline training and show robustness against parametric uncertainty. 
Recurrent NN-based controllers (Yogi, Tripathi, & Behera, 2021) further 
enhance finite-time convergence through improved dynamic modeling.
Hybrid NN-sliding mode designs (Li, Chen, Qi, & Peng, 2025; Razmi 
& Afshinfar, 2019) offer disturbance rejection while preserving stabil-
ity under bounded uncertainties. To handle actuator faults and high-
frequency turbulence, disturbance observers combined with finite-time 
backstepping and command filters (ning Li, Chen, & Qi, 2025) have 
been proposed. These schemes offer improved tracking performance. 
However, they often assume open or lightly confined operating condi-
tions and are thus limited in terms of their practical applicability.
Reinforcement learning (RL)-based approaches aim to adapt control 
policies for dynamic environments. Actor–critic RL frameworks (Cai, 
Yang, Huang, & Li, 2025) and policy-relief methods (Ma et al., 2023) 
improve adaptability under external disturbances; however, they re-
quire large datasets and careful reward design. Neural-Fly (O’Connell 
et al., 2022) has demonstrated rapid online adaptation via meta-learned 
wind representations but relies on prior wind tunnel training, limiting 
its transferability to unstructured spaces such as penstocks.
Recent work has also introduced active wind rejection strategies
(Xing, Zhang, & Su, 2023) and event-triggered adaptive NN con-
trol (Wu, Chen, Li, & Chadli, 2024), focusing on resource-efficient adap-
tation and unknown wind compensation. However, most methods lack 
explicit modeling of the multimodal, spatially coupled disturbances that 
occur in semi-enclosed environments.
In summary, adaptive and learning-based methods offer promis-
ing solutions to compensate for uncertain, time-varying disturbances 
in UAV systems. However, most existing approaches either assume 
open-air conditions or focus on single-mode disturbances. Their ap-
plicability to complex, multimodal, semi-enclosed environments such 
as hydropower penstocks remains underexplored, necessitating frame-
works that explicitly consider spatial–temporal wind heterogeneity and 
dynamic coupling. This work is thus conducted specifically to address 
these challenges.
1.2.4. Proposed method and contribution
This paper proposes a Multimodal Adaptive Anti-Disturbance Com-
posite Control (MAADC) framework for UAV trajectory tracking in 
hydropower penstock inspections, where complex and confined wind 
fields pose significant challenges. Its main contributions are as follows:
• 1. Task-Oriented Disturbance Modeling and Decomposition:
A dynamic model of UAVs in semi-enclosed penstock environ-
ments is developed, capturing multimodal aerodynamic distur-
bances, including mean wind, wind shear, and turbulence. A 
Frequency-Domain Wind Disturbance Decomposition (FDWDD) 
strategy is introduced to separate and compensate for low- and 
high-frequency components via adaptive filtering.
• 2. Dual Adaptive Observer for Real-Time Wind Estimation: A 
Multimodal Adaptive State Compensation Observer (MASCO) is 
designed using cascaded error propagation and dual gain adapta-
tion. It enables accurate and real-time estimation of spatially cou-
pled disturbances without relying on slow-varying assumptions, 
significantly improving robustness in turbulent flows.
• 3. Hierarchical Composite Control with Field Validation: A 
feedforward-feedback composite controller based on Fast Nonsin-
gular Terminal Sliding Mode Control (FNTSMC) is integrated with 
the observer and decomposition modules. The complete system is 
validated on a field-tested UAV platform, achieving up to 74.6% 
improvement in tracking accuracy over benchmark controllers in 
indoor and real penstock environments.

2. Multimodal adaptive anti-disturbance composite control
In this work, a Multimodal Adaptive Anti-Disturbance Compos-
ite Control (MAADC) framework is developed to address the unique 
aerodynamic challenges of UAV operation in hydropower penstock 
inspections. Here, multimodality refers to the coexistence of distinct 
aerodynamic phenomena that manifest at both the environmental and 
control-response levels. With regard to the environment in which the 
UAVs are intended to operate, the enclosed and curved geometry of 
the penstock induces interacting wind field modes, including low-
frequency mean flows driven by chimney-effect convection, spatially 
varying shear arising from curvature and structural transitions, and 
high-frequency turbulence generated by rotor downwash and wall-flow 
interactions. From a control perspective, these external aerodynamic 
perturbations are decomposed into slowly varying components, such 
as gradual airflow shifts due to boundary-layer friction, and rapidly 
varying impulsive components, such as turbulence generated at elbows 
or by rotor-induced vortices. This dual-layer interpretation of multi-
modality underpins the MAADC design, enabling the control law to 
allocate disturbance rejection mechanisms according to the spectral 
characteristics of the encountered wind field.
This decomposition enables targeted compensation and robust con-
trol under mixed disturbance conditions.
The overall MAADC architecture (Fig. 3) comprises four tightly 
integrated modules:
1. Transition Reference Model (TRM): generates smooth refer-
ence trajectories with consistent position, velocity, and acceler-
ation profiles;
2. Multimodal

modulated based on decomposed disturbance characteristics.
To cope with the compound aerodynamic environment of
hydropower penstock inspections, the proposed control framework 
adopts a mission-oriented strategy that explicitly links disturbance 
characteristics to stage-specific control objectives. For sustained mean 
winds, the reference trajectory and inspection velocity are pre-adjusted 
to reduce steady offset loads; for spatially varying wind shear, transi-
tional acceleration limits are applied in trajectory generation to prevent 
abrupt attitude changes; for high-frequency turbulence, control band-
width and gain parameters are adaptively tuned to maximize rejection 
capability while suppressing chattering. This hierarchical planning-
control alignment ensures that each disturbance mode is addressed 
through the most effective countermeasure at the corresponding mis-
sion phase.

Algorithmically, the MAADC architecture achieves this through 
tightly coupled functional modules: TRM generates dynamically smooth 
position–velocity–acceleration profiles resilient to mean-flow bias;
MASCO delivers real-time estimation of total aerodynamic disturbances 
using cascaded error propagation and dual adaptive gains; FDWDD 
isolates low-frequency components for feedforward compensation and 
high-frequency components for robust feedback suppression; and
FNTSMC integrates both inputs to achieve finite-time convergence and 
minimal residual oscillations. The coordinated operation of these mod-
ules ensures that spatially and temporally coupled wind disturbances 
are decomposed, compensated for, and attenuated in real time, thereby 
sustaining high-precision trajectory tracking in confined, disturbance-
rich environments. Subsequent sections detail the modeling, observer 
design, decomposition strategy, and control implementation of the 
MAADC framework.

3. Flight experiments
(1) To experimentally validate the proposed MAADC framework, 
flight tests were conducted in a constructed indoor pipeline. The indoor 
duct exhibits a chimney effect, i.e., a mean wind field, due to the

direction. Turbulence is caused by disturbance from the propeller of the 
UAV, and the environment simulates the wind field inside the penstock

actual hydropower plant. In accordance with the inspection task, the 
UAV flew along the centerline of the penstock from the lower horizontal 
section near the spiral case to the upper horizontal section near the 
large gate.

3.4.3. Discussion of experimental results
Based on the experimental data and results, the MAADC method 
demonstrates excellent wind disturbance estimation capabilities, par-
ticularly in handling complex wind field disturbances. A comparison 
of MASCO and LESO in terms of their position and velocity estima-
tion errors in the X- and Z-directions shows that MASCO significantly 
reduces the errors induced by wind disturbances, especially under high-
frequency disturbances and unstable wind fields. The FDWDD module 
successfully decomposes wind disturbances into low-frequency and 
high-frequency components, providing MASCO with further advantage 
in compensating for low-frequency disturbances.
With regard to the controller design, MAADC utilizes a TRM to 
smooth the step response and incorporates an FDWDD module to 
decompose the wind disturbance, ensuring smooth tracking of the 
desired trajectory. Although the step size for the desired target was 
set to 0.1, the response does not exhibit significant jaggedness, and 
the TRM effectively transforms it into a smooth trajectory, significantly 
improving the tracking accuracy of the proposed framework.
Moreover, MAADC performs exceptionally well in estimating low-
frequency disturbances. The experimental results show that the
FNTSMC controller compensates effectively for low-frequency distur-
bances, which is crucial for the stability and accuracy of the drone 
in the presence of wind disturbances. MAADC provides more ac-
curate estimation and compensation for wind disturbances during 
flight, demonstrating its practical potential for industrial applications, 
particularly in environments like hydropower stations.
The MAADC framework proves robust in industrial UAV applica-
tions under complex wind disturbances. Key outcomes include:
State Estimation: MASCO reduces position and velocity RMSE by 
up to 95.5% compared to those of LESO.
Disturbance Rejection: Frequency-domain decomposition enables 
targeted LF/HF compensation, which is critical for stable tracking.
Control Precision: The proposed method achieves 63.9–81.4% re-
duction in RMSE compared to benchmarks (FNTSMC, ADRC-ESO).
These advancements underscore the value of integrating adaptive 
observation with hierarchical disturbance mitigation. In future research 
studies, the computational efficiency of the framework will be opti-
mized, and further validations will be performed in multirotor swarm 
scenarios.

bances, including mean wind, spatial wind shear, and high-frequency 
turbulence. A Multimodal Adaptive Anti-Disturbance Composite Con-
trol (MAADC) framework was proposed, integrating mission-
level strategies, i.e., adjusting reference paths, acceleration limits, 
and control gains according to disturbance modes, with algorithmic 
modules comprising a Transition Reference Model (TRM), Multimodal 
Adaptive State Compensation Observer (MASCO), Frequency-Domain 
Wind Disturbance Decomposer (FDWDD), and Fast Nonsingular Termi-
nal Sliding Mode Controller (FNTSMC). Indoor and field experiments 
showed that MASCO reduced position estimation RMSEs by 95.5% 
compared to those of conventional ESO, and the complete MAADC im-
proved trajectory tracking accuracy by up to 74.6% over benchmarks, 
ensuring robust and accurate flight under varied wind conditions. 
The framework provides a systematic solution for UAV operations in 
confined, disturbance-rich environments. Future work will be focusing 
on additional disturbance scenarios such as actuator faults, center-of-
gravity shifts, and structural vibration effects, and broader applications 
to autonomous inspection tasks.
