Robust trajectory maneuvering of 
VTOL aircraft using higher-order 
sliding mode control (HOSMC)
Idriss Dagal1 & Bilal Erol2,3
Vertical Takeoff and Landing (VTOL) aircraft present significant control challenges due to their 
nonlinear, strongly coupled dynamics and sensitivity to disturbances, particularly during hover–
forward flight transitions. This study focuses on developing a Higher-Order Sliding Mode Control 
(HOSMC) strategy, based on the Super-Twisting algorithm, to enhance trajectory tracking accuracy and 
robustness for small-scale VTOL platforms. The proposed method was designed with tailored sliding 
variables and refined gain tuning, and its performance was validated through extensive simulations 
under realistic urban flight conditions, including Dryden-model turbulence, thrust perturbations, 
and multi-phase trajectories. Results show that HOSMC achieved superior tracking performance, 
with a maximum error of 0.038 m and an RMS error of 0.017 m, representing a 61% improvement in 
RMS error over SMC and a 50% reduction in maximum error compared to ASMC, while also delivering 
smoother control inputs, a thrust-to-weight ratio of 0.17, and energy consumption as low as 0.1866 
kWh/h. These findings confirm that HOSMC not only suppresses chattering but also improves energy 
efficiency by up to 20% compared to PID, underscoring its potential as a robust and efficient control 
solution for autonomous VTOL operations in urban air mobility (UAM) applications.

Literature review
 Vertical Takeoff and Landing (VTOL) aircraft combine the benefits of both helicopters and fixed-wing aircraft, 
providing versatile capabilities such as airborne hovering and the ability to take off and land vertically[1,2]. This 
unique functionality has sparked significant research interest in the control systems for VTOL aircraft. Stability 
and tracking controls are particularly critical for VTOL systems, as they ensure safe and efficient operation 
during various phases of flight, especially in constrained environments3. In recent years, numerous studies 
have focused on enhancing the stability and performance of VTOL aircraf4t. For example, in5, a linearization 
technique was developed to address the zero dynamic instability problem, while6 proposed a method for global 
configuration stabilization, emphasizing strong input coupling for VTOL aircraft systems. Additionally7,8,9, 
utilized the backstepping method to achieve global output asymptotic tracking by employing observers to 
estimate the velocity signals. The application of robust control algorithms has also been explored, especially in the 
context of mitigating challenges such as crosswind effects on VTOL aircraft, as seen in,101112. examined different 
algorithms for planning feasible and energy-efficient paths, while13 introduced a special saturation technique 
to improve the stabilization of VTOL systems. A key development in VTOL control is the implementation of 
Sliding Mode Control (SMC), which has been used extensively for trajectory tracking in nonlinear and under-
actuated systems14. demonstrates the use of SMC for trajectory tracking in VTOL aircraft, particularly when 
input saturation is a challenge. However, despite its advantages, SMC is known for its susceptibility to chattering, 
a phenomenon caused by system nonlinearity and dead zone issues. VTOL aircraft are classified as strongly 
coupled, under-actuated, and non-minimum phase systems, which makes them inherently difficult to control15. 
outlines the challenges of controlling these systems and highlights the need for advanced control methods 
to handle their complex dynamics. The urgency for advanced control solutions is underscored by the rapid 
growth of the Urban Air Mobility (UAM) sector, which projects a need for tens of thousands of eVTOL aircraft, 
placing a premium on safety and autonomous reliability16. This expansion is driving parallel advancements in 
critical infrastructure, such as vertiport development and air traffic management systems, which require highly 
predictable and robust vehicle behavior17. In response, recent research has focused on adaptive and intelligent 
control paradigms. For instance, studies have proposed nonlinear adaptive control schemes to maintain stability 
under parametric uncertainties and actuator faults18, while unified neural network-based controllers have 
been developed to efficiently manage complex flight dynamics across different modes of tilt-rotor VTOLs19. 
Concurrently, integrated sensing technologies, such as micro-Doppler analysis, are being refined to provide 
precise real-time flight state identification, which is crucial for advanced navigation and fault detection20. 
These developments highlight a critical research gap in achieving real-time computational efficiency without 
compromising robust performance under dynamic flight conditions and external disturbances21. This challenge 
is further amplified by the operational realities of the burgeoning “low-altitude economy” and the need to 
manage energy consumption effectively, given that current battery technology remains a key limiting factor 
for mission duration22,23. The ongoing evolution in propulsion systems, including hybrid-electric models, also 
introduces new dynamic characteristics that control systems must accommodate24. Over the years, various 
control techniques have been proposed to overcome the challenges posed by VTOL aircraft, particularly in terms 
of stability, trajectory tracking, and energy efficiency. Despite the progress, issues like chattering and system 
coupling continue to drive ongoing research in this field. The development of Vertical Takeoff and Landing 
(VTOL) aircraft has gained significant attention due to their ability to take off and land vertically, making 
them highly suitable for operations in constrained environments such as urban areas and emergency response 
scenarios. These low-scale VTOL aircraft are increasingly being considered for both civilian and military 
applications, including autonomous aerial vehicles (UAVs), surveillance systems, and cargo transport. However, 
maneuvering these aircraft through complex flight trajectories, while maintaining stability and precision, 
presents significant challenges due to their highly nonlinear dynamics and sensitivity to external disturbances. 
Traditional control strategies, such as Proportional-Integral-Derivative (PID) and Linear Quadratic Regulator 
(LQR), have been widely used for stabilizing and controlling aircraft flight. While these methods can provide 
satisfactory performance under ideal conditions, they often struggle to handle the uncertainties and non-
linearity inherent in the system dynamics of VTOL aircraft, particularly during aggressive maneuvers and in 
the presence of external disturbances. Sliding Mode Control (SMC) has emerged as a promising solution for 
controlling non-linear systems due to its robustness against model uncertainties, external disturbances, and 
parameter variations. By switching between different control modes based on the system state, SMC ensures 
that the system trajectories adhere closely to the desired paths, even in the presence of uncertainties. This paper 
proposes an SMC-based approach for the trajectory maneuvering of VTOL low-scale aircraft, aiming to enhance 
both the stability and accuracy of flight trajectory tracking. The objective of this study is to develop and validate 
a Sliding Mode Control strategy for precise trajectory maneuvering of low-scale VTOL aircraft. We investigate 
the potential benefits of SMC in terms of robustness, system performance, and adaptability in dynamic flight 
conditions. The proposed control methodology is evaluated using simulations, demonstrating its effectiveness 
in handling complex flight trajectories while maintaining stability, robustness, and performance under varying 
operational conditions.

Scopes and motivation
Building upon the aforementioned research, the Sliding Mode Controller (SMC) presents significant potential for 
controlling dynamic systems, particularly in VTOL low-scale aircraft. SMC is renowned for its robustness, ease 
of implementation, quick dynamic response, order reduction, stability, and capability to achieve ideal motion 
under uncertain conditions. These advantages make it an ideal method for handling complex dynamic systems, 
particularly those with inherent nonlinearity, such as aircraft flight dynamics. However, the inherent drawback 
of chattering in SMC has drawn considerable attention and effort toward its mitigation. Despite this, the SMC 
remains highly regarded due to its resilience to external disturbances, system nonlinearities, and modeling 
errors, making it suitable for controlling flight systems during critical phases such as takeoff and landing. In the 
context of VTOL aircraft, the flight dynamics are particularly challenging due to the nonlinear nature of their 
motion. The need for efficient control techniques to ensure stability and precise maneuvering is paramount. 
During takeoff and landing, the accuracy of flight performance heavily depends on piloting techniques and 
ground conditions. Thus, it is crucial to develop advanced control strategies capable of reducing takeoff and 
landing ground roll distance while ensuring that the aircraft maintains a controlled and safe trajectory. This 
research seeks to explore and enhance the applicability of SMC for controlling the flight trajectory of VTOL 
low-scale aircraft, with a focus on overcoming the challenges posed by chattering and optimizing the overall 
performance during takeoff and landing maneuvers. The goal is to develop an efficient, robust, and reliable 
control system that ensures safe, precise, and optimized flight operations in real-world conditions.

tracking performance. To address stability29, proposed a sliding mode control approach based on a high-gain 
observer technique, focusing on achieving exponential stability solutions for VTOL aircraft tracking systems. 
This approach ensures robust performance in the presence of uncertainties and external disturbances. For 
enhancing quadrotor performance30, proposed a simple yet effective method to plan the actions of each variable 
controller in the system. Their work emphasized the challenges associated with implementing control systems

Description of the aircraft
Motion plays a crucial role in pilot perception, often leading to disorientation and impacting performance, 
particularly during critical phases of flight. This is especially true for Vertical Take-Off and Landing (VTOL) 
aircraft, where unique accelerations and dynamic responses can challenge a pilot’s situational awareness. The 
VTOL TurboSquid, as depicted in Fig. 1, is designed to address this challenge, serving as both an advanced 
training platform and a highly capable operational aircraft. For training, the Turbosquid’s stable hover, precise 
controls, and predictable responses during vertical and horizontal maneuvers provide an ideal environment for 
developing essential piloting skills. Trainees gain proficiency in flight handling, mastering the critical transition 
between vertical and forward flight, and navigating complex environments. By experiencing realistic motion 
cues within the TurboSquid, pilots develop a deep understanding of the aircraft’s dynamics, ensuring a smoother 
transition to real-world operations and enhancing their ability to handle unexpected situations. Beyond its 
training applications, VTOL Turbosquid’s inherent VTOL capabilities make it a versatile operational platform. 
Its ability to take off and land vertically from confined spaces, such as rooftops or urban landing pads, opens 
up new possibilities for urban air mobility, rapid response emergency services, and other specialized missions. 
The aircraft’s advanced control systems and robust design ensure stable flight and precise maneuvering, even 
in challenging weather conditions or complex terrain. Whether training the next generation of VTOL pilots or 
executing critical real-world missions, the TurboSquid represents a significant advancement in modern aviation.
The VTOL TurboSquid is a lightweight, high-performance vertical take-off and landing aircraft designed for 
operations in compact and unconventional spaces. The TurboSquid achieves exceptional maneuverability and 
stable hover capabilities by utilizing a distributed electric propulsion system with eight ducted fans. Its advanced 
fly-by-wire control system and integrated GPS/INS navigation enable precise flight control and autonomous flight 
modes, making it a popular choice for urban air mobility, rescue missions, and other specialized applications. 
The TurboSquid offers superior performance and efficiency, constructed from lightweight composite materials 
and featuring an aerodynamically optimized airframe. Its ability to take off and land vertically allows it to 
operate in regions with minimal infrastructure or challenging terrain, enabling missions in areas inaccessible to 
conventional fixed-wing designs. It is one of the most promising platforms for next-generation aviation needs 
worldwide.
The VTOL TurboSquid exhibits exceptional takeoff, climbing, and landing performance, enabling operations 
in diverse and challenging environments. During takeoff, its eight electric ducted fans provide a thrust-to-weight 
ratio exceeding 1.2, allowing for rapid vertical ascent at a rate of 6.5 m/s. The advanced flight control system 
compensates for the ground effect and ensures stability during the initial phase of takeoff. The transition to

Higher-order sliding mode controller (HOSMC) design
Advantages of HOSM in VTOL control
The advantages of HOSMC in VTOL control include precision tracking, which ensures high accuracy even 
under disturbances and uncertainties; chattering mitigation, where smooth control inputs reduce chattering 
and actuator wear; robustness, as the method is inherently resilient to matched uncertainties; and finite-time 
convergence, which guarantees rapid convergence to the desired trajectory.
Application to VTOL performance
The proposed HOSMC framework ensures precise trajectory tracking even under disturbances or model 
uncertainties, while its smooth control inputs mitigate chattering, thereby reducing actuator wear and noise. 
It is inherently robust to uncertainties and external disturbances, and its applicability extends to VTOLs with 
complex dynamics, such as high-speed transitions between hover and forward flight. In addition, it enables 
smooth vertical transitions during takeoff and landing, provides robust hovering stability in the presence of wind 
gusts, and achieves accurate path tracking during forward flight and complex maneuvers.
