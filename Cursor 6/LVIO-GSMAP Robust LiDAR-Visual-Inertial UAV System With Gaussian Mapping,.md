Abstract—This paper presents a robust SLAM framework
that integrates LiDAR-Visual-Inertial Odometry (LVIO) with
3D Gaussian Splatting (3DGS) to address critical challenges
in outdoor environments, including photometric degradation,
weak textures, and motion blur. Traditional LiDAR-Visual-
Inertial Odometry systems struggle under these conditions, while
existing 3DGS-based SLAM methods are limited to indoor scenes
due to their reliance on dense RGB-D inputs. Our approach
introduces two key innovations: First, a state estimation pipeline
that transitions entirely to LiDAR-Inertial Odometry (LIO)
when photometric reliability drops, leveraging LIVO architecture
to achieve millimeter-level accuracy and robustness. Second,
an outdoor-capable 3DGS reconstruction system that utilizes
geometrically consistent LiDAR point clouds from the LVIO
frontend, overcoming the scalability limitations of neural implicit
representations in large-scale environments. Our system achieves
real-time performance at 25 Hz while maintaining high-fidelity
mapping. This work bridges the gap between robust odometry
and photorealistic reconstruction for outdoor SLAM, enabling
reliable operation in complex, outdoors environments.

I. INTRODUCTION
In recent years, Simultaneous Localization and Mapping
(SLAM) has made significant strides, enabling real-time self-
localization and map construction through sensor data fusion.
Commonly used sensors include LiDAR, inertial measurement
units (IMUs), and cameras. While visual-inertial odometry
(VIO) systems such as VINS-Mono [1] and ORB-SLAM3 [2]
have achieved remarkable progress, their performance remains
challenged in real-world outdoor scenarios, particularly un-
der rapid illumination changes, texture-deficient environments,
and motion blur—conditions frequently encountered in prac-
tical applications.
A novel mapping approach known as 3D Gaussian Splatting
(3DGS) has recently emerged in computer vision [3], inno-
vatively combining the advantages of differentiable rendering
pipelines with point-based rendering techniques. This method

enabling explicit scene representation and novel view synthe-

SLAM systems such as Photo-slam [4] and Gaussian-slam [5]
have shown promising results, their performance is still limited
in large-scale, open outdoor environments.

II. RELATED WORKS
A. LiDAR-Visual-Inertial Odometry Systems
LiDAR-Visual-Inertial multi-modal fusion SLAM signifi-
cantly enhances system robustness by leveraging complemen-
tary heterogeneous sensors. These systems capitalize on the
unique strengths of each sensor modality: LiDAR provides
precise range measurements, cameras offer rich visual infor-
mation, and IMUs deliver high-frequency motion data. Early
works such as LIO-SAM [6] introduced a sliding window opti-
mization framework that tightly couples LiDAR and IMU data,
achieving high-precision odometry through tight LiDAR-IMU
integration. However, this approach lacked visual semantic
awareness. LVI-SAM [7] pioneered a tightly-coupled LiDAR-
visual-inertial framework, implementing deep sensor fusion
through cascaded factor graph optimization, demonstrating
strong robustness in degraded environments.
R3LIVE [8] is a pioneering system that achieves tight
coupling of LiDAR, visual, and inertial data, enabling real-
time 3D mapping and state estimation with high accuracy and
robustness in dynamic environments. Point-LIO [9] introduces
a novel point-based LiDAR-inertial odometry framework that
efficiently handles large-scale environments by leveraging
point feature extraction and optimization, significantly reduc-
ing computational overhead while maintaining high accuracy.
Fast-LIO [10] focuses on real-time performance by adopting
a sparse feature-based approach and optimizing computational
pipelines, making it suitable for high-speed applications and
resource-constrained platforms. Fast-LiVO [11] extends this
approach by incorporating visual data into the LiDAR-inertial
framework, further enhancing robustness and accuracy in
scenarios with limited LiDAR features. Fast-LiVO2 [12] builds
upon its predecessor by introducing advanced feature matching
and outlier rejection techniques, improving performance in
dynamic and texture-poor environments.
These systems, through continuous optimization of sensor
fusion strategies and technical details, have advanced the
application capabilities of multi-modal SLAM in complex
environments.
B. 3D Gaussian Splatting in SLAM
In recent years, 3D Gaussian Splatting (3DGS) has emerged
as a groundbreaking approach in the field of Simultaneous
Localization and Mapping (SLAM), offering a novel and
efficient means of scene representation. By leveraging Gaus-
sian distributions to model 3D points, this technique adeptly
merges the computational efficiency of point-based methods
with the detailed reconstruction capabilities of voxel-based
approaches, thereby providing a versatile solution for various
SLAM applications.
The evolution of 3DGS-SLAM systems has been marked by
significant strides aimed at enhancing real-time performance,
boosting scene representation accuracy, and ensuring robust
operation across dynamic and intricate environments. NICE-
SLAM [13], as a pioneering system in this domain, has
set a new benchmark by introducing a sophisticated neural
implicit scene representation. This innovation enables the
high-fidelity reconstruction of dynamic scenes through the
seamless integration of Gaussian splatting with neural radiance
fields (NeRF), thus paving the way for more realistic and
detailed scene modeling. Building on this foundation, GS-
SLAM [14] has made notable advancements by optimizing
the Gaussian splatting process specifically for real-time perfor-
mance. Through the incorporation of efficient feature match-
ing algorithms and robust outlier rejection mechanisms, GS-
SLAM achieves remarkable accuracy in state estimation, even
in highly cluttered and challenging environments, thereby ex-
panding the applicability of 3DGS-SLAM systems. SparseGS
[15] addresses the critical challenge of computational com-
plexity by adopting a sparse Gaussian splatting approach. This
strategic optimization not only reduces resource requirements
but also maintains a high standard of reconstruction quality,
making it an ideal solution for deployment on resource-
constrained platforms without compromising on the richness
of scene details. NeuralGS [16] further elevates the capabilities
of 3DGS-SLAM by integrating deep learning techniques into
the Gaussian splatting framework. By leveraging the power
of pre-trained neural networks for feature extraction and
scene understanding, NeuralGS significantly enhances system
robustness, particularly in scenarios characterized by texture-
poor surfaces and low-light conditions, thus broadening the
operational scope of 3DGS-SLAM systems. In a parallel
development, SplaTAM [17] has introduced an innovative
keyframe-based Gaussian distribution management strategy.
This approach effectively tackles the issue of memory ex-
plosion that often arises in large-scale scene reconstruction,
thereby ensuring sustainable and efficient performance as the
scale of the environment increases.
Despite these commendable advancements, the field of
3DGS-SLAM continues to face challenges, particularly in
achieving consistent performance across diverse environments
and handling large-scale scenes with significant dynamic ac-
tivity. Ongoing research is actively exploring novel scene rep-
resentation strategies, more efficient optimization algorithms,
and advanced feature extraction methods to overcome these
hurdles and further propel the state of the art in 3DGS-SLAM
systems toward new heights of efficiency and accuracy.

B. Vision Pipeline
1) Data Preprocessing: Existing LIO systems include a
point-wise framework that fuses LiDAR points at their ac-
tual sampling times without accumulating them into frames.
Removing point accumulation eliminates intra-frame motion
distortion and allows high-frequency odometry outputs and
map updates close to the point sampling rate, further enabling
the system to track extremely fast motions.
In real-world scenarios, the CPU cannot handle updating
individual or sparse points. Since our LiDAR outputs at 10Hz
and the camera operates at approximately 15Hz, we split
the received LiDAR point cloud scans into more segments,
ensuring the segmentation times align precisely with the
camera’s sampling intervals. This allows the visual module
to utilize more accurate LIO-optimized poses instead of the
original VIO-based pose estimation.

IV. EXPERIMENTS
A. Real World Experiments
Our hardware system was implemented on YiFei H700
UAV, comprising a Raspberry Pi 4B as the central controller,
with sensors including a Livox Mid-40 LiDAR, HBVCAM-
U2205GS V11 camera, a microelectronic mechanical system
(MEMS) grade IMU (CMP10A), and a GNSS receiver (ublox
ZED-F9P 10HZ) are installed together with a small GNSS
antenna (BT-560). The sensor integration system and sensor-
equipped UAV are illustrated in Fig.2. We collected multiple
datasets within the campus environment and conducted com-
prehensive evaluations on these data.

In this section, we evaluate reconstruction quality by com-
paring 3DGS-rendered images with ground-truth imagery us-

method for image creation has better localization performance

ground-truth images. Experimental results demonstrate that
our framework achieves high-fidelity rendering and accurate
geometric reconstruction.
